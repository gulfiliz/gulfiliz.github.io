The Philosopher's Nephew — On Nepotism, Merit, and the Betrayal of Ideas

Created on January 21, 2026

2026   ·   fragmenta   ,   skepsis   ,   metaxy   ,   philosophy   ,   EN

In 347 BCE, Plato died.

He was eighty years old. He had founded the Academy — the first institution of higher learning in the Western world — and run it for forty years.

He left it to his nephew.

Speusippus.

Not to Aristotle — who had studied at the Academy for twenty years, who was by universal agreement the most brilliant mind in Athens, who would go on to write the Organon, the Physics, the Metaphysics, the Nicomachean Ethics, the Poetics, the Politics — who would, in short, produce more original philosophy than any human being before or since.

Not to Aristotle.

To the nephew.

Speusippus was Plato’s sister’s son. He was a competent philosopher. He wrote works that are now almost entirely lost — and their loss, frankly, has not been felt the way the loss of Aristotle’s early dialogues has been felt.

He led the Academy for eight years. It declined under his leadership.

Aristotle left Athens. He traveled, did biological research, tutored the young Alexander of Macedon, and eventually returned to Athens to found his own school: the Lykeion.

There, walking in the covered walkways — peripatos, hence the Peripatetics — he produced the body of work that shaped Western thought for two thousand years.

Plato’s nepotism gave us the Lyceum.

This is the most expensive act of favoritism in the history of ideas.

I. What Nepotism Is

Nepotism — from the Italian nipote, nephew — entered English in the 17th century to describe the practice of popes appointing their nephews to positions of power.

The etymology is precise: nipote derives from Latin nepos, nephew — the same root as nephew in English.

The popes were systematic about it. Several Renaissance popes appointed multiple nephews as cardinals — creating dynasties of ecclesiastical power based on blood rather than virtue.

The practice was so common that it was considered a fact of life rather than a scandal.

But the word captures something older and more universal:

The preference for the familiar over the excellent. The replacement of merit by connection. The substitution of “who you are” for “what you can do.”

Plato did not call it nepotism. He did not need to. It was simply what you did.

II. Plato’s Contradiction

Here is the irony that history has not been kind enough to highlight:

Plato wrote, at length, about the philosopher-king — the ruler who governs by knowledge, not by birth or wealth or connection.

He wrote about the meritocratic selection of guardians in the Republic — the careful testing of candidates, the long education, the rigorous evaluation.

He wrote about the Form of the Good — the highest object of knowledge — accessible only to those who had done the work, followed the argument, earned the understanding.

He built an institution dedicated to this ideal.

And then he gave the institution to his nephew.

The gap between the philosophy and the practice is one of the most instructive moments in the history of ideas.

Not because Plato was a hypocrite — though he was, in this instance — but because it shows how deeply the instinct toward nepotism runs.

Even the philosopher who articulated the most rigorous theory of merit in the ancient world could not, when the moment came, override the pull of blood.

III. Aristotle’s Response

Aristotle did not write about this directly.

He was too careful, too formal, too committed to the appearance of impartiality in his philosophical writing.

But his response was unmistakable:

He left.

He did not protest. He did not argue. He did not try to reform the Academy from within.

He built something new.

The Lyceum was, in many ways, a deliberate contrast to the Academy.

Where the Academy focused on mathematics and Platonic forms — Aristotle focused on observation, classification, the study of living things.

Where the Academy was elite and restricted — Aristotle lectured publicly, in the morning to advanced students, in the evening to general audiences.

Where the Academy became increasingly mystical under Speusippus — Aristotle became increasingly empirical.

The Lyceum had a library — the first research library in history. It had a collection of biological specimens. It had students doing fieldwork — collecting data on constitutions of city-states, dissecting animals, observing tidal patterns.

Aristotle created the model of the research university two thousand years before the research university existed.

And he did it because he was excluded from the institution he should have led.

IV. The Taxonomy of Nepotism

Plato’s case is ancient and obvious. The modern versions are more subtle.

Academic nepotism:

The advisor who steers the best postdoc positions to their own students — regardless of quality.

The hiring committee that advances the candidate who went to the right school, who knows the right people, who shares the committee’s research agenda — rather than the candidate who would most advance the field.

The journal editor who fast-tracks papers from colleagues and collaborators, who sends difficult papers to hostile reviewers and friendly papers to sympathetic ones.

The grant committee that funds established names — safe, credentialed, well-connected — rather than risky, unconventional, potentially revolutionary work.

None of these is called nepotism. They are called “quality assessment,” “cultural fit,” “network effects,” “track record evaluation.”

The outcome is the same: merit is subordinated to connection.

Institutional nepotism:

The department that has not hired outside its own PhD graduates in thirty years.

The research group that has become an intellectual dynasty — where the founder’s ideas are reproduced but never challenged, where the students who stay are the ones who agree, where the students who leave are the ones who think differently.

The conference circuit where the same names appear on the same panels year after year — not because no one else has anything to say but because the network is closed.

Intellectual nepotism:

Perhaps the most insidious form.

The preference for ideas from familiar sources. The tendency to cite the same authorities. The dismissal of work from outside the network as “not rigorous,” “not relevant,” “not serious” — when what is really meant is “not from us.”

The peer review system — intended to be meritocratic — is undermined by the fact that the community of peers is itself structured by network effects.

We review each other. We hire each other. We cite each other. We fund each other.

The system is not corrupt in the simple sense — no money changes hands, no laws are broken. It is corrupt in the structural sense: the outputs reflect the network as much as they reflect the merit.

V. Why It Happens — The Psychology

Nepotism is not primarily a moral failure. It is a cognitive one.

Familiarity bias: We trust what we know. We are more comfortable with the familiar. The candidate who went to our institution, who knows our colleagues, who uses our vocabulary — feels more legible, more trustworthy, more likely to succeed.

This feeling is often wrong. But it is reliable.

Risk aversion: Choosing the unknown is risky. If the unconventional hire fails, you are blamed. If the conventional hire fails, you shrug — you made the reasonable choice.

Nepotism is risk management. It distributes blame safely.

Confirmation bias: We evaluate candidates through the lens of what we already believe success looks like. The brilliant outsider — who approaches the problem differently, who has read different books, who comes from a different tradition — looks wrong.

Not because they are wrong. Because they don’t fit the template.

Reciprocity: Academic networks run on exchange. I support your student, you support mine. I review your paper favorably, you review mine favorably. I invite you to my conference, you invite me to yours.

This is not corruption. It is social fabric.

But it has the same effect as corruption: resources flow along network lines rather than merit lines.

VI. The Cost

What does nepotism cost?

The obvious cost: the brilliant outsider who does not get the position, the funding, the platform.

The Aristotle who must leave and build something new — if they are lucky enough, talented enough, resilient enough to do so.

Many are not.

The cost that is harder to measure: what is not done.

The research that is not funded because it challenges established paradigms.

The ideas that are not developed because the people who had them were excluded from the institutions where development happens.

The revolutions that do not occur — or occur decades late — because the revolutionary could not get a position.

We cannot measure this cost. We do not know what we have lost.

We only know, in retrospect, when someone escaped the system — when an Einstein worked in a patent office, when an Aristotle founded his own school — and produced work that the establishment missed.

We do not know how many could not escape. How many gave up. How many adjusted their thinking to fit what was fundable, publishable, acceptable — and lost whatever was original in them.

This is the true cost of nepotism: not the individual injustice, but the aggregate loss — the intellectual universe that did not happen.

VII. The Meritocracy Illusion

Here I need to complicate the argument.

Pure meritocracy does not exist. It has never existed. It cannot exist — for reasons that are structural, not merely practical.

Merit is not objective.

What counts as merit depends on what you are measuring, who is doing the measuring, and what framework they use.

In a field dominated by one paradigm, “merit” means “excellence within that paradigm.” Work that challenges the paradigm does not look meritorious — it looks confused, underdeveloped, irrelevant.

Kuhn’s Structure of Scientific Revolutions describes this: normal science evaluates by the standards of the paradigm. Revolutionary science is, by definition, incommensurable with those standards.

The revolutionary looks like a bad scientist to those who measure by the existing paradigm.

Social capital is part of merit.

A researcher who communicates well, who presents compellingly, who networks effectively — is, in practical terms, more productive than an equally brilliant researcher who cannot communicate.

Is this merit or connection? Both. The line is blurry.

Early advantage compounds.

The student who goes to the best undergraduate institution gets access to the best graduate programs. The graduate student who gets the best advisor gets access to the best positions. The early career researcher who gets the first grant gets the data for the next grant.

Inequality of opportunity becomes, over time, the appearance of inequality of merit.

I am not arguing against merit. I am arguing that “we hired on merit” is often self-deception — the comfortable belief that our preferences are objective evaluations, that our networks are just the natural result of quality seeking quality.

They are not. They are networks. Networks have structure. Structure produces path-dependence. Path-dependence produces oligarchy.

VIII. What Can Be Done

I want to be honest here. I do not have clean solutions.

But I have observations.

Blind review works.

When orchestras began conducting blind auditions — candidates behind a screen — the proportion of women hired increased dramatically.

The reviewers were not sexist in their intentions. They were biased in their evaluations. Removing the identity removed the bias.

Blind peer review. Blind grant review. Blind hiring, where feasible.

These are imperfect — in small fields, people recognize work by style — but they are better than the alternative.

Diversity of network is a quality signal, not a compromise.

A department that has hired only from three institutions for thirty years has a problem — not a diversity problem in the political sense, but an intellectual problem.

Intellectual monocultures are fragile. They produce sophisticated elaboration of a small set of ideas.

The most productive intellectual environments — Bell Labs, the Cavendish Laboratory, the Vienna Circle, the Manhattan Project — were characterized by radical intellectual diversity: people from different traditions, different countries, different disciplines, arguing fiercely with each other.

Diversity is not charity. It is quality insurance.

Transparency about process.

The most powerful antidote to nepotism is accountability — the requirement that decisions be explained, that criteria be stated, that evaluations be documented.

Nepotism thrives in opacity. “We just felt they were the right fit” — this is the language of networks doing what networks do, disguised as judgment.

Force the judgment into language. Language has to be defensible. Defensibility constrains bias.

Cultivate epistemic humility.

The deepest cause of nepotism is the conviction that we know what excellence looks like — that our template for quality is the template for quality.

This conviction is almost always wrong.

The history of science and philosophy is the history of excellence looking nothing like what was expected.

Darwin was a failed medical student. Einstein failed to get an academic position. Ramanujan had no formal training. Barbara McClintock was ignored for thirty years.

Excellence is not well-behaved. It does not arrive credentialed. It does not respect your network.

The institutions that found it were the ones willing to be surprised — willing to look at something unfamiliar and ask: is this good? Rather than: is this like us?

IX. Back to Plato

I want to return to Plato — because his case is not simply a failure. It is a paradox.

Plato was, in many respects, one of the most radical meritocrats in history.

His vision of the ideal state — the philosopher-king selected by merit, the guardians chosen through rigorous testing, the gold and silver and bronze souls sorted by their nature — is one of the most thoroughgoing anti-aristocratic visions in ancient thought.

He explicitly rejected hereditary rule. He explicitly rejected wealth as a qualification. He explicitly proposed that women could be guardians and philosophers.

And then he gave the Academy to his nephew.

What do we do with this?

Not: Plato was a hypocrite, therefore ignore him.

But: Plato was human — which means his ideals and his instincts were in tension, and his instincts won at the crucial moment.

This is not Plato’s failure alone. It is the human condition.

We all believe in merit — in the abstract, in principle, when it is not our nephew, our student, our colleague, our tradition on the line.

When it is: the instinct toward the familiar, the trusted, the connected is very strong.

Stronger, often, than principle.

The question is not: are you capable of nepotism?

You are. We all are.

The question is: have you built systems that constrain your instincts when they conflict with your values?

Plato had not.

We should.

X. The Aristotle Lesson

Aristotle’s response to being passed over is the most important part of this story.

He did not become bitter — or if he did, he did not stay bitter.

He did not compromise his intellectual standards to make himself acceptable to Speusippus.

He did not spend his energy trying to correct the injustice — trying to get what he deserved.

He built.

He took what he knew, what he had learned in twenty years at the Academy, and he built something new — something that reflected his own vision rather than Plato’s.

The Lyceum was not a better Academy. It was a different thing entirely.

And it produced work that the Academy, under any leadership, could not have produced — because Aristotle’s empirical, systematic, encyclopedic approach to knowledge was fundamentally different from Plato’s mathematical, formal, idealist approach.

The nepotism that excluded Aristotle gave us both the Academy and the Lyceum — both the Platonic and the Aristotelian traditions — both the mathematical and the empirical approaches to knowledge.

History is strange.

The injustice produced richness.

I am not recommending injustice.

I am noting that the person who responds to exclusion by building — rather than by seeking revenge, or adjusting to fit, or abandoning the work —

is the person who matters.

Aristotle mattered.

Speusippus is a footnote.

Coda

Every institution has its Speusippus.

The nephew who got the position. The connected candidate who got the grant. The well-networked mediocrity who got the platform.

Every field has its Aristotle — or had one, once.

The brilliant outsider who was passed over. Who left, or was forced out, or simply was never let in.

Some of them built their own Lyceum. Many did not.

We do not know what we lost when they did not.

We know what we gained when they did.

The lesson is simple. The practice is hard.

Judge ideas, not networks. Judge work, not credentials. Judge merit, not familiarity.

And when you cannot — when the instinct toward the familiar wins — at least be honest about what you are doing.

Don’t call it quality assessment. Call it what it is.

Plato didn’t call it anything. He just did it.

Two and a half thousand years later, we still remember.

Not him for doing it — but Aristotle, for what he built despite it.
