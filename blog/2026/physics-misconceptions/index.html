Physics Lied to You — Common Misconceptions That Even Smart People Believe

Created on April 11, 2026

2026   ·   research-log

Physics education has a dirty secret.

The way we teach physics — the metaphors we use, the simplifications we make, the intuitions we build in the first years — are often not just incomplete.

They are wrong.

Not wrong in the “technically this is a simplification” sense. Wrong in the “this will actively mislead you for the rest of your intellectual life” sense.

I am a physicist. I love physics. And I want to tell you about the things that physics education gets wrong — because correcting them is one of the most intellectually satisfying experiences available.

Prepare to unlearn.

I. Gravity Does Not Pull

Let’s start with the biggest one.

You learned: gravity is a force that pulls objects together. The Earth pulls you down. The Sun pulls the Earth. Massive objects attract each other.

This is Newton’s description of gravity. It is extraordinarily useful. It put men on the moon. It predicts the motion of planets with stunning accuracy.

It is also, fundamentally, wrong.

Einstein’s General Relativity — published in 1915 — replaced the concept of gravitational force with something far stranger and far more beautiful:

Gravity is the curvature of spacetime.

Massive objects do not pull on each other. They curve the fabric of spacetime around them, and other objects follow the straightest possible path through that curved spacetime.

When you fall off a building — you are not being pulled toward the Earth. You are following a geodesic — the straightest possible line through curved spacetime.

The floor is what is doing something unusual — it is accelerating upward, pushing you off your natural free-fall path.

This is not a metaphor. In General Relativity, the person in free fall is in an inertial frame — experiencing no force. The person standing on the ground is in a non-inertial frame — experiencing a force.

The floor pushes you up. Gravity does not pull you down.

Why does this matter?

Because it completely changes the conceptual structure. In Newtonian gravity, force acts at a distance — mysteriously, instantaneously, across empty space. In GR, there is no action at a distance. There is no force. There is geometry.

Spacetime curves. Things move along curves.

That’s it.

II. Space Is Not Empty

“The vacuum of space” — empty, cold, silent.

This is one of the most persistent misconceptions in popular science.

Space is not empty.

Even perfect vacuum — a region with no particles whatsoever — is filled with quantum fields.

Quantum field theory — the most precisely tested theory in the history of physics — describes reality not as particles moving through space but as excitations of fields that permeate all of spacetime.

The electromagnetic field. The electron field. The quark fields. The Higgs field. These fields are everywhere — including in vacuum.

And quantum fields in vacuum are not zero. They fluctuate.

Vacuum fluctuations — the spontaneous creation and annihilation of virtual particle pairs — are real, measurable phenomena.

The Casimir effect demonstrates this directly: two uncharged metal plates placed close together in vacuum experience an attractive force — because the vacuum fluctuations between the plates are restricted to certain wavelengths, while the fluctuations outside are unrestricted, creating a pressure differential.

This effect has been measured. It matches theoretical predictions. Empty space is pushing the plates together.

More startling:

The vacuum has energy — zero-point energy. And this vacuum energy appears to be what is driving the accelerating expansion of the universe — what cosmologists call dark energy.

The “emptiest” thing we know is churning with quantum activity.

Space is not empty. Space is a seething, fluctuating quantum foam that happens to have no particles in it.

III. Electrons Do Not Orbit the Nucleus

The image is iconic: a nucleus in the center, electrons orbiting around it like tiny planets. It is in every chemistry textbook. It is on the logos of nuclear agencies worldwide.

It is wrong.

Electrons do not orbit the nucleus.

In quantum mechanics, electrons do not have definite positions and trajectories. They are described by wave functions — probability distributions that tell you the likelihood of finding the electron in any given location if you measure it.

The electron is not going around the nucleus. The electron is in a quantum state — an orbital (note: not orbit, orbital) — that describes a probability distribution of where it might be found.

Some orbitals are spherically symmetric — the electron is equally likely to be found at any angle from the nucleus at a given distance.

Some orbitals are shaped like dumbbells. Some like four-leaf clovers. Some like donuts with a sphere through the middle.

None of them involve the electron moving in a circular path around the nucleus.

Why is the Bohr model — the planetary model — taught at all?

Because it gets the energy levels of hydrogen right. By accident. The quantization condition Bohr imposed — without theoretical justification — happened to give correct energy predictions for the simplest atom.

It fails for every other atom. And it gives a completely wrong picture of what an atom actually is.

The electron is not a tiny planet. It is a quantum field excitation in a superposition of positions described by a complex-valued wave function that collapses probabilistically upon measurement.

Much less paintable. Much more true.

IV. Lightning Absolutely Strikes Twice

“Lightning never strikes the same place twice.”

This is perhaps the most practically dangerous misconception in this list.

Lightning absolutely strikes the same place twice. Lightning preferentially strikes the same place twice.

Lightning is looking for the path of least resistance between the charged cloud and the ground. Tall, pointed, conductive objects — like trees, towers, skyscrapers — reduce the resistance for lightning discharge.

The Empire State Building is struck by lightning approximately 20–25 times per year.

The CN Tower in Toronto: approximately 75 times per year.

Lightning rods work precisely because lightning strikes the same place predictably — you give it a preferred path that directs the energy safely into the ground.

The saying presumably originated as encouragement — “don’t worry, you won’t get struck twice” — and somehow became treated as physics.

It is not physics. It is superstition with weather.

V. There Is No Sound in Space — But Not For the Reason You Think

“In space, no one can hear you scream.”

This is correct — but the standard explanation is incomplete.

The standard explanation: sound requires a medium — air, water, solid material — to propagate. Space is a vacuum. Therefore no sound.

This is true for the vast, sparse regions between stars.

But space is not uniformly empty.

The interstellar medium — the gas and dust between stars — is a medium. Very thin, but real. Sound can propagate through it.

In 2003, NASA astronomers detected pressure waves — sound waves — propagating through the hot gas surrounding the Perseus galaxy cluster.

The wavelength: approximately 30,000 light-years. The frequency: B-flat, 57 octaves below middle C — roughly \(10^{-15}\) Hz.

This sound wave has been propagating for approximately 2.5 billion years.

You cannot hear it — the frequency is far below the threshold of human hearing, and you would need to be floating in the thin hot plasma of a galaxy cluster.

But it exists.

Space is mostly silent. But “in space, no one can hear you scream” is a statement about the specific vacuum of interstellar space near Earth — not a universal law.

VI. We Do Not Have Five Senses

Aristotle identified five senses: sight, hearing, smell, taste, touch.

This is wrong.

Humans have many more than five senses.

Proprioception: the sense of your body’s position in space. Close your eyes. Touch your nose. How did you know where your hand was? Not sight. Proprioception — sensory receptors in your muscles, tendons, and joints that continuously report your body’s configuration.

Vestibular sense: the sense of balance and acceleration. Your inner ear contains the vestibular apparatus — three semicircular canals oriented in three planes — that detects rotational and linear acceleration.

Thermoception: the sense of temperature. Distinct from touch — you can feel heat without touching a warm object.

Nociception: the sense of pain. Distinct from touch — pain receptors respond to tissue damage, not contact.

Interoception: the sense of your internal bodily state — hunger, thirst, heart rate, the need to breathe. This is increasingly recognized as a distinct sensory modality, crucial to emotional processing.

Time perception: humans have a surprisingly accurate internal sense of duration, mediated by circadian rhythms and other neural mechanisms.

Some researchers count 21 or more distinct senses. The number depends on how you define “sense.”

Aristotle’s five is wrong. The exact number is genuinely contested. What is certain: we are far more richly sensory creatures than the ancient classification suggests.

VII. The Great Wall of China Is Not Visible From Space

This one is not physics — it’s geography. But it appears in so many science books that it deserves to be corrected.

The Great Wall of China is approximately 15–30 feet wide. From low Earth orbit — the International Space Station, approximately 400 km up — this is equivalent to trying to see a human hair from 3 kilometers away.

The wall is not visible from space with the naked eye.

This has been confirmed by Chinese astronaut Yang Liwei, who specifically looked for the wall during his 2003 mission and could not see it.

The wall is long — but length does not help visibility from orbit. Width determines visibility. Roads are also long and often wider — they are also not visible from space with the naked eye.

The misconception has been repeated so often that it appears in Chinese textbooks as a point of national pride. It is not true.

VIII. Mass and Weight Are Not the Same Thing

You have mass. You also have weight. These are different things.

Mass is a measure of the amount of matter in an object — more precisely, a measure of inertia (resistance to acceleration) and gravitational charge. Mass does not change with location. Your mass on Earth is the same as your mass on the Moon, the same as your mass in deep space.

Weight is a force — the gravitational force exerted on your mass by a nearby massive object.

\[W = mg\]

where \(g\) is the local gravitational acceleration.

On the Moon, \(g \approx 1.62 \text{ m/s}^2\) — about 1/6 of Earth’s. Your weight on the Moon is 1/6 of your weight on Earth. Your mass is unchanged.

In deep space, far from any massive object: your weight is approximately zero. Your mass is unchanged.

When astronauts on the ISS are “weightless” — they are not massless. They are in free fall — in continuous freefall around the Earth — and in a free-fall reference frame, the gravitational force is locally undetectable.

Their mass is entirely normal. Their weight — in the frame of the ISS — is zero.

This distinction is not pedantic. In general relativity, mass is a fundamental quantity. Weight is a frame-dependent quantity — it depends on your reference frame. Mass does not.

IX. Atoms Are Mostly Empty Space — But Not In The Way You Think

“Atoms are mostly empty space. If you scaled a hydrogen atom to the size of a football stadium, the nucleus would be a marble in the center.”

This is true and deeply misleading simultaneously.

The scaling is accurate. The nucleus is \(10^{-15}\) meters across. The atom is \(10^{-10}\) meters across. The ratio is \(10^5\) — one hundred thousand. An atom is mostly “empty” in the sense that the nucleus occupies a tiny fraction of the atom’s volume.

But “empty space” suggests that the rest of the atom is genuinely empty — vacuum.

It is not.

The electron is not a tiny particle orbiting at a distance from the nucleus. (We established this already — no orbits.)

The electron wave function fills the entire atom. The electron exists, probabilistically, throughout the volume of the atom.

When two atoms come close together — when you touch something — what prevents them from merging is not a physical collision between nuclei. It is the Pauli exclusion principle — the quantum mechanical rule that no two electrons can occupy the same quantum state.

The electrons of your hand and the electrons of the table cannot overlap states. This creates an effective repulsive force — the force you feel as “solidity.”

You are not touching the table. You are hovering, nanometers above it, repelled by quantum mechanics.

The table feels solid because of a quantum rule about the indistinguishability of electrons.

Physics is strange.

X. The Sun Is Not On Fire

The Sun is not burning.

Fire is a chemical reaction — rapid oxidation, the combination of a fuel with oxygen. The Sun contains almost no oxygen. There is nothing to combust.

The Sun is powered by nuclear fusion.

In the Sun’s core — under pressures of \(2.5 \times 10^{11}\) atmospheres and temperatures of \(1.5 \times 10^7\) Kelvin — hydrogen nuclei (protons) fuse together to form helium nuclei.

The mass of four protons is slightly greater than the mass of one helium nucleus. The missing mass — \(\Delta m\) — is converted to energy via \(E = mc^2\).

Every second, the Sun converts approximately 600 million tons of hydrogen to helium. In the process, approximately 4 million tons of mass are converted to energy.

\[E = (4 \times 10^9 \text{ kg}) \times (3 \times 10^8 \text{ m/s})^2 = 3.6 \times 10^{26} \text{ J}\]

Every second.

This has been happening for approximately 4.6 billion years. It will continue for approximately 5 billion more years.

Fire, by comparison, releases approximately \(10^7\) joules per kilogram of fuel. Fusion releases approximately \(6.3 \times 10^{14}\) joules per kilogram — sixty million times more energy per unit mass.

The Sun is not on fire. The Sun is a thermonuclear reactor, 4.6 billion years old, 150 million kilometers away, converting mass directly to energy and sending a tiny fraction of it to warm your face on a spring afternoon.

That is better than fire.

XI. Evolution Does Not Have A Goal

This is biology, not physics — but too important to omit.

“Humans evolved from monkeys.”

More precisely: humans and other great apes share a common ancestor. We did not evolve from chimpanzees — we share a common ancestor with chimpanzees, from which both human and chimpanzee lineages diverged.

But the deeper misconception is this: evolution is not directed toward humans. Evolution does not have a goal. Humans are not the pinnacle of evolution.

Natural selection is a blind, undirected process. Organisms that reproduce more successfully pass on more genes. There is no direction. No destination. No progress in any meaningful sense.

Bacteria are not primitive forms on the way to becoming humans. Bacteria are extraordinarily successful organisms — more numerous, more metabolically diverse, and better adapted to more environments than any vertebrate.

If evolution has a direction — which it does not — it is toward bacteria, not away from them.

The tree of life has no trunk pointing toward us. We are one branch among millions — no more or less evolved than any other.

XII. The Speed of Light Is Not The Universal Speed Limit In The Way You Think

“Nothing can travel faster than light.”

This is approximately true — but needs precision.

The precise statement: No object with mass can be accelerated to or beyond the speed of light in vacuum.

This is because the energy required to accelerate a massive object approaches infinity as its velocity approaches \(c\).

But this constraint applies to objects moving through spacetime. It does not apply to the expansion of spacetime itself.

The universe is expanding. Distant galaxies are receding from us. Galaxies sufficiently far away are receding faster than the speed of light — not because they are moving through space faster than light, but because the space between us and them is expanding faster than light can cross it.

This is permitted. The speed-of-light limit applies to motion through space — not to the expansion of space itself.

There is also quantum entanglement — which produces correlations between measurements on separated particles that appear instantaneous.

These correlations do not transmit information faster than light — a crucial distinction. You cannot use entanglement to send a message. The correlations are real but cannot be controlled in a way that allows faster-than-light communication.

And phase velocity — the velocity of the phase of a wave — can exceed the speed of light. This does not transmit energy or information. It is a feature of wave mathematics, not a violation of relativity.

The speed of light is a deep and real constraint. It is also more subtle than the textbook version.

Coda: Why This Matters

You might think: these are technical corrections. Who cares whether electrons orbit or occupy orbitals? Whether gravity pulls or curves spacetime?

Here is why it matters:

The misconceptions are not just wrong. They point to a deeper pattern of thinking — the assumption that the world at its most fundamental must resemble the world at human scales.

That atoms are like tiny solar systems. That gravity is like magnetism. That the vacuum is like a room with the furniture removed.

But the world at quantum scales does not resemble the world at human scales. The world at cosmological scales does not resemble the world at human scales.

Reality is strange all the way down — and all the way up — and only by releasing the assumption that familiar intuitions must apply everywhere can we begin to understand what physics is actually saying.

The misconceptions are comfortable. The truth is vertiginous.

And the vertigo — the dizziness of genuinely confronting how strange reality is — is one of the most valuable experiences that physics can give you.

Welcome to the vertigo.
