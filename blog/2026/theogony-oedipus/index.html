Hesiod, the Theogony, and Our Oedipal Wounds — On Myths That Know Us Better Than We Know Ourselves

Created on April 11, 2026

2026   ·   readings,   philosophy,   metaxy,   skepsis,   fragmenta,   EN

Before there was anything, there was Chaos.

Not disorder — the word means something more precise, something closer to the primordial gap, the yawning abyss, the opened mouth of nothingness.

Χάος — Kháos.

From this opening, everything comes.

Gaia — the deep-breasted earth.

Tartarus — the dark pit beneath the earth.

Eros — desire, the force that makes things reach toward each other.

Erebus — darkness. Night.

And from Night: Day.

From Darkness: Space.

This is how Hesiod begins. Not with a god who creates. With a gap that opens — and from the opening, generation.

The universe does not begin with intention. It begins with absence that cannot sustain itself, that generates in order to fill itself, that produces the world as a consequence of its own incompleteness.

This is, I want to argue, not only a cosmogony.

It is the deepest description we have of what it is to be human.

I. Hesiod and the Theogony

Hesiod was a Greek poet — contemporary with Homer, perhaps slightly later, perhaps slightly earlier.

He came from Ascra, in Boeotia — a small farming village, not the heroic world of the Homeric epics.

Where Homer wrote about warriors and kings — the world of kleos, glory, the great deed — Hesiod wrote about farmers, laborers, the grinding difficulty of ordinary life.

He wrote two major poems that survive:

Works and Days — a poem of practical instruction, addressed to his brother Perses, about farming, justice, and the rhythms of the year.

Theogony — the birth of the gods, the genealogy of the cosmos, the account of how everything came to be.

The Theogony is our source for the systematic Greek cosmogony — the account of origins that shaped Western thought from the pre-Socratics through Plato through Ovid through Milton through Freud.

It is approximately a thousand lines. It is the founding document of Western mythology.

II. The Violence at the Origin

What the Theogony tells us, relentlessly, is that creation requires violence.

Not violence as accident. Violence as structure.

Ouranos — Sky — mates with Gaia — Earth — but refuses to let his children be born. He pushes them back into their mother as they emerge.

Gaia, in agony, fashions a great sickle and asks her children to castrate their father.

Only Kronos agrees.

He castrates Ouranos. The severed genitals fall into the sea. From the foam — aphros — that gathers around them, Aphrodite is born.

From the blood that falls on the earth: the Giants, the Furies, the ash-tree Nymphs.

Creation from dismemberment. Beauty from castration. The goddess of love born from an act of violence.

This is not incidental. It is the Theogony’s central argument:

The world as it is required the destruction of what was.

Every generation of gods comes to power by overthrowing the previous generation.

Ouranos is castrated by Kronos. Kronos swallows his own children to prevent them from overthrowing him — because Ouranos and Gaia have prophesied that he will be overthrown by his son.

Zeus, hidden by his mother Rhea, forces Kronos to vomit up his siblings, defeats the Titans in the Titanomachy, and establishes the current order.

The current order — our world, the world we inhabit — is built on a foundation of successive violations.

Father castrated by son. Children swallowed by father. Father defeated by son.

This is not just myth. It is the structure of time itself — the new requiring the destruction of the old, the future feeding on the past, every generation haunted by what it had to do to become itself.

III. Kronos and the Devouring Father

Kronos swallows his children.

Hestia. Demeter. Hera. Hades. Poseidon. Each one, as born, taken from Rhea’s arms and swallowed.

Why?

Because the oracle said his son would overthrow him — as he overthrew his father.

He knows what he is. He knows what he did. He knows what will be done to him.

And so he swallows his children — not from malice, but from fear. From the terror of the inevitable.

This is the Theogony’s most psychologically precise moment.

The father who destroys his children is not a monster. He is a man who knows history — who knows that the structure of time requires his replacement — and who cannot bear it.

He swallows them to stop time. To prevent the future from happening. To remain the sovereign of the present by eliminating those who would make him the past.

It does not work.

It never works.

Zeus is hidden. Zeus grows. Zeus returns. The swallowed children are vomited up. Kronos is defeated.

The future cannot be swallowed. It can only be delayed — and the delay makes it more violent when it finally comes.

IV. Zeus and the New Order

Zeus wins.

He defeats the Titans. He defeats Typhon. He establishes the Olympian order — assigns domains to gods, marries Hera, produces an extraordinary number of children through an extraordinary number of liaisons.

Zeus is not a gentle god.

He rules through power — through the thunderbolt, through the threat of force, through the reminder that he has defeated everyone who challenged him.

But he also rules through intelligence — mêtis — a quality so important that he swallows the goddess Metis when she is pregnant with Athena, because the oracle says that Metis will bear a child greater than the father.

He cannot let that happen. So he swallows Metis — and Athena is born from his head, fully formed, in armor.

Wisdom born from the inside of the sovereign’s skull. Intelligence that belongs entirely to power — that cannot exist outside it, that is born through the sovereign’s self-cannibalism.

Notice the repetition:

Ouranos prevents birth. Kronos swallows children. Zeus swallows the mother of his most important child.

Each generation finds a new way to try to stop the next generation.

Each fails.

Because the structure of time cannot be stopped — only deformed.

V. The Oedipal Structure

Freud named his most famous complex after Oedipus.

This has been so thoroughly absorbed into culture that we have forgotten how strange it is — how much it both illuminates and distorts what Sophocles actually wrote.

Freud’s Oedipus complex: the child desires the parent of the opposite sex, fears castration (or its equivalent) from the same-sex parent, and eventually resolves this through identification with the same-sex parent and renunciation of the desire.

This is a story about desire and its renunciation — about the child’s entry into the social order through the abandonment of an impossible wish.

Sophocles’s Oedipus is something different.

Oedipus kills his father and sleeps with his mother — but he does not know it.

The horror of Oedipus Rex is not desire. It is knowledge.

Oedipus is the man who will not stop asking.

Warned by Tiresias to stop the investigation — told, effectively, that the answer will destroy him — he continues.

“I will not listen. I must know the truth.”

He finds out. He knows. He puts out his own eyes.

Why does he blind himself?

Not as punishment for the incest — which was unknowing — but because the eyes that saw but could not see are no longer worthy of the light.

He was blind when he could see. Now he will be blind without pretense.

VI. Oedipus as Epistemological Hero

This is the reading that mythology gives us that psychology obscures:

Oedipus is not primarily about desire. He is about the will to know — and what knowing costs.

He is the detective — the first detective in Western literature — who will not stop investigating even when the investigation leads to himself.

He is the scientist who will not falsify data to protect a comfortable conclusion.

He is the philosopher who follows the argument wherever it leads — including to the place where it destroys the philosopher’s own foundations.

“I must know the truth” — this is not hubris in the conventional sense. It is the deepest form of intellectual courage: the willingness to be the object of your own investigation, the refusal to grant yourself exemption from the questions you ask of others.

Oedipus is destroyed by truth. He would not have had it otherwise.

There is something in this that resonates beyond Greek tragedy.

Every serious intellectual — every person who has followed an argument to its uncomfortable conclusion, who has found themselves implicated in what they set out to investigate, who has discovered that the questioner is part of the question — has been Oedipus.

The willingness to put out your own eyes rather than unsee what you have seen — this is not pathology. It is the highest form of intellectual honesty.

VII. The Hesiodic and the Oedipal — Together

What connects Hesiod’s cosmogony to Sophocles’s tragedy?

Both are stories about the violence of origins — about what must be destroyed for anything to come into being.

In Hesiod: the cosmos requires castration, swallowing, defeat. The world as it is is built on the ruins of what it destroyed to become itself.

In Sophocles: knowledge requires the destruction of the self that did not know. The Oedipus who knows at the end of the play is not the Oedipus who began it. He has been destroyed and remade — at the cost of his eyes, his throne, his family, his city.

Both stories say: you cannot become without destruction.

The cosmos cannot be born without the father being unmade.

The truth cannot be known without the knower being transformed by what they learn.

This is the deepest mythological wisdom:

Origin is violence. Knowledge is violence. Becoming is violence — not in the sense of cruelty, but in the sense of irreversibility.

What has been destroyed to make you cannot be restored.

The eyes that saw Ouranos castrated — the ears that heard the oracle — cannot unremember.

The only question is: what do you do with the irreversibility?

VIII. The Mythological Answer

Greek mythology does not offer comfort.

It does not say: the violence was worth it. It does not say: everything happens for a reason. It does not say: the suffering will be redeemed.

It says: this is the structure of things.

Chaos opens. Generation begins. Each generation destroys and is destroyed. The cosmos is built on castration. Knowledge is built on self-blinding. The order we inhabit is the product of violences we did not choose and cannot undo.

And we live here. In this order. With these origins. Carrying these wounds.

The Theogony does not ask us to overcome our origins. It asks us to know them.

Oedipus does not ask us to avoid the truth. It asks us to follow it — wherever it leads — including into darkness.

This is the mythological answer:

Not redemption. Recognition.

The courageous act is not to transcend the violence at the origin of things. It is to look at it clearly — with eyes that have earned their sight through the willingness to see — and say:

Yes. This is where I come from. This is what made me. I know it now. I will not look away.

IX. What We Carry

We are all Oedipal in the mythological sense — not in Freud’s narrower sense, but in the sense that Sophocles intended:

We are all the products of origins we did not choose and cannot fully know.

We are all carrying wounds inflicted before we could consent to them — family wounds, cultural wounds, historical wounds, the wounds of being born into a specific time and place and body.

And we are all, like Oedipus, confronted with the choice:

To stop asking. To accept the comfortable not-knowing. To live in the city of Thebes without looking too closely at how it was built.

Or to ask. To follow the question. To risk what Oedipus risked — the discovery that the investigator is implicated in what they investigate, that the wound and the healer are the same person, that knowing changes nothing about what happened and everything about who you are in relation to what happened.

Tiresias warned Oedipus. He did not listen.

I think Sophocles admired him for it.

I do too.

X. Hesiod’s Last Word

The Theogony ends not with resolution but with catalogue — the endless list of gods and their children, the proliferation of divine genealogy that is the cosmos’s way of sustaining itself through multiplication.

There is something honest about this ending.

The cosmos does not resolve. It generates. It continues. It produces more of itself — more gods, more heroes, more stories, more attempts to understand the original gap that opened before anything was.

Chaos — the first word. The gap that is not nothing but the condition of everything.

We come from this.

Not from order, not from intention, not from a god who shaped us with a clear purpose in mind —

but from an opening, a gap, an abyss that could not sustain its own emptiness and began, from that unbearable fullness of nothing, to generate.

We are the cosmos knowing itself.

Imperfectly. Painfully. With eyes that sometimes need to be put out before they can truly see.

But knowing.

That is what we are. That is what we do.

That is, perhaps, enough.
