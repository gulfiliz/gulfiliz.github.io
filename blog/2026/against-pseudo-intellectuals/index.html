A Manifesto Against Pseudo-Intellectualism — On the Most Dangerous People in the Room

Created on April 11, 2026

2026   ·   skepsis

My favorite word in English is “pseudo”. I seriously love it. You can put it in front of anything and it just completely messes up the meaning. Anyway…

I want to talk about the most dangerous people in contemporary intellectual life.

Not the ignorant. The ignorant are not dangerous — they know they don’t know, or they don’t know they don’t know, but in either case they are not actively doing damage to the infrastructure of knowledge.

Not the stupid. Stupidity is a cognitive limitation, not a moral failure. You cannot hold someone responsible for the architecture of their brain.

I want to talk about the pseudo-intellectual.

The person who has received education — sometimes excellent education — and emerged from it not with knowledge but with the performance of knowledge.

The person who has learned not how to think but how to sound like they think.

The person who deploys vocabulary as camouflage, citations as authority, complexity as a substitute for clarity, and the appearance of rigor as a replacement for actual rigor.

This person is dangerous in a way that ignorance never is — because they are convincing. Because they have credentials. Because they sit on committees and write reports and give talks and shape policy and teach students and review papers and make decisions that affect real people’s real lives —

while understanding, at a fundamental level, almost nothing about what they are doing.

I. The Taxonomy

Let me be precise about what I mean — because “pseudo-intellectual” is often used as a simple insult, and I want to use it as a diagnosis.

There are several distinct varieties.

The Credential Hoarder.

Collects degrees, affiliations, publications — not as byproducts of genuine inquiry but as the goal itself.

The CV is the intellectual life. The paper is the thought. The conference is the work.

They have learned to produce the outputs of intellectual labor — the paper, the grant application, the review, the talk — without the inputs.

Without genuine curiosity. Without the sustained encounter with difficulty. Without the willingness to be wrong.

The credential hoarder’s papers cite correctly. The methodology section is technically adequate. The conclusions are hedged appropriately.

And the work contributes nothing — because nothing was genuinely asked, genuinely investigated, genuinely risked.

The Jargon Welder.

Has learned that complexity signals intelligence.

Writes sentences like: “The heteronormative discursive formations that undergird the epistemological violence of neoliberal subjectification require a transgressive praxis of intersectional counter-hegemonic resistance.”

This sentence means something — or rather, it could mean something — in the right context, with the right interlocutor, with genuine analytical work beneath it.

Usually it means nothing.

It is a performance of having thought about a subject without the inconvenience of actually thinking about it.

The jargon welder uses complexity not to illuminate but to intimidate — to make the reader feel that if they do not understand, it is their failure, not the writer’s.

This is the inversion of intellectual virtue.

Good writing makes the difficult clear. Bad writing makes the simple obscure — and calls the obscurity depth.

The Reference Dropper.

Has read — or skimmed, or read about — the important texts.

Drops names with confidence: Foucault, Derrida, Lacan, Žižek, Agamben. Or: Kahneman, Taleb, Gladwell, Peterson. Or: Feynman, Hawking, Penrose, Tegmark.

The references are real. The understanding is not.

The reference dropper has learned that Discipline and Punish is about power, that The Black Swan is about tail risk, that A Brief History of Time is about cosmology.

They have not read these books carefully. They have not tested their understanding by trying to explain the arguments to someone who has also read them carefully.

They have not asked: does this argument actually work? What are its assumptions? Its limits? Where does it fail?

They have acquired the brand without the product.

The Confident Generalist.

Has opinions on everything. Strong opinions. Stated confidently.

Speaks with authority on quantum mechanics, political economy, evolutionary psychology, the history of the Ottoman Empire, the neuroscience of addiction, the future of artificial intelligence.

Has thought seriously about none of them.

The confident generalist has learned that the appearance of confidence is socially rewarded — that hesitation reads as weakness, that “I don’t know” reads as ignorance, that the person who speaks first and loudest is perceived as the most knowledgeable.

They are exploiting a genuine cognitive bias — the Dunning-Kruger effect — but from the inside, as a strategy.

They know just enough to be convincing. They do not know enough to know what they do not know.

The Ideological Ventriloquist.

Has replaced thought with tribe.

All questions have predetermined answers — determined by political affiliation, disciplinary loyalty, institutional interest, or personal identity.

They do not investigate. They confirm. They do not argue. They signal. They do not change their minds — because their minds were never genuinely engaged in the first place.

The ideological ventriloquist is particularly dangerous in academia and in policy — where they occupy positions designed for genuine inquiry and use them for tribal warfare.

II. Why They Are Dangerous

You might ask: isn’t this just vanity? Aren’t they harmless pretenders, whose bluster affects nothing important?

No.

They occupy space.

Academic positions, editorial boards, policy committees, expert panels — these are finite resources. A pseudo-intellectual in a position of intellectual authority displaces a genuine thinker.

The mediocre paper published is the good paper rejected. The confident fool on the committee is the careful thinker silenced.

They corrupt standards.

When pseudo-intellectual work is rewarded — published, cited, funded — it signals to everyone in the system what the system values.

It trains the next generation in the production of the performance of thought rather than thought itself.

It creates a race to the bottom of credentialed mediocrity.

They make consequential decisions.

The pseudo-intellectual is not contained in the seminar room.

They write the reports that shape policy. They review the drugs that reach patients. They evaluate the technologies that are deployed. They advise the governments that make decisions affecting millions of people.

Their confident wrongness — their inability to know what they do not know — is not an academic problem.

It is a public health problem. It is a political problem. It is a civilizational problem.

They undermine trust in expertise.

This is perhaps the most damaging effect.

When pseudo-intellectuals speak with authority — and are wrong — and are later revealed to be wrong — the damage does not fall only on them.

It falls on expertise itself.

The person who trusted the confident generalist and was burned by their confident wrongness does not conclude: “I should find better experts.”

They conclude: “Experts cannot be trusted.”

This is how pseudo-intellectualism feeds anti-intellectualism.

The fake expert and the expert-denier are not opposites. They are collaborators — each making the other’s position possible.

III. The Difference — What Genuine Thinking Looks Like

It is not enough to diagnose the disease. We need to describe the health.

What distinguishes genuine intellectual work from its simulation?

Genuine thinking is uncomfortable.

Real inquiry goes places you did not expect. It produces conclusions you did not want. It requires you to revise beliefs you held firmly.

If your thinking never makes you uncomfortable — if you always end up where you expected — you are not thinking. You are confirming.

The pseudo-intellectual’s thinking is always comfortable because it is not thinking. It is performance — and the performance is rehearsed.

Genuine thinking fails publicly.

Real intellectual work includes wrong hypotheses, dead ends, failed experiments, retracted papers, revised positions.

The willingness to be publicly wrong — to say I was wrong, here is why, here is what I now think — is one of the clearest markers of intellectual honesty.

The pseudo-intellectual is never wrong — or never admits it — because they were never genuinely committed to a position that could be falsified. Their positions are held for social reasons. Social reasons do not require falsification.

Genuine thinking has limits.

The genuine expert knows the edge of their expertise.

They know which questions they can answer, which they cannot, which they could answer with more work, and which are genuinely open.

They say I don’t know without shame.

“I don’t know” is the most important phrase in intellectual life.

It is also the phrase the pseudo-intellectual can never say — because not knowing is not part of the performance.

Genuine thinking is slow.

Real understanding takes time. Reading carefully. Thinking between readings. Returning to texts. Changing your mind. Sitting with difficulty.

The pseudo-intellectual is always fast — because they are not building understanding, they are assembling presentation.

Presentation can be assembled quickly. Understanding cannot be.

IV. The Institutional Enablers

The pseudo-intellectual does not appear in a vacuum. They are produced and sustained by institutions that have lost sight of what they are for.

The publish-or-perish system rewards quantity over quality, the production of papers over the production of ideas. It creates systematic pressure to produce the appearance of research rather than research itself.

The grant system rewards legibility over importance — the ability to describe your work in terms that a committee will understand and fund, rather than the work that most needs doing.

It rewards the already-established over the genuinely new. It rewards the safe over the risky. It rewards the pseudo-intellectual who knows how to write a grant application over the genuine thinker who has not learned that particular performance.

The conference system rewards presence over substance — the ability to network, to be seen, to deliver a polished twenty-minute talk over the ability to think deeply about a problem over years.

The peer review system has been corrupted in many fields by the same dynamics — reviewers who are not experts in the work they review, who evaluate papers by surface features rather than by genuine engagement with the argument.

These are systemic failures. They produce pseudo-intellectualism at scale.

Fixing them requires institutional courage — the willingness to reward genuine inquiry over productive output, honest failure over performed success, the long slow work of thinking over the rapid production of credentials.

V. The Self-Examination

Here is the hardest part.

Everything I have said about the pseudo-intellectual applies, at different times and in different ways, to every person who has been educated — including me. Including you.

We all perform. We all sometimes deploy vocabulary we have not earned. We all sometimes speak with confidence that exceeds our knowledge. We all sometimes avoid the discomfort of genuine intellectual engagement.

The difference between the pseudo-intellectual and the genuine thinker is not that the genuine thinker never performs.

It is that the genuine thinker knows when they are performing — and is troubled by it — and returns, as often as possible, to the harder work of actually thinking.

The Socratic legacy is precisely this: the recognition that the examined life requires constant examination — not of others, but of oneself.

“The unexamined life is not worth living.”

The unexamined intellectual performance is not worth giving.

Ask yourself:

Do I actually understand this, or do I understand how to talk about it?

Could I explain this argument to a genuinely intelligent person who has never encountered it — explain it so they understand it, not so they are impressed by my familiarity with it?

If someone pushed back on my position — if someone found a genuine flaw — would I be able to engage with the flaw, or would I retreat into jargon?

Have I changed my mind about something important in the last year? The last five years? If not — am I thinking, or confirming?

These questions are uncomfortable. They are supposed to be.

VI. What We Owe Each Other

There is a moral dimension here that I want to state directly.

Knowledge is a public good.

When you claim expertise you do not have — when you occupy an intellectual position under false pretenses — you are not just embarrassing yourself.

You are stealing from the public.

You are taking the social trust that is extended to experts — trust built up over generations by people who did the hard work, who were genuinely right about important things, who earned the authority of expertise —

and spending it on your performance.

Every confident wrong pronouncement by someone with impressive credentials depletes that trust.

We are already in a trust crisis. We are already in a moment where expertise is distrusted — often wrongly, but not always wrongly — by large portions of the population.

The pseudo-intellectual is one of the causes of this crisis.

Not the only cause. Not the primary cause. But a real cause.

You owe the truth to the people who trust you with their attention, their credulity, their decisions.

You owe them your genuine best thinking — not your polished performance of thinking.

If you don’t have genuine thinking to offer — if you have not done the work — then the most intellectually honest thing you can do is silence.

“Whereof one cannot speak, thereof one must be silent.”

Wittgenstein said this about metaphysics. It applies equally to expertise.

If you do not know — if you have not done the work — be silent.

Or say: I don’t know.

These are not failures. They are the beginning of honesty. And honesty is the beginning of thought.

VII. The Standard

I want to end with what I am for, not just what I am against.

I am for the person who reads a paper and asks: does this argument actually work?

I am for the person who encounters a new idea and sits with the discomfort of not yet understanding it — rather than immediately assimilating it into what they already know.

I am for the person who changes their mind when the evidence changes — not because changing your mind is fashionable, but because it is what thinking requires.

I am for the person who says I don’t know without shame — and means it as an invitation to find out, not as the end of the conversation.

I am for the person who is more interested in being right than in appearing right.

I am for the slow thinker — the person who takes years to write a book because the ideas required years to develop.

I am for the careful reader — the person who reads slowly, who marks up the margins, who returns to difficult passages, who argues with the author in their head.

I am for the honest failure — the person who publishes the result that contradicts their hypothesis, who reports the null finding, who says: we were wrong, here is what we learned.

I am for genuine intellectual humility — not the performed humility of the person who says of course I could be wrong and then proceeds as if they cannot be — but the lived humility of the person who has been wrong enough times to know it can always happen again.

This is not a low standard.

It is the only standard that deserves the name intellectual.

Coda

“The first step to knowledge is knowing what you do not know.”

This is attributed to various people. The attribution is uncertain. The content is Socratic.

The pseudo-intellectual knows everything. That is how you know they know nothing.

The genuine thinker knows what they don’t know — and spends their life, carefully, reducing the territory of ignorance.

Not eliminating it. Reducing it.

The territory of ignorance is infinite. The genuine thinker is at peace with this.

The pseudo-intellectual cannot afford to be.

This is a manifesto, not a verdict. Including against myself. On the days when I perform rather than think. Which is more days than I would like.
