The System That Rewards Visibility Over Knowledge

Created on February 27, 2026

2026   ·   skepsis   ,   filizinbetween

There is a system.

It is not a conspiracy. No one designed it with malicious intent. It emerged — the way most destructive things emerge — from the accumulation of individually rational decisions that are collectively catastrophic.

The system rewards visibility.

Not knowledge. Not depth. Not accuracy. Not the slow, careful, unglamorous work of actually understanding something.

Visibility.

How many people saw it. How many liked it. How many shared it. How many followed. How many subscribed.

The metric is reach. The reward is attention. The currency is recognition.

And the person who knows the most is systematically disadvantaged relative to the person who performs knowing most convincingly.

This is not a peripheral malfunction in an otherwise healthy system.

It is the central operating principle.

I. How the Inversion Happened

To understand the current situation, we need to understand what it replaced.

The pre-visibility system:

In the pre-internet intellectual world, knowledge was rewarded — imperfectly, with significant distortions, but substantially.

To become a recognized expert, you needed:

  • Years of training
  • Peer review
  • The slow accumulation of a reputation among people who knew the field
  • The ability to withstand expert criticism

This system had serious problems:

  • It was gatekept by existing elites
  • It excluded people without access to institutions
  • It was conservative — slow to accept genuinely new ideas
  • It was subject to nepotism and network effects (as we have discussed elsewhere)

But it had one important virtue: the people doing the evaluating were the people who knew the most.

Peer review, whatever its failures, involves experts evaluating expert claims. The currency of academic reputation — whatever its distortions — is roughly correlated with expertise.

The visibility system:

The internet — and specifically social media — changed the evaluators.

Now anyone can evaluate anything. Now the metric is not expert judgment but aggregate attention.

A paper read carefully by 50 experts and found to be important is less visible than a tweet read carelessly by 50,000 people and found to be exciting.

The tweet wins.

Not because the tweet is more correct. Because visibility is now the currency — and the tweet has more of it.

II. The Mechanics of Visibility

Why does the visibility system consistently reward certain things over others?

Simplicity over complexity:

Complex ideas require time to understand. Simple ideas are immediately accessible.

In a system that rewards reach — where the marginal reader has a short attention span and many competing demands — the simple idea always has an advantage.

The complex idea must be simplified to travel. Simplification loses information. The information that is lost is often the most important part.

The viral version of any complex idea is necessarily a distortion.

Certainty over uncertainty:

Uncertainty is uncomfortable. “We don’t know” is not a satisfying answer. “The situation is complicated” does not generate engagement.

The confident wrong answer travels further than the hesitant right one.

In expert discourse, the expression of uncertainty is a virtue — it is epistemic honesty, the acknowledgment of the limits of evidence.

In the visibility system, uncertainty reads as weakness. The person who says “I’m not sure” loses to the person who says “I’m absolutely certain” regardless of which one is actually right.

Story over argument:

Narratives are processed differently than arguments.

A story activates emotion, memory, identification. An argument requires effortful processing — attention, working memory, logical tracking.

In a system optimized for engagement, story wins every time.

The person who can make a vivid anecdote do the work of a careful argument will reach more people than the person who makes the careful argument.

Even if the argument is better. Even if the anecdote is misleading.

Outrage over nuance:

Outrage is the most effective engagement driver in algorithmic systems.

Platforms have discovered — empirically, through optimization — that content producing anger and indignation generates more clicks, shares, and comments than content producing calm understanding.

The nuanced analysis that makes you think differently loses to the outrageous claim that confirms and amplifies what you already feel.

Every platform algorithm, optimized for engagement, is therefore optimized for outrage — not by design, but by consequence.

III. The Produced Experts

The visibility system produces a specific type: the produced expert.

The produced expert is not produced by knowledge. They are produced by visibility.

They appeared on the right podcast. They wrote the right viral thread. They have the right number of followers. They are invited to the right conferences because they have the right followers because they were invited to the right conferences.

Their credential is their platform. Their platform is their credential.

The circularity is the point.

The produced expert’s characteristics:

They are confident on a wide range of topics. The confidence is not proportional to knowledge — it is the performance that produced the visibility in the first place.

They are accessible. Complex ideas are simplified until they can be explained to anyone. What is lost in the simplification is not their concern.

They are consistent. They have a brand — a set of positions, a recognizable style, an identifiable perspective. The brand must be maintained. New evidence that complicates the brand is a threat, not an opportunity.

They are reactive. The visibility system rewards being first to respond to events — rapid takes, instant analysis, the hot interpretation of whatever just happened.

The person who waits to think carefully is punished by the person who speaks immediately.

The produced expert versus the genuine expert:

The genuine expert:

  • Knows a specific domain deeply
  • Knows the limits of that domain
  • Is uncertain about things outside it
  • Changes their mind when evidence warrants
  • Is uninteresting to the general public on most days

The produced expert:

  • Has opinions on everything
  • Projects confidence uniformly
  • Rarely acknowledges limits
  • Changes their position only when the audience does
  • Is consistently engaging, consistently accessible, consistently present

In the visibility system: the produced expert wins.

IV. The Academic Version

The visibility problem is not only in popular media. It has penetrated academia.

Citation metrics:

Academic reputation is increasingly measured by citation counts, h-index, impact factor.

These are visibility metrics — they measure how often work is seen and cited, not how important or correct it is.

Highly cited papers: — in “high-impact” journals — on fashionable topics — from prestigious institutions — by established names

Low-cited papers: — in specialist journals — on unfashionable topics — from less prestigious institutions — by unknown names

The correlation between citations and quality is positive but weak.

The correlation between citations and visibility is almost definitional.

The conference circuit:

Academic conferences select speakers partly by expertise and partly by the ability to generate interesting, accessible talks.

The person who gives a compelling 20-minute performance is invited back. The person who does the most important work but gives dense, difficult talks is not invited back.

Over time: the conference circuit selects for performance.

The most visible academics are those who have learned to perform — to give the stimulating talk, to generate the quotable phrase, to be the name that draws an audience.

Grant funding:

Grant proposals must be readable by committees that are not specialists in the proposed research.

The proposal must tell a story: here is the big question, here is why it matters, here is why we are uniquely positioned to answer it.

This is a visibility skill — the ability to make work seem important to non-experts.

The person who can tell the story well gets the grant. The person doing the most important work may not be able to tell the story.

Social media in academia:

Twitter, LinkedIn, Substack — academics are increasingly expected to have a public presence.

The academic who tweets effectively builds a following. The following generates invitations. The invitations generate opportunities. The opportunities generate publications. The publications generate citations.

The visibility loop is now fully embedded in the academic career structure.

V. What Is Lost

When visibility displaces knowledge, specific things are lost.

The slow work:

Some of the most important intellectual work is invisible for years.

Darwin spent 20 years developing the theory of evolution before publishing. He was waiting until he had enough evidence to defend the idea against objection.

The 20 years of preparation generated nothing visible. In a visibility-rewarded system, 20 years of invisible work is 20 years of career death.

The system cannot accommodate the person who needs decades to be right.

The honest negative result:

Scientific progress requires knowing what does not work. The null result — we tried this, it did not work — is essential information.

Null results are not published in high-impact journals. They do not generate citations. They do not generate visibility.

So they are not published.

This is the publication bias problem — systematic suppression of negative results because they are not visible enough to matter.

The consequence: replication failures, inflated effect sizes, entire research programs built on results that cannot be reproduced.

The careful qualifier:

Expertise requires qualification. “This is true in these conditions, with these limitations, contingent on these assumptions.”

The qualification is not weakness. It is accuracy.

In the visibility system, the qualification is weakness. It makes the claim less shareable. Less usable. Less interesting.

The careful expert is outcompeted by the reckless confident one — not because the reckless one is right but because they are more visible.

The genuinely novel:

Genuinely new ideas are initially incomprehensible to most people.

The novel idea requires new concepts — new vocabulary, new frameworks, a restructuring of existing categories.

This takes time to understand. It takes effort. It is not immediately accessible.

In the visibility system: what is not immediately accessible is not shared, is not amplified, is not rewarded.

The genuinely novel is selected against.

What is rewarded: the novel-seeming — the repackaging of existing ideas in new vocabulary, the old insight made to look new, the familiar made to feel profound.

VI. The Audience’s Complicity

The visibility system does not operate without an audience.

We — the consumers of intellectual content — are complicit.

We choose the stimulating over the accurate. We choose the confident over the uncertain. We choose the story over the argument. We choose the familiar-in-new-clothes over the genuinely new.

Not because we are stupid. Because we are human.

Our cognitive systems are not optimized for evaluating complex arguments under conditions of information abundance.

We are overwhelmed. We are time-poor. We are processing more information than any human being in history has processed.

Under these conditions, we use heuristics:

Is this person confident? → Probably knows what they’re talking about. Is this widely shared? → Probably true. Is this from a recognizable name? → Probably reliable. Does this confirm what I already think? → Probably correct.

All of these heuristics are wrong. Confidence correlates weakly with accuracy. Sharing correlates negatively with accuracy. Name recognition correlates with visibility, not knowledge. Confirmation bias is the enemy of truth.

But we use them anyway — because we have to. Because there is no time for anything else. Because the alternative is paralysis.

VII. The Political Consequences

The visibility system has political consequences that extend far beyond intellectual culture.

Policy is made by the visible:

Policy decisions are influenced by the most visible voices on a topic — not the most knowledgeable ones.

The scientist who has studied the topic for 20 years and communicates with careful qualification is less influential than the pundit who has read three articles on it and communicates with total confidence.

The politician listens to the pundit. The pundit has 2 million followers. The scientist has a university position and a research group of 12.

Public understanding is shaped by the visible:

What the public believes about climate, vaccines, economics, nutrition — is shaped primarily by visible communicators, not by domain experts.

The visible communicators have been selected for their ability to communicate, not for their accuracy.

The most effective communicators on contentious topics are often the most wrong — because willingness to simplify and distort is a competitive advantage in the visibility market.

The expert gap:

The visibility system produces a systematic mismatch between public understanding and expert consensus.

The public believes things that experts know to be false. The public disbelieves things that experts know to be true.

This is not primarily an education problem. It is a visibility problem.

The false belief is more visible than the accurate one. The visible wins.

VIII. The Exceptions — And What They Show

There are exceptions to the visibility system.

Some people who are genuinely expert are also genuinely visible.

Some platforms reward accuracy and depth. Some audiences seek out genuine expertise. Some corners of the internet maintain standards.

The exceptions matter — not because they refute the general pattern but because they show what is possible.

What the exceptions have in common:

They have found a niche where the audience is self-selected for caring about accuracy.

They have built trust over time — the trust that comes from being right when it was hard to be right, from admitting error, from being specific enough to be falsifiable.

They have accepted smaller reach in exchange for higher quality.

They have not tried to be maximally visible. They have tried to be useful to the people who actually care about what they are saying.

This is the only viable alternative within the current system: accept the reach penalty and serve the audience that can bear the truth.

Not an inspiring conclusion. But an honest one.

IX. What Can Be Done

I want to offer something practical — not naive optimism, but concrete possibility.

As a producer of knowledge:

Refuse the visibility trap when it requires distortion.

Do not simplify past the point where the simplification is still true.

Qualify when qualification is necessary — even when it costs reach.

Publish the negative result. Admit the error. Say “I don’t know.”

Accept that genuine expertise is rarely viral.

Build a reputation over time with the people who care about accuracy — not a following over weeks with people who care about excitement.

As a consumer of knowledge:

Distinguish between confidence and accuracy. The confident voice is not the reliable one.

Seek the qualifiers — the expert who says “it depends,” who says “the evidence is mixed,” who says “I might be wrong” — these are signs of accuracy, not weakness.

Follow the correctors — the people who say “I was wrong about this, and here is why” — these are the most trustworthy voices in any field.

Slow down. The article that requires effort to read is more likely to be accurate than the one that is immediately satisfying.

Be suspicious of your own engagement. If you find something immediately compelling, ask why. Is it compelling because it is accurate? Or because it is well-packaged?

As an institution:

Decouple reward from visibility.

Evaluate academic work by its quality, not its citation count.

Fund the slow work. Fund the null result. Fund the uncharismatic genius who cannot give a good talk but is producing the most important ideas in the field.

Create space for genuine expertise to develop without the constant demand to perform for an audience.

X. The Deeper Problem

Beneath all of this is a philosophical problem that no institutional fix will solve.

The problem of evaluating expertise from outside the expertise.

To know that someone is a genuine expert, you need some expertise yourself.

The lay person cannot evaluate the climate scientist’s claim on its technical merits. They can only evaluate the performance — the confidence, the communication skill, the social proof of followers and credentials.

This means: for the non-expert, performance and expertise are indistinguishable.

The produced expert has learned to perform expertise convincingly. The genuine expert may not perform well.

The non-expert, rationally, may prefer the produced expert — because the performance is the only signal available.

This is not stupidity. It is an epistemic constraint.

The visibility system exploits this constraint.

It produces people who are optimized for being indistinguishable from experts without being experts.

And in a world where most knowledge is necessarily second-hand — where we must trust others for most of what we know — this indistinguishability is catastrophic.

Coda

Knowing is not the same as being seen to know.

This sentence should be obvious. In the current system, it is radical.

The person who has spent ten years learning something difficult — who knows it with the precision that only years of work can produce — is systematically disadvantaged relative to the person who has spent ten years learning to appear to know things.

This inversion is not inevitable. It is not natural. It is the product of specific choices — platform design, incentive structures, metric selection — that could be made differently.

They have not been made differently.

The system continues.

And somewhere in it — doing the slow, invisible work, accepting the reach penalty, refusing to distort for the audience —

someone actually knows something.

They are not trending.

They are right.
