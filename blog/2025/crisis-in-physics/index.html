The Crisis in Fundamental Physics — An Honest Assessment

Created on July 13, 2025

2025   ·   research-logs,   physics,   EN,

Let me be precise about what I mean by “crisis.”

I do not mean that physics has failed. Physics has not failed.

Quantum electrodynamics predicts the anomalous magnetic moment of the electron to ten significant figures. General relativity predicts gravitational wave signals that LIGO measures to extraordinary precision. The Standard Model has not failed a single experimental test in fifty years.

Physics works.

What is in crisis is fundamental physics — the part of physics concerned with the deepest structure of reality: the unification of forces, the nature of spacetime at the Planck scale, the origin of the constants of nature, the interpretation of quantum mechanics.

In this domain — the domain where the questions are hardest and the stakes are highest — we have made almost no progress in forty years.

This is not pessimism. It is an accurate description of where we are.

I. The Standard Model — A Fifty-Year-Old Theory

The Standard Model was essentially complete by 1973.

Quantum chromodynamics: 1973. Electroweak unification: 1967-68 (Weinberg, Salam, Glashow). The full gauge group \(SU(3) \times SU(2) \times U(1)\): confirmed by 1973.

The Higgs mechanism: proposed 1964. The Higgs boson: discovered 2012.

Between 1973 and 2012: 39 years of experimental confirmations and zero theoretical surprises.

Every particle the Standard Model predicted was found — eventually.

The W and Z bosons: 1983. The top quark: 1995. The tau neutrino: 2000. The Higgs boson: 2012.

Each discovery confirmed the Standard Model. None extended it. None pointed beyond it.

The Standard Model is not a temporary scaffold to be replaced by something better. It is a mature, complete, verified theory.

And it is clearly incomplete.

It does not include gravity. It has 25 free parameters with no explanation. It does not explain dark matter or dark energy. It does not explain the matter-antimatter asymmetry. It does not explain the hierarchy problem. It does not explain neutrino masses properly.

We know it is incomplete. We do not know how to extend it.

II. The LHC — What We Expected and What We Found

The Large Hadron Collider began operations in 2008. It reached design energy (13 TeV) in 2015.

It cost approximately $10 billion. It involves 10,000 scientists from over 100 countries. It is the most expensive and complex scientific instrument ever built.

What we expected to find:

The theoretical physics community, in 2008, had strong expectations.

1. The Higgs boson.

The Standard Model required it. The electroweak precision data constrained its mass to \(< 200\) GeV. It was expected. It was found.

2. Supersymmetric particles.

The hierarchy problem required new physics at the TeV scale. SUSY was the dominant solution. Naturalness arguments placed SUSY particles at \(100 - 1000\) GeV — directly accessible to the LHC.

The most conservative prediction: the lightest stop quark at \(\sim 200-500\) GeV.

3. Extra dimensions.

If extra dimensions exist at the TeV scale (Arkani-Hamed, Dimopoulos, Dvali — ADD model), the LHC should produce Kaluza-Klein graviton resonances — peaks in the dilepton invariant mass spectrum.

4. Dark matter candidates.

WIMPs — Weakly Interacting Massive Particles — are the dominant dark matter candidates. If WIMPs have mass \(\sim 100\) GeV and weak-scale interactions, the LHC should produce them in pairs, visible as missing transverse energy.

5. Compositeness / new resonances.

Various models predicted new heavy particles — \(W'\), \(Z'\), leptoquarks, excited quarks — at TeV scales.

What we found:

The Higgs boson at 125.25 GeV. ✓

Everything else: nothing.

No SUSY particles. No extra dimensions. No new resonances. No WIMP dark matter. No leptoquarks. No excited quarks. No \(W'\) or \(Z'\) bosons.

The full Run 1 (2011-2012) and Run 2 (2015-2018) dataset, representing \(\sim 150\) fb\(^{-1}\) of proton-proton collisions at 13 TeV:

One discovery: the Higgs boson. Everything else: null results.

III. What the Null Results Mean

Let me be precise about what has been excluded.

Supersymmetry:

Current exclusion limits (approximate, model-dependent):

Particle Lower mass limit
Gluino \(\tilde{g}\) \(> 2.2\) TeV
Squark (light flavor) \(\tilde{q}\) \(> 1.8\) TeV
Stop \(\tilde{t}_1\) \(> 1.1\) TeV
Sbottom \(\tilde{b}_1\) \(> 1.3\) TeV
Chargino \(\tilde{\chi}^\pm_1\) \(> 700\) GeV
Neutralino \(\tilde{\chi}^0_1\) \(> 200\) GeV

These are not exclusions of SUSY. They are exclusions of SUSY at the TeV scale.

SUSY could still exist at higher masses. But higher-mass SUSY does not solve the hierarchy problem naturally.

Quantifying the fine-tuning:

The electroweak fine-tuning in SUSY:

\[\Delta_{EW} \equiv \max_i \left|\frac{\partial \ln m_Z^2}{\partial \ln p_i}\right|\]

where \(p_i\) are the SUSY parameters.

For a stop at \(m_{\tilde{t}} = 1\) TeV: \(\Delta_{EW} \sim 100\) (1% fine-tuning)

For \(m_{\tilde{t}} = 3\) TeV: \(\Delta_{EW} \sim 1000\) (0.1% fine-tuning)

SUSY was supposed to solve the hierarchy problem — to explain why the Higgs mass is small without fine-tuning.

With current bounds, SUSY requires fine-tuning at the percent level or worse.

The solution has become a smaller version of the problem.

Dark matter direct detection:

Independent of the LHC, dark matter direct detection experiments have excluded large regions of WIMP parameter space:

XENON1T, LUX, PandaX: For \(m_{WIMP} \sim 100\) GeV: \(\sigma_{SI} < 4 \times 10^{-47}\) cm\(^2\)

This is extraordinarily sensitive — equivalent to detecting an interaction weaker than one per century per kilogram of detector material.

No signal found.

The classic WIMP dark matter candidate — the lightest supersymmetric particle with weak-scale mass and interactions — is essentially excluded for most of parameter space.

IV. String Theory — The Promise and the Problem

String theory is the dominant framework for quantum gravity.

Its promise:

  • Finite quantum gravity
  • Unification of all forces
  • Explanation of the Standard Model from geometry
  • Derivation of the constants of nature

Its status in 2024:

The technical achievements are real:

The AdS/CFT correspondence (Maldacena, 1997) is one of the most powerful results in theoretical physics. It has applications in:

  • Strongly coupled gauge theories
  • Condensed matter physics
  • Quantum information
  • Black hole thermodynamics

The superstring theories (Type I, IIA, IIB, Heterotic) and M-theory are mathematically extraordinarily rich and consistent.

Finiteness of string amplitudes at loop level is established (at least to low orders).

The fundamental problems are also real:

1. The landscape:

String theory does not predict a unique vacuum. It predicts \(\sim 10^{500}\) metastable vacua — each with different effective physics, different constants, different gauge groups.

The landscape means string theory does not uniquely predict the Standard Model. It predicts a vast space of possible theories, of which the SM is one point.

This is either: — a profound feature (multiverse) — a failure of predictivity

2. No testable predictions:

String theory makes no predictions accessible to any foreseeable experiment.

The string scale: \(\sim m_P \sim 10^{19}\) GeV. The LHC: \(13 \times 10^3\) GeV.

15 orders of magnitude between experiment and theory.

The specific predictions of string theory — Kaluza-Klein modes, string resonances, extra dimensions at the string scale — require Planck-energy accelerators.

These will never be built.

3. The de Sitter problem:

The universe has positive cosmological constant — it is de Sitter space.

Constructing stable de Sitter vacua in string theory has proven extraordinarily difficult.

The KKLT construction (2003) proposed a mechanism for de Sitter vacua — but its validity is disputed.

The swampland program (Vafa et al., 2018) proposes that de Sitter vacua are not in the “landscape” (consistent string vacua) but in the “swampland” (inconsistent).

If the swampland conjectures are correct: string theory is incompatible with dark energy.

The universe has dark energy. If string theory cannot accommodate it — something is seriously wrong.

4. Background dependence:

String theory is defined perturbatively around a fixed background spacetime.

In GR, the spacetime itself is dynamic — there is no fixed background.

A complete theory of quantum gravity must be background-independent. String theory, as currently formulated, is not.

V. The Interpretation of Quantum Mechanics

We have discussed this elsewhere — but its status as an open problem deserves emphasis here.

Quantum mechanics is 100 years old.

The measurement problem — why measurements have definite outcomes — is unsolved.

The major interpretations: Copenhagen, Many-Worlds, Bohmian mechanics, GRW collapse models, QBism, Relational QM — make the same experimental predictions.

They cannot be distinguished by experiment.

This means: either the interpretational question is not empirical — it is metaphysics, not physics — or we have not yet found the experiment that distinguishes them.

Recent experimental developments:

Wigner’s Friend experiments (2019-present):

Implementations of extended Wigner’s Friend scenarios using photonic systems probe the consistency of quantum mechanics across multiple observers.

Results: consistent with quantum mechanics, but the specific implications depend on your interpretation.

Quantum Darwinism tests:

Zurek’s quantum Darwinism — the emergence of classical reality through environmental redundancy — has been tested in small quantum systems.

Results: qualitative support, not conclusive.

Objective collapse tests:

GRW and related objective collapse models predict small deviations from standard QM for large superpositions.

These are being tested with matter-wave interferometry (Arndt group, Vienna), optomechanical systems, and macroscopic quantum states.

No deviation from standard QM found — yet.

The measurement problem is not solved. The experiments are getting better. The problem is not going away.

VI. The Muon Anomalous Magnetic Moment

In April 2021, the Muon g-2 experiment at Fermilab announced:

\[a_\mu^{exp} = 116592061(41) \times 10^{-11}\]

The Standard Model prediction (as of 2020):

\[a_\mu^{SM} = 116591810(43) \times 10^{-11}\]

Discrepancy: \(\Delta a_\mu = 251 \times 10^{-11}\)

Significance: \(4.2\sigma\)

This is tantalizing — \(4.2\sigma\) is significant, \(5\sigma\) is the conventional discovery threshold.

If this discrepancy is real and not a theoretical error, it is evidence for new physics beyond the Standard Model.

The complication:

In 2021, a lattice QCD calculation (BMW collaboration) obtained a SM prediction that agrees with experiment within \(1.5\sigma\).

Different theoretical methods (dispersive vs. lattice) give different SM predictions.

The discrepancy may be: (a) real — new physics (b) a theoretical error in the dispersive approach (c) a statistical fluctuation

The community is not in agreement.

Run 2 and Run 3 data from Muon g-2 (released 2023) confirmed the experimental value. The theoretical uncertainty remains the issue.

This is the most tantalizing hint of new physics in current data — and the theoretical situation is unresolved.

VII. Other Anomalies — A Survey

Beyond the muon g-2, several other anomalies exist at varying levels of significance.

B meson anomalies (LHCb):

Measurements of ratios:

\[R_K = \frac{\mathcal{B}(B \to K\mu^+\mu^-)}{\mathcal{B}(B \to Ke^+e^-)}\] \[R_{K^*} = \frac{\mathcal{B}(B \to K^*\mu^+\mu^-)}{\mathcal{B}(B \to K^*e^+e^-)}\]

SM prediction: \(R_K = R_{K^*} = 1\) (lepton universality).

Previous LHCb measurements: \(R_K \approx 0.85\) (\(3.1\sigma\) deviation).

Update (2022): LHCb updated their analysis with improved methodology. New result: \(R_K = 0.994^{+0.090}_{-0.082}\)

Consistent with the SM. The anomaly disappeared.

This is important: an apparent \(3\sigma\) anomaly — taken seriously for years as potential new physics — vanished with improved analysis.

W boson mass (CDF, 2022):

The CDF experiment at Fermilab measured:

\[m_W = 80.4335 \pm 0.0094 \text{ GeV}\]

SM prediction: \(80.357 \pm 0.006\) GeV

Discrepancy: \(7\sigma\)

If true: the largest discrepancy from the SM ever measured.

But: ATLAS (LHC) measurement (2023): \(m_W = 80.360 \pm 0.016\) GeV

Consistent with the SM. Inconsistent with CDF.

The CDF result may reflect an unidentified systematic error. The situation is unresolved.

The lesson:

Anomalies appear and disappear. Extraordinary claims require extraordinary systematic control. The history of particle physics is littered with “discoveries” that vanished under scrutiny.

The physicist’s response to anomalies must be: take them seriously, but assume systematic error until proven otherwise.

VIII. The Hubble Tension

One of the most significant current tensions:

The Hubble constant \(H_0\):

CMB measurements (Planck 2018): \(H_0 = 67.4 \pm 0.5 \text{ km/s/Mpc}\) (early universe, \(z \sim 1100\))

Local distance ladder (SH0ES, 2022): \(H_0 = 73.04 \pm 1.04 \text{ km/s/Mpc}\) (late universe, \(z \lesssim 1\))

Discrepancy: \(\sim 5\sigma\)

Both measurements use completely independent methods. Both are well-understood systematic uncertainties. The discrepancy has grown more significant as measurements have improved.

Possible explanations:

  1. Systematic error in one or both measurements. (Most physicists’ first instinct.)

  2. New physics between recombination and today:
    • Early dark energy: an additional dark energy component active before recombination
    • Interacting dark matter
    • Modified gravity
  3. A fundamental flaw in the standard cosmological model.

The Hubble tension is the most statistically significant current discrepancy in cosmology.

If it persists — if future measurements (DESI, Euclid, Roman telescope) confirm both values — it requires new physics.

IX. What Is Not a Crisis

I want to be precise about what is NOT in crisis.

Precision tests of the Standard Model:

The anomalous magnetic moment of the electron:

\(a_e^{theory} = 0.001159652182032(720)\) \(a_e^{exp} = 0.00115965218059(13)\)

Agreement to 12 significant figures. No crisis here.

Gravitational waves:

LIGO/Virgo have detected \(\sim 100\) gravitational wave events. Binary black hole mergers, neutron star mergers. All consistent with General Relativity.

GW170817 — binary neutron star merger:

  • Gravitational wave + gamma-ray burst: \(\Delta t < 1.7\) s
  • Constraint on graviton mass: \(m_g < 7.7 \times 10^{-23}\) eV/c\(^2\)
  • Constraint on GW speed: $$ v_{GW} - c /c < 10^{-15}$$

General Relativity passes every test.

Neutrino physics:

Neutrino oscillations are established. The mixing parameters are being measured precisely. The mass ordering (normal vs. inverted) is being determined.

This is an active, productive field — not a crisis.

Cosmology:

The \(\Lambda\)CDM model fits CMB, BAO, supernovae, and large-scale structure with extraordinary precision.

The cosmological parameters are measured to percent-level accuracy.

The model is successful — apart from the Hubble tension and the unexplained nature of dark matter and dark energy.

X. The Honest Assessment

Here is the situation as I see it, trying to be as objective as possible:

What has been achieved:

The Standard Model is a complete, correct theory of all known particle physics. It will not be “overthrown” — it will be extended.

General Relativity is a complete, correct theory of classical gravity. It will be extended to include quantum effects.

The experimental tools — the LHC, gravitational wave detectors, CMB telescopes, dark matter experiments — are extraordinarily powerful.

What has not been achieved:

No confirmed extension of the Standard Model. No confirmed quantum gravity theory. No explanation of dark matter. No explanation of dark energy. No explanation of the baryon asymmetry. No solution to the measurement problem. No derivation of the constants of nature.

The uncomfortable facts:

The theories that dominated BSM physics from 1980 to 2010 — SUSY, extra dimensions, technicolor — have not been confirmed.

String theory has not made a single testable prediction in fifty years of development.

The two dominant frameworks for physics beyond the Standard Model — SUSY and string theory — are either excluded at natural scales (SUSY) or untestable (string theory).

This is a genuine problem. Not a catastrophe — but a problem.

The questions that remain:

Are we in a desert — no new physics between the electroweak scale and some much higher scale?

Or are we missing something conceptual — asking the wrong questions, using the wrong framework?

Or is new physics present but hidden — at slightly higher energies, or in very rare processes, or in gravitational effects we cannot yet measure?

We do not know.

XI. The Path Forward

What does the community propose?

Higher energy:

The FCC (Future Circular Collider) — a 100 km circumference collider at 100 TeV center-of-mass energy.

If approved and built (\(\sim\)2040s): would extend the energy frontier by factor \(\sim 8\). Could produce SUSY particles if they exist below \(\sim 10\) TeV.

Cost: $$\sim$€20 billion.

Precision:

The ILC (International Linear Collider): an electron-positron collider as a “Higgs factory” — measuring Higgs couplings to 0.1% precision.

Deviations from SM Higgs couplings would indicate new physics.

Dark matter:

Next-generation direct detection experiments (XENON-nT, LZ, PandaX-4T): extending sensitivity by factor \(\sim 100\).

If no signal: most of natural WIMP parameter space excluded.

Gravitational:

LISA (Laser Interferometer Space Antenna): space-based gravitational wave detector. Sensitive to mergers of supermassive black holes, early universe gravitational waves, possible exotic compact objects.

Could detect gravitational wave signals from phase transitions in the early universe — including the electroweak phase transition if it was first order.

Theoretical:

The amplituhedron (Arkani-Hamed et al.): a new mathematical object that computes scattering amplitudes in \(\mathcal{N}=4\) SYM without Feynman diagrams.

Whether this points to a new formulation of quantum gravity is unclear.

Celestial amplitudes, twistor theory, positive geometry — new mathematical frameworks for scattering amplitudes that may point beyond QFT.

XII. The Meta-Problem

Here is the deepest problem, which is rarely stated plainly:

We do not know what we are looking for.

In 1900, physicists knew what the problems were: the blackbody spectrum, the photoelectric effect, the stability of the atom.

The problems pointed toward quantum mechanics.

In 1905, the problems were: the incompatibility of electromagnetism and mechanics. The problem pointed toward special relativity.

In 1907, the problem was: the incompatibility of special relativity and gravity. The problem pointed toward general relativity.

In each case: a specific, concrete, well-defined problem pointed toward a specific, concrete solution.

Today’s problems are less specific.

“We need to unify gravity with quantum mechanics.” “We need to explain dark matter.” “We need to explain the constants.”

These are real problems. But they do not point to specific solutions the way the 1900 problems did.

We have the problems. We do not have the clue about what direction the solution lies in.

This is the deepest sense of the crisis: not that we have the wrong theories — but that we do not yet have the right problems.

Or rather: we have not yet found the experimental anomaly, the theoretical inconsistency, the empirical discrepancy that will crack the current framework open and reveal the path to the next one.

We are waiting for the data that will tell us where to look.

That data may be: the Hubble tension, if it persists. The muon g-2, if the theory settles. A dark matter signal, if it appears. A gravitational wave background, if LISA detects it. A Higgs coupling deviation, if the ILC measures it.

Or something entirely unexpected.

The history of physics suggests: when the breakthrough comes, it will not be where we expected.

It will be in some anomaly that seemed minor, that was attributed to systematic error, that someone decided to take seriously —

and could not explain with anything that already existed.

We are waiting.
