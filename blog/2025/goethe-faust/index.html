Goethe's Faust — The Man Who Made a Deal With the Devil and Won

Created on November 11, 2025

2025   ·   readings,   literature,

“Zwei Seelen wohnen, ach! in meiner Brust.”

“Two souls, alas, dwell in my breast.”

Heinrich Faust speaks these words in the first part of Goethe’s masterwork — standing at the city gate, watching the Easter crowds, feeling in himself the irreconcilable tension between the soul that clings to the earth and the soul that reaches toward the infinite.

He is a scholar. He has mastered philosophy, law, medicine, theology. He has spent his life in his study — surrounded by books, instruments, scrolls — and he has reached the end of knowledge and found it empty.

“Und sehe, daß wir nichts wissen können!”

“And I see that we can know nothing!”

This is not ignorance speaking. This is the exhaustion of mastery.

Faust knows everything that can be known in the world of the early 16th century. He has gone as far as learning can take him. And he has arrived, not at wisdom, but at the recognition that the infinite lies beyond the reachable, that the desire for total knowledge is a desire that no accumulation of knowledge can satisfy.

He is, in short, the most dangerous kind of human being: the intelligent person who has learned the limits of intelligence and cannot accept them.

Into this moment of crisis — Mephistopheles arrives.

I. Goethe and the Faust Legend

The historical Faust — if he existed at all — was a minor 16th-century figure: an alchemist, a charlatan, a wanderer who claimed magical powers and was dismissed by serious men including Phillip Melanchthon.

The legend grew.

The Historia von D. Johann Fausten (1587) — the first Faust book — told the story of a scholar who made a pact with the devil, enjoyed 24 years of supernatural power, and was torn apart by demons when the contract expired.

The moral was clear: pride destroys. The intellectual who reaches beyond human limits is punished. Stay within your place.

Christopher Marlowe’s Doctor Faustus (1592) followed this structure: Faustus sells his soul, enjoys knowledge and pleasure, repents too late, is damned.

The Faust legend was, originally, a warning against overreaching.

Goethe inverted it.

Johann Wolfgang von Goethe (1749-1832) spent sixty years writing his Faust.

The first sketches: 1770s. Faust, Part One: published 1808. Faust, Part Two: completed 1832, published posthumously.

Sixty years. A work that grew with its author — that contains, within its two parts, the entire trajectory of German idealism, the French Revolution, the birth of modernity, and Goethe’s own philosophical development from Storm and Stress through Classicism to something beyond both.

Goethe’s inversion of the legend:

Faust is not damned. Faust is saved.

Not because he repents. Not because he renounces his deal with the devil.

But because he never stops striving.

“Wer immer strebend sich bemüht, Den können wir erlösen.”

“Whoever strives with all his might, That man we can redeem.”

The angels speak these words at the end of Part Two, carrying Faust’s soul to heaven.

Mephistopheles, who bet that he could lead Faust to a moment of pure satisfaction — to the moment where Faust would say “Verweile doch! du bist so schön!” (“Stay a while! You are so beautiful!”) — and thereby win the wager —

loses.

Faust never stops wanting more. And in the endless wanting — in the refusal to be satisfied — lies his redemption.

This is Goethe’s radical inversion: the very quality that damns Faust in the legend saves him in Goethe.

II. The Wager in Heaven

The work begins not with Faust but with a Prologue in Heaven — a scene of breathtaking audacity.

God and Mephistopheles make a bet.

Mephistopheles:

“Was wettet ihr? den sollt ihr noch verlieren, Wenn ihr mir die Erlaubnis gebt, Ihn meine Straße sanft zu führen.”

“What do you wager? You shall lose him yet, If you give me permission To lead him gently on my path.”

God agrees.

This framing is everything.

Goethe places the Faust story within a cosmic wager — as in the Book of Job — in which the devil is permitted to test a human being.

But Goethe’s God is not the God of Job. He is serene, almost playful. He is not threatened by Mephistopheles.

“Des Menschen Tätigkeit kann allzuleicht erschlaffen, Er liebt sich bald die unbedingte Ruh; Drum geb ich gern ihm den Gesellen zu, Der reizt und wirkt und muß als Teufel schaffen.”

“Man’s activity can all too easily slacken, He quickly loves unconditional rest; So I gladly give him a companion Who provokes and works and must act as a devil.”

Mephistopheles is not the enemy of God. He is the agent of God’s purpose — the force that prevents stagnation, that keeps humanity in motion, that makes comfort impossible and therefore makes growth necessary.

The devil as the principle of restlessness.

This is not orthodox Christianity. It is Goethe’s mature philosophy — his conviction that negation, resistance, striving against, are as essential to human development as affirmation.

Mephistopheles himself states his nature:

“Ich bin der Geist, der stets verneint! Und das mit Recht; denn alles, was entsteht, Ist wert, daß es zugrunde geht.”

“I am the spirit that always negates! And rightly so; for everything that comes to be Deserves to perish.”

The devil is the spirit of negation — of nihilism, of destruction. But in Goethe’s dialectic, negation is necessary. Without the force that negates, nothing new is created.

Mephistopheles: “Ein Teil von jener Kraft, Die stets das Böse will und stets das Gute schafft.”

“A part of that force Which always wills evil and always creates good.”

The devil, willing evil, serves good against his will. This is Goethe’s profoundest irony: the principle of negation is part of the creative process.

III. The Pact — Precisely

Faust’s pact with Mephistopheles is often misunderstood.

It is not: I give you my soul in exchange for pleasure.

It is a wager — with the terms precisely stated by Faust himself:

“Werd ich zum Augenblicke sagen: Verweile doch! du bist so schön! Dann magst du mich in Fesseln schlagen, Dann will ich gern zugrunde gehn!”

“If ever I say to the passing moment: ‘Stay a while! You are so beautiful!’ Then you may cast me into chains, Then I will gladly go to my ruin!”

Faust bets that no moment will ever satisfy him — that he will never want time to stop, that the desire for more will always override the pleasure of the present.

Mephistopheles bets that he can produce such a moment — a moment of pure satisfaction that will make Faust want to stay.

This transforms the traditional pact.

Faust is not selling his soul for power. He is betting his soul on his own insatiability. He is wagering that he is the kind of being who cannot be satisfied — whose desire always exceeds its object.

He is betting on his own humanity.

IV. Gretchen — The Catastrophe of Part One

Mephistopheles leads Faust to Gretchen — Margarete — a young, innocent, deeply religious girl.

Faust falls in love with her — or falls into desire, or falls into the projection of his longing for simplicity, purity, the life he himself cannot live.

The distinction matters.

Gretchen is genuinely good — uncomplicated, faithful, loving. She represents the world that Faust’s restlessness has placed beyond his reach: the simple human happiness of ordinary life, ordinary love, ordinary faith.

Faust wants her. Mephistopheles provides the means.

The consequences: — Gretchen’s mother dies, poisoned by a sleeping draught Faust gives to allow their meetings — Her brother Valentin is killed by Faust in a duel — Gretchen is seduced, abandoned, becomes pregnant — She kills her illegitimate child — She is imprisoned, condemned to death

In the dungeon scene — one of the most devastating scenes in German literature — Faust finds Gretchen mad, offering to rescue her.

She refuses. She will not flee. She accepts her punishment.

As Faust and Mephistopheles flee, a voice from heaven:

“Ist gerettet!”

“She is saved!”

The innocent Gretchen — who sinned, who killed, who went mad — is saved.

Faust, who caused all of it, continues.

The moral is not simple. Goethe is not offering easy comfort.

Gretchen’s salvation comes from her absolute faith, her acceptance of guilt, her refusal to escape.

Faust’s condemnation — if it came — would come from his inability to accept limits, to stay, to be responsible for the wreckage his striving leaves behind.

Part One ends without resolution. Faust flees. The question of his guilt is deferred to Part Two.

V. Part Two — The Allegory of History

Faust, Part Two is a different work entirely.

Where Part One is personal — the drama of a soul — Part Two is cosmic, allegorical, historical.

It spans: — The court of the Holy Roman Emperor (a critique of political power without wisdom) — Ancient Greece (the marriage of Northern and Southern spirit) — The creation of Homunculus (the attempt to create life artificially) — The reclamation of land from the sea (Faust’s final project — and his final failure)

The language shifts from the colloquial directness of Part One to a dense, symbolic, allusive richness that has defeated many readers and translators.

This difficulty is intentional.

Part Two is not a drama to be followed. It is a meditation to be inhabited — slowly, with reference to Goethe’s sources and his philosophical development.

The Helena Act:

The centerpiece of Part Two: Faust summons Helena of Troy — the archetype of classical beauty.

He marries her. They have a son — Euphorion — who represents the union of classical form and romantic energy.

Euphorion cannot contain himself. He reaches too high. He falls. He is Icarus. He is Byron (whom Goethe had in mind).

With Euphorion’s death, Helena dissolves back into the classical world.

The union of North and South, of Romantic desire and Classical form — is possible only briefly. The energy of the modern world cannot sustain the calm of the ancient.

The land reclamation:

In old age, Faust undertakes a vast engineering project: reclaiming land from the sea, creating space for a new people, a new civilization.

This is his final project — the project for which he almost says the fatal words.

But even here, there is a shadow: Philemon and Baucis — an old couple whose cottage and chapel stand in the middle of Faust’s project — are killed when Mephistopheles burns their home at Faust’s implied command.

Even in the final vision, Faust’s striving destroys innocence.

And in the moment when Faust imagines the free people on his reclaimed land — he speaks the nearest approach to the fatal words:

“Zum Augenblicke dürft ich sagen: Verweile doch, du bist so schön!”

“To the moment I might say: Stay a while, you are so beautiful!”

Note: dürft ich — might say, not say.

The conditional. The subjunctive. He does not say it. He imagines saying it.

Mephistopheles believes he has won. He begins preparing Faust’s grave.

Faust dies. The angels descend. They carry his soul upward.

Mephistopheles, distracted by the beauty of the angels, fails to seize the soul.

The wager is lost.

Faust is redeemed — not by goodness (he was not good), not by faith (he did not believe), not by repentance (he did not repent) —

but by striving. By the refusal to be satisfied. By the forward motion of desire that never came to rest.

VI. Mephistopheles — The Most Interesting Character

The devil in Goethe’s work is not the devil of tradition.

He is not horrifying. He is not seductive in the simple sense. He is — and this is Goethe’s masterstroke —

funny.

Mephistopheles is witty, sardonic, intelligent, profoundly realistic about human nature, and deeply contemptuous of human pretension.

He sees through everything. He believes in nothing. He finds human aspiration absurd.

“Ich bin zu alt um nur zu spielen, Zu jung um ohne Wunsch zu sein.”

These are not his words — they are Faust’s — but they describe the predicament that Mephistopheles exploits.

His contempt for humanity is not cruelty. It is a kind of exhausted honesty.

He knows what humans are. He knows what they want. He knows the gap between aspiration and reality.

And he uses this knowledge not for grand dramatic evil but for the small, corrosive, entirely realistic corruption of getting people what they want.

The devil does not deceive Faust with false promises. He delivers what he promises. The corruption is in the wanting — not in the getting.

This is Goethe’s deepest insight into evil:

The devil is the accommodator — the one who says yes when wisdom would say no.

Mephistopheles does not force Faust. He enables him. He removes the obstacles that would otherwise force reflection.

He is the spirit of: “Why not? You want it. Here it is.”

And why not is, in Goethe’s vision, the most dangerous question in the world.

VII. The Philosophical Architecture

Faust is not only a drama. It is a philosophical system — Goethe’s mature philosophy expressed in dramatic form.

The polarity principle:

Goethe’s Naturphilosophie (his philosophy of nature) was organized around the principle of polarity:

Every phenomenon involves two opposing forces in dynamic tension.

Light and darkness. Expansion and contraction. Attraction and repulsion. The finite and the infinite.

Faust embodies this: the two souls in his breast — the earthly and the transcendent — are not to be resolved but to be held in tension.

The resolution of the tension into one pole is death — either physical or spiritual.

The life of the tension — the striving — is Faust’s vitality.

The Steigerung (intensification):

Goethe believed in Steigerung — the progressive intensification of natural and spiritual phenomena.

Life does not simply repeat. It spirals upward — returning to similar configurations but at higher levels of complexity.

Faust’s trajectory is Steigerung: each cycle of desire, achievement, failure leaves him at a higher level — more capable of the next striving, more capable of the deeper error, more capable of the greater vision.

The final vision — the free people on the reclaimed land — is only possible after everything that preceded it.

The redemption through striving:

The theological heart of Goethe’s Faust:

Salvation is not achieved by faith alone (Luther). Salvation is not achieved by works alone (Catholic).

Salvation is achieved by Streben — striving — by the dynamic, forward-moving energy that refuses rest and refuses satisfaction.

This is neither Lutheran nor Catholic. It is Goethean — a secular theology of effort that places the value of a life not in its achievements but in its continued motion.

“Es irrt der Mensch, solang er strebt.”

“Man errs as long as he strives.”

God says this in the Prologue. Error is the price of striving. Striving is the condition of redemption. Error and redemption are the same process.

VIII. Goethe Himself — The Man Behind the Work

To understand Faust, you must understand Goethe.

Johann Wolfgang von Goethe was the last universal man of European culture — the last figure who could plausibly claim competence in poetry, drama, philosophy, science, political administration, visual art, and the natural sciences.

He was: — The greatest German poet (Faust, The Sorrows of Young Werther, the Urfaust, the Italian Journey) — A practicing scientist (theory of color, comparative anatomy, discovery of the intermaxillary bone in humans, contributions to botany and geology) — The chief minister of Weimar for decades (running roads, mines, finances) — A visual artist of real accomplishment — A theater director — A collector — A friend of Schiller, Herder, Hegel

He lived to 82. He worked until the last months of his life.

He completed Part Two of Faust in the last year of his life — sealed the manuscript — and died weeks later.

The breadth of his life is not incidental to understanding Faust.

Faust’s tragedy — and his redemption — is the tragedy and redemption of the person who cannot specialize, who cannot accept a bounded existence, who must know and do and be everything — and who will fail at everything, and who must fail, and who is saved by the failing.

Goethe was Faust. He knew it.

He wrote himself into the work over sixty years — his crises, his loves, his political frustrations, his scientific obsessions, his old age — all of it is in Faust.

The work is the longest personal journal in German literature, disguised as a philosophical drama.

IX. The Language

Faust is untranslatable.

This is a strong claim. It is also accurate.

The work is written in multiple registers — sometimes in rhymed couplets (Knittelverse) that evoke medieval German poetry, sometimes in blank verse, sometimes in the Madrigal verse of the court, sometimes in Greek meters, sometimes in prose.

Each register carries meaning. The shift between registers tells you what kind of world you are in, what kind of consciousness is speaking.

The German original has a density — a compression of sound and meaning — that no translation preserves.

Consider:

“Grau, teurer Freund, ist alle Theorie, Und grün des Lebens goldner Baum.”

“Grey, dear friend, is all theory, And green the golden tree of life.”

The contrast of grey theory and green-golden life — the sound of the German (the assonance, the rhythm, the weight of “grau” against “grün”) — the paradox of “green golden tree” (green and gold are themselves opposed, yet both describe life) —

no translation catches all of this.

You must read Goethe in German. If you cannot read German — read him in German anyway, alongside a translation, and listen to what you hear even in what you cannot understand.

On translations:

The best English translations:

Stuart Atkins (1984): most faithful to the original verse forms. Uneven but often brilliant.

David Luke (1994): Oxford World’s Classics. Readable, scholarly, excellent notes. The best for the first-time reader.

Martin Greenberg (1992, 1998): two volumes. Extraordinary fidelity to tone. The most literary of the translations.

Walter Kaufmann (1961): Part One only. Perhaps the most readable English version. Takes liberties, but earns them.

None of these is Goethe. All of them are essential.

X. What Faust Says to Us Now

Faust is a 19th-century work about a 16th-century scholar. Why does it speak to the 21st century?

Because the problem it diagnoses has not been solved.

The problem of unlimited desire:

The modern world was built on the Faustian premise: that more is better, that limits are obstacles, that the proper response to any boundary is to push through it.

More knowledge. More technology. More wealth. More capability. More speed. More scale.

The Faustian drive produced the industrial revolution, modern science, the digital world, the entire apparatus of contemporary civilization.

It also produced: climate change, the destruction of ecosystems, nuclear weapons, the colonization and exploitation of everything that could be exploited.

The Faustian deal: unlimited power in exchange for the soul — for the inner life, for the relations of care, for the capacity to say “Enough. This is enough.”

Goethe saw this. He saw it in 1808. He saw it in the figure of his scholar — who wanted everything and therefore destroyed everything he touched.

The question Faust raises:

Is Faust’s redemption actually redemptive?

The angels carry his soul to heaven because he never stopped striving. But Philemon and Baucis are dead. Gretchen went mad.

The striving redeems the striver. The people in the striver’s path are not redeemed. They are sacrificed to the striving.

Is Goethe endorsing this? Is the redemption through striving a moral vision or a self-serving fantasy of the relentlessly ambitious?

Goethe does not resolve this. He lets it stand.

The work ends with Faust saved and the dead unresurrected.

The reader must decide what to do with this.

XI. Reading Faust — A Guide

If you have not read Faust:

Start with Part One. It is complete in itself. It is dramatically powerful — the Gretchen tragedy is one of the great tragic sequences in literature. It does not require knowledge of German idealism or classical mythology.

Read slowly.

The opening monologue — Faust in his study, surveying his knowledge and finding it empty — is one of the great openings in literature. It deserves an hour of your time.

Do not skip the Prologue in Heaven. The theological framework it establishes — God, Mephistopheles, the wager — is essential to everything that follows.

Pay attention to the Walpurgis Night. The great witches’ sabbath scene in Part One is often skimmed because it seems like a digression. It is not. It is Goethe’s diagnosis of modernity — a world of chaos, appetite, and spectacle with no organizing principle.

When you reach Part Two:

Do not try to read it as you read Part One. It is not drama. It is dream, allegory, and meditation.

Read it in sections. Let it sit. Return to it.

The Helena Act (Act III) is the most beautiful part. The land reclamation Act (Act V) is the most philosophically rich.

Read the secondary literature:

Harold Jantz, The Form of Faust (1978) Jane Brown, Goethe’s Faust: The German Tragedy (1986) Stuart Atkins, Goethe’s Faust: A Literary Analysis (1958)

Goethe himself: The Conversations with Eckermann — in which Goethe discusses Faust, his other works, and his philosophy with his secretary in his final years — is indispensable.

He says, among other things:

“The Germans are strange people. They make their life more difficult than necessary by seeking everywhere for deep thoughts and ideas. Just have the courage to give yourself up to your impressions, allow yourself to be delighted, moved, elevated, taught, inspired for something great; but do not imagine that all is vanity if it is not abstract thought and idea.”

He is describing how to read Faust. He is describing how to live.

Coda

“Das Ewig-Weibliche zieht uns hinan.”

“The eternal feminine draws us upward.”

The last line of Faust.

After everything — the deal with the devil, Gretchen’s ruin, the court of the Emperor, Helena, Euphorion, the land reclamation, the death —

a choir of angels, blessed women, penitents, and the soul of Gretchen draw Faust’s soul upward.

The “eternal feminine” is not simply women. It is the principle of love, of grace, of the beyond — the force that pulls the striving soul toward what is higher than any specific achievement.

It is what Faust was always reaching for — not the power, not the knowledge, not the pleasure — but the thing beyond the power, beyond the knowledge, beyond the pleasure.

The thing that cannot be named or possessed or stopped.

The thing that the wager — “Stay a while, you are so beautiful!” — would have stopped.

Faust never named it. He never stopped reaching for it. He was saved by not finding it.

This is Goethe’s most impossible idea:

That the not-arriving is itself the destination.

That the desire that cannot be satisfied is not a curse but a grace.

That the two souls in the breast — the one that clings to earth and the one that reaches for the infinite — are not enemies.

They are the condition of being human.

Both of them. Together. Always in tension. Never resolved.

Verweile doch —

Stay a while.

But do not stop.

Goethe — Italian Journey (1816) (trans. W.H. Auden & Elizabeth Mayer) — essential context for Part Two
