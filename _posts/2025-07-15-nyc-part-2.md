## I. Mornings at Bryant Park

Before anything else: the morning ritual.

Wake up.
Walk to Bryant Park.
Stop at Pret a Manger on the way.

*[photo: Pret a Manger or morning coffee]*

Pret a Manger does not need defending.
The sandwiches are fresh, the coffee is good,
and it is right there, between the hotel and the park.

Take the sandwich and the coffee into Bryant Park.
Find a chair — or better, the grass.
Lie down.
Look up at the buildings and the sky between them.

*[photo: Bryant Park morning]*

New York mornings in Bryant Park
are genuinely one of the better things
available to a person in this world.

The city is already moving —
it is always already moving —
but the park holds a small pocket of stillness.
People reading, people on their phones,
pigeons doing pigeon things,
office workers cutting through.

I stayed longer than I planned every time.

**Bryant Park in the evening:**

Bryant Park also does movie nights in summer —
free outdoor screenings on the lawn,
films projected on a large screen
with the Midtown skyline behind it.

*[photo: Bryant Park movie night if you have one]*

Hundreds of people on blankets and chairs.
Classic films mostly.
The city as a backdrop.

I have not managed to time a visit
to catch one of these.
This is on the list.

Bryant Park also hosts concerts —
free, regular, across genres.
I caught part of one once,
standing at the edge of the park
with my coffee,
not planning to stay,
staying anyway.

## II. The Bookstores

New York has bookstores
the way other cities have coffee shops —
everywhere, varied, each with its own character.

I visited as many as I could.

**The Strand**

The Strand is the one everyone knows.
18 miles of books, the sign says.
I believe it.

*[photo: Strand exterior or interior]*

The Strand is overwhelming in the best way.
Four floors, every section, new and used,
rare books upstairs in glass cases,
staff recommendation shelves
that are genuinely worth reading.

I have a rule at the Strand:
I go in with a budget.
I always spend more than the budget.
This is fine.

The rare books room upstairs —
first editions, signed copies,
books that are themselves objects —
is worth the trip even if you buy nothing.

I looked at a signed first edition of
a Hemingway novel for a long time.
I did not buy it.
I thought about it for the rest of the day.

*[photo: Strand interior, shelves]*

**Other bookstores:**

New York has independent bookstores
in almost every neighborhood —
each one reflecting its neighborhood,
its owners, its particular idea
of what a bookstore should be.

I wandered into several
without planning to.
This is the correct approach to
New York bookstores.

*[photo: any independent bookstore]*

The rule: if you walk past a bookstore
and something in the window catches your eye,
you go in.
You always go in.

## III. Chinatown and Little Italy

They are next to each other —
or rather, Chinatown has been slowly
absorbing Little Italy for decades,
which is either a shame or just
how neighborhoods work.

Both are still there.
Both are worth an afternoon.

**Chinatown:**

*[photo: Chinatown street]*

Dense, loud, completely itself.
Canal Street with its market stalls,
the fish markets with tanks in the windows,
the bakeries with pork buns and egg tarts,
the tea shops, the vegetable vendors.

I ate a pork bun standing on the street.
This is mandatory.

*[photo: pork bun or Chinatown food]*

Chinatown does not perform itself for tourists.
It is just what it is —
a neighborhood where people live and work,
that happens to have extraordinary food
available on every corner.

**Little Italy:**

*[photo: Little Italy street]*

Smaller than it used to be —
Mulberry Street is the main artery now,
a few blocks of restaurants and cafés
with red and green decorations
and menus in the windows.

It is slightly touristy.
It is also genuinely good.

I had a cannoli from a bakery on Mulberry Street
that was the correct size,
filled to order,
dusted with powdered sugar.

*[photo: cannoli or Little Italy café]*

I ate it walking down the street.
Also mandatory.

## IV. SoHo

SoHo is expensive now —
expensive shops, expensive restaurants,
expensive coffee in cups
that have been designed very carefully.

It is also beautiful.

*[photo: SoHo cast iron buildings]*

The cast iron architecture —
19th century buildings
with elaborate iron facades,
wide windows,
the particular light that comes through
those wide windows in the afternoon —

is some of the most distinctive
streetscape in America.

Walk down Greene Street or Broome Street
on a good afternoon
and the buildings do something
to your sense of what a city can look like.

The shops are too expensive.
I looked in windows.
This is free.

*[photo: SoHo street or window display]*

SoHo also has good galleries —
smaller than the major museums,
more varied, more willing to show
work that hasn't been canonized yet.

I walked into two or three.
Stayed in one for a while.
This is the other correct approach
to New York galleries:
walk in without a plan,
leave when you're ready.

## V. New Yorkers

A note on the people.

New Yorkers walk fast.
This is not rudeness — it is physics.
Eight million people in a small space,
all of them somewhere to be.

*[photo: busy New York street]*

The first time I was in New York
I kept getting in the way.
I was walking at a normal human pace
and people were flowing around me
the way water flows around a rock —
efficiently, without acknowledgment.

Now I walk faster.
I have learned.

New Yorkers are not actually rude.
They are efficient.
There is a difference.

Ask someone for directions —
genuinely, clearly —
and they will stop,
give you very specific directions,
possibly walk you partway there
if you seem lost enough.

They will not make small talk while doing it.
This is also fine.

**Seinfeld and Friends:**

I grew up watching both shows.

Seinfeld's New York:
the Upper West Side, Monk's Cafe,
Jerry's apartment on West 81st Street,
the specific rhythm of four people
whose entire lives happen
within a few city blocks.
The show is famously about nothing —
and the nothing is entirely New York:
the diner conversations,
the street arguments,
the elaborate social contracts
of people living too close together.

*[photo: Upper West Side street, or just a New York diner]*

Friends' New York:
less accurate, more aspirational —
those apartments do not exist
on those salaries,
that couch in Central Perk
is always available,
the city is somehow both enormous
and a small village
where everyone keeps running into everyone.

But Friends got something right too:
the energy of being young in New York,
the feeling that the city is a character
in whatever story you are living,
that around any corner
something interesting might be happening.

*[photo: Central Park or coffee shop]*

The first time I walked through
the Upper West Side,
I recognized it.
Not because I had been there —
but because I had seen it
a thousand times on a screen.

The buildings were the right height.
The streets were the right width.
The delis had the right signs in the windows.

New York was the first city I visited
that I already knew before I arrived.

That is what decades of television will do.

The city delivered on both shows —
the efficiency of Seinfeld's New York
and the energy of Friends' New York.
Slightly more Seinfeld than Friends,
if I'm being honest.

The apartments are smaller.
The couch at Central Perk is never free.

## VI. Broadway — The Unfinished Item

I have not been to Broadway.

This needs to be corrected.

Every visit, I think: this time.
Every visit, something else fills the days —
another museum, another neighborhood,
another afternoon in Bryant Park
that stretches longer than planned.

Broadway remains on the list.

**Hamilton:**

Hamilton is the specific thing I want to see.

*[photo: Hamilton marquee if you have one,
or Richard Rodgers Theatre exterior]*

Lin-Manuel Miranda's musical
about Alexander Hamilton —
told through hip-hop, R&B, and show tunes,
with a cast that reflects
the America Hamilton's generation
was building but could not yet imagine.

I have listened to the cast recording
more times than I will admit here.

I know most of the lyrics.

I have not seen it live.

This is an ongoing situation
that will be resolved
on the next visit.

Or the one after that.
But it will be resolved.

The Richard Rodgers Theatre on 46th Street —
I have walked past it.
I looked at the marquee.
I did not go in because I had not planned ahead
and the tickets are expensive
and I told myself next time.

Next time.

## VII. The List That Doesn't Finish

Every visit to New York
adds more to the list than it removes.

**Done:**
The Met. MoMA. The Strand.
Bryant Park mornings. Lobster rolls.
Chinatown pork buns. Little Italy cannoli.
Filaga pizza. The High Line.
The NYPL Treasures. SoHo streets.
Refik Anadol. Basquiat. Van Gogh twice.

**Still on the list:**
Hamilton.
Bryant Park movie night.
The Frick Collection.
The Whitney.
A proper diner breakfast — eggs, coffee, the works.
The Brooklyn Botanic Garden in spring.
Coney Island, once, just to say I went.
A full day in the West Village
with no plan at all.
Jazz somewhere small and dark
on a weeknight.
The Tenement Museum on the Lower East Side.
One full evening just walking
without any destination.

New York is the only city I know
where the list grows faster than you can walk.

This is not a problem.
It is the point.

*New York, multiple visits.*
*The list continues.*
