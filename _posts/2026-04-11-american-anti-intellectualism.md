In Paris, in 1945,
Jean-Paul Sartre was a celebrity.

Not a celebrity in the academic sense —
not famous among philosophers
and known to the educated public.

A celebrity in the full sense:
recognized on the street,
photographed by paparazzi,
quoted in newspapers by people
who had not read his books
but knew his name and his positions.

His café — Les Deux Magots —
was a destination.
His arguments about freedom and commitment
were debated in bars and kitchens
by people who worked in factories and offices.

He was, in the precise sense,
a **public intellectual** —
an intellectual whose work
circulated beyond the academy
and entered public life as public life.

France has had a continuous tradition of these:
Voltaire, Rousseau, Zola,
Sartre, Beauvoir, Camus,
Foucault, Derrida, Bourdieu,
and their successors today.

America does not have this tradition.

America has universities.
America has think tanks.
America has public commentators —
pundits, columnists, podcasters.

But the philosopher who shapes
public moral and political imagination —
the thinker whose ideas
become the common vocabulary
of people who have never studied philosophy —

this figure is almost entirely absent
from American public life.

Why?

---

## I. Tocqueville's Warning

The diagnosis begins in 1835.

Alexis de Tocqueville —
a French aristocrat visiting America —
published *Democracy in America*,
the most perceptive account of American civilization
ever written by an outsider.

He admired much.
He also noticed something troubling:

*"I know of no country where there is generally
less independence of mind
and real freedom of debate
than in America."*

And more specifically:

*"In America, the majority builds an impenetrable wall
around thought.
Within these limits, a writer is free;
but woe to him who dares to go beyond them."*

Tocqueville identified
what he called the **tyranny of the majority** —
not the political tyranny of a despot,
but the social tyranny of consensus:

The pressure to conform,
not through legal force,
but through the withdrawal of social approval —
the isolation, the dismissal,
the casual disregard
that greets anyone who diverges
too far from what most people think.

In a democracy,
the majority is not only the government.
The majority is the culture.
And in American democracy,
the majority's opinion
functions as a kind of gravity —
pulling all thought toward the center,
toward the practical, toward the common sense,
toward what everyone already knows.

The intellectual who stands apart from this —
who insists on the uncomfortable,
the complex, the counterintuitive —
is not persecuted.

They are simply ignored.

Tocqueville wrote this in 1835.
It has not improved.

## II. The Pragmatist Tradition

American philosophy has a native tradition:
**pragmatism.**

Peirce, James, Dewey —
the great American philosophical school —
defined truth not as correspondence to reality
but as what works.

A belief is true if it produces
successful action in the world.
A theory is valuable if it makes
a practical difference.
Philosophy must be useful —
must connect to experience,
must improve life,
must solve problems.

William James:

*"The true is only the expedient
in the way of our thinking,
just as 'the right' is only the expedient
in the way of our behaving."*

John Dewey:

*"Philosophy recovers itself
when it ceases to be a device for dealing
with the problems of philosophers
and becomes a method, cultivated by philosophers,
for dealing with the problems of men."*

This is a genuine philosophical tradition —
rich, sophisticated, influential.

But it has a cultural consequence:

**It licenses the dismissal of impractical thought.**

If truth is what works —
if philosophy must solve the problems of ordinary life —
then the philosopher who works on abstract problems
with no immediate application
is not merely specialized.

They are, in the pragmatist framework,
doing something suspect —
something that may not even be real philosophy.

The pragmatist tradition
gave American culture a philosophical justification
for its pre-existing suspicion
of useless thinking.

## III. Anti-Intellectualism as American Tradition

Richard Hofstadter won the Pulitzer Prize in 1964
for *Anti-Intellectualism in American Life.*

His argument:
anti-intellectualism is not a deviation
from American values.
It is an expression of them.

**Evangelical Christianity:**

The revivalist tradition in American religion —
from the Great Awakening through the 19th century —
celebrated direct emotional experience of God
over theological learning.

The learned minister was suspect.
Too much education, the tradition implied,
might distance you from God —
might replace faith with argument,
experience with abstraction.

The revivalist preacher who spoke
from the gut, from the heart,
from direct access to the Holy Spirit —
this was the American religious ideal.

Not the theologian.

**Jacksonian democracy:**

Andrew Jackson's presidency (1829-1837)
celebrated the "common man"
against the educated elite.

The expert was suspect.
The professional was suspect.
The man of learning was suspect.

What was trusted:
practical experience,
common sense,
the wisdom of ordinary people
doing ordinary things.

This is not merely political rhetoric.
It is a deep American cultural value —
the suspicion that too much learning
corrupts the natural good sense
that ordinary Americans possess.

**The business culture:**

American capitalism produced a specific ideal:
the self-made man —
the entrepreneur who created value
through practical ingenuity,
not through inherited knowledge
or academic credentials.

Carnegie. Rockefeller. Ford. Edison.

None of them had advanced degrees.
All of them were celebrated
as quintessentially American —
their lack of academic formation
not a deficiency but a virtue,
proof that real intelligence is practical,
that real success comes from doing,
not from thinking.

The businessman became the American hero.

The intellectual —
who thinks without building,
who criticizes without creating,
who questions without solving —
became, in this cultural framework,
a suspicious figure.

Parasitic on productive society.
Too clever to be trusted.
Too abstract to be useful.

## IV. The Frankfurt School's American Experience

In the 1930s and 1940s,
some of the greatest European intellectuals
fled to America.

The Frankfurt School —
Adorno, Horkheimer, Marcuse, Fromm —
landed in New York and California.

They brought with them
a tradition of critical theory:
the analysis of culture, ideology, and capitalism
using a synthesis of Marx, Freud, and Hegel.

They encountered something they had not expected.

Not persecution.
Not censorship.

**Indifference.**

Adorno, in California,
studying the culture industry,
watching American popular culture
absorb everything:

*"The culture industry
perpetually cheats its consumers
of what it perpetually promises.
The promissory note which,
with its plots and staging,
it draws on pleasure
is endlessly prolonged."*

What Adorno observed:
American culture was extraordinary
at absorbing criticism —
at commodifying dissent,
at packaging the radical
as entertainment,
at neutralizing the dangerous
by making it popular.

The critic who attacked consumer culture
found their books in the consumer culture's bookstores.
The philosopher who critiqued the spectacle
found themselves on the spectacle's television.

The system was not hostile to ideas.
It was indifferent to them —
which is worse.

You cannot fight indifference.
It does not argue back.
It simply does not engage.

Marcuse stayed.
He became influential in the 1960s counterculture —
*One-Dimensional Man* became a student movement text.

Adorno and Horkheimer returned to Germany.

They could not find, in America,
the public they needed —
the audience that would take ideas seriously
as ideas, not as consumer products
or political tools.

## V. The Structural Problem — The University and the Public

In Europe, the university professor
has traditionally had a public role.

The French **normalien** —
graduate of the École Normale Supérieure —
was expected to engage in public debate,
to write for general audiences,
to take positions on political and moral questions.

The German **professor** —
holder of an academic chair —
was a public figure,
whose expertise carried cultural authority
beyond the seminar room.

In America, the university was shaped differently.

**The research university model (post-Civil War):**

The American research university —
modeled partly on the German university
but modified by American conditions —
made the production of specialized knowledge
its primary purpose.

The professor's job:
to produce research,
to train graduate students,
to advance a discipline.

Not to form citizens.
Not to engage the public.
Not to participate in the **Öffentlichkeit** —
the public sphere —
as European academics were expected to.

The **publish-or-perish** system
rewarded narrow specialization.

The professor who wrote for general audiences
was spending time that should be spent
on peer-reviewed publications.

The professor who engaged in public debate
was doing something that counted for nothing
in the tenure file.

The system actively discouraged
the public intellectual role.

**The disciplinary fragmentation:**

American academia became
intensely specialized.

The philosopher of language.
The historian of 14th-century Catalonia.
The economist specializing in
labor markets in sub-Saharan Africa.

Each field has its journals,
its conferences,
its internal debates —
conducted in a technical vocabulary
inaccessible to outsiders.

The intellectual who crosses boundaries —
who writes about ethics AND politics AND economics
AND culture simultaneously —
is suspect in every discipline.

Not specialist enough to be trusted.
Not rigorous enough to be cited.
Writing for a general audience
is taken as evidence
that you are not serious.

## VI. France vs. America — The Structural Difference

Why does France produce public intellectuals
and America does not?

**The French grandes écoles:**

The École Normale Supérieure,
Sciences Po, the École Polytechnique —
these institutions train
a small elite that is expected
to move between disciplines,
between academia and journalism,
between theory and practice.

The normalien is not a specialist.
They are an intellectual — full stop.
Expected to have views on everything
and to express them publicly.

This tradition is institutional.
It is reproduced deliberately.

**The French intellectual press:**

*Le Monde, Le Figaro, Libération, Le Nouvel Observateur* —
French newspapers and magazines
print philosophical and cultural essays
at length,
by academics,
for general audiences.

The essay form — the intellectual essay —
has a home in French journalism.

American newspapers do not print
3,000-word essays on the nature of power
by philosophy professors.

They print op-eds.
800 words.
Accessible. Practical. Immediately relevant.
Not what Sartre wrote.

**The café culture:**

This is perhaps too romantic —
but not entirely wrong.

The Parisian café as intellectual space —
where ideas circulate
between academics, writers, journalists,
students, and interested citizens —
has no American equivalent.

The American equivalent is the university seminar.
Closed. Credentialed. Inaccessible.

The café is public.
The seminar is private.

## VII. The American Exceptions — And What They Reveal

America has had public intellectuals.

**The New York Intellectuals (1930s-1960s):**

Lionel Trilling, Irving Howe, Philip Rahv,
Mary McCarthy, Edmund Wilson, Hannah Arendt —
writing in *Partisan Review*, *Commentary*, *The New York Review of Books.*

Genuinely public: they wrote about
literature, politics, philosophy, culture
for educated general audiences.

Genuinely intellectual: they took ideas seriously
as ideas, not as tools.

Why did they exist?
Several reasons:
— They were mostly outsiders — Jewish intellectuals
  at the margins of WASP cultural institutions —
  with less investment in academic respectability
— They wrote in magazines that paid for serious ideas
— They had a specific audience: the educated non-academic
  who read seriously but was not in the university

They were possible because of specific historical conditions.
Those conditions no longer obtain.

**Noam Chomsky:**

The most widely read American intellectual globally —
cited, translated, influential in every country except his own.

In America: marginalized, dismissed as a crank,
virtually absent from mainstream media.

The contrast is instructive.
Chomsky's ideas travel everywhere except home —
because his criticism of American power
is exactly the kind of thought
that the tyranny of American consensus
makes invisible.

Not censored. Marginalized.
There is a difference.
Marginalization is more effective.

**Cornel West:**

Genuinely public, genuinely intellectual —
appears on television, engages popular culture,
writes for general audiences.

But note: West is a Black intellectual
in the tradition of Du Bois —
a tradition that was public by necessity,
because the Black intellectual
could not retreat into the white academy
and could not afford to be invisible
in public life.

The Black public intellectual tradition
is one of the few genuine public intellectual traditions
in American life —
produced by exclusion from the private academy
and urgency about the public stakes.

## VIII. The Internet — Promise and Failure

The internet seemed to promise
a new public sphere.

Substack, podcasts, YouTube —
the dismantling of the gatekeepers
who kept intellectuals invisible.

The promise has been partially kept.

There are serious thinkers
building genuine audiences online.
Tyler Cowen, Scott Alexander,
Freddie deBoer, Agnes Callard —
people doing real intellectual work
and reaching real audiences.

But the internet has also reproduced
the American pathologies at scale:

**The platform replaces the argument.**
The number of subscribers
is the measure of seriousness.

**The practical drives out the theoretical.**
The most successful online intellectuals
write about productivity, money, relationships —
the pragmatic concerns of daily life.
The philosopher of consciousness
has fewer readers than the life coach.

**Speed replaces depth.**
The newsletter must come out weekly.
The podcast must come out daily.
The thinking must fit the production schedule.

The internet gave intellectuals a platform.
It gave them the platform on the platform's terms.

The terms are American terms:
practical, accessible, frequent, engaging.

Not wrong terms.
But not sufficient for the kind of thinking
that France produces.

## IX. What America Loses

The invisibility of American intellectuals
is not merely a cultural aesthetic failure.
It has concrete consequences.

**Policy without theory:**

American policy debates are conducted
in the language of pragmatism —
what works, what is practical,
what can be sold to voters.

The deeper questions —
what kind of society do we want to be,
what values should animate our institutions,
what is the philosophical basis of our rights —
are conducted without philosophers.

Policy is made without philosophy.

This produces policy
that is technically sophisticated
and philosophically incoherent —
that can optimize for metrics
without being able to ask
whether the metrics measure what matters.

**Democracy without citizens:**

Tocqueville's fear was not merely cultural.
It was political.

Democracy requires citizens who think —
who can evaluate arguments,
who have the intellectual resources
to participate in self-governance,
who cannot be easily manipulated
by the confident and the visible.

The invisible intellectual
leaves a vacuum.

The vacuum is filled by
the pundit, the influencer, the demagogue —
the visible without the knowledge,
the confident without the expertise.

**The colonization by expertise from elsewhere:**

When Americans want philosophical frameworks,
they import them.

Rawls is an exception —
but Rawls is read more carefully in Europe
than in American policy discussions.

Foucault, Habermas, Bourdieu, Rancière —
the frameworks that educated Americans use
to think about power, justice, culture —
are largely European.

America has exported its practical knowledge.
It has imported its theoretical frameworks.

This is an intellectual trade deficit
with specific structural causes.

## X. The Foucault-Chomsky Debate — A Case Study in Visibility

In November 1971,
Dutch television broadcast a debate
between Michel Foucault and Noam Chomsky.

Two hours.
Two of the greatest living thinkers.
On human nature, power, justice, and the state.

In the Netherlands, it was a television event.
In France, it was discussed for years.
Across Europe, it became a reference point —
a moment in the public intellectual tradition
that people cited, argued about,
returned to.

In America: almost unknown
until it was uploaded to YouTube decades later.

The debate is extraordinary.

Foucault and Chomsky disagree
on almost everything —
on human nature (Chomsky believes in it,
Foucault is skeptical),
on the foundation of justice
(Chomsky appeals to natural rights,
Foucault argues all justice claims
are themselves expressions of power),
on the role of the intellectual
in political struggle.

But they agree on one thing:
that these questions matter,
that they are worth two hours of television,
that the public deserves to think about them.

Imagine this on American television in 1971.
Or in 2024.

It is not imaginable.

Not because Americans could not follow it.
Because the system that produces American television
has no category for it.

It is not news.
It is not entertainment.
It is not practical.
It does not tell you how to live better,
make more money, lose weight,
or understand what happened today.

It is two people thinking in public.

This — thinking in public —
is precisely what American television
has never been able to accommodate.

**The flaneur and the hustle:**

Baudelaire's **flaneur** — the wanderer,
the observer of the city,
the person who has nowhere to be
and therefore sees everything —
is a distinctly European figure.

The flaneur requires something
American culture has never accepted
as a legitimate use of time:

**purposeless presence.**

The flaneur is not productive.
They are not networking.
They are not optimizing.
They are simply walking, watching, thinking.

This is, in the American framework, waste.

*"Time is money"* — Benjamin Franklin,
the founding pragmatist,
said this in 1748.

It became the organizing principle
of a civilization.

If time is money,
the flaneur is spending money
for no return.
The philosopher thinking useless thoughts
is wasting capital.
The intellectual debating justice on television
is two hours of productivity
that will never be recovered.

The European city — Paris, Berlin, Vienna —
was built for the flaneur:
cafés where you can sit for three hours
with one coffee,
squares designed for wandering,
a public life that accommodates
the person who has no destination.

The American city — with exceptions —
was built for productivity:
the grid, the car, the suburb,
the destination always predetermined,
the wandering designed out.

You cannot be a flaneur in a city
that was built against flanerie.

You cannot be a public intellectual
in a culture that was built
against purposeless thought.

## X. The Possibility of Change

I want to end with what is possible —
not what is probable.

**The conditions for American public intellectuals:**

A public intellectual requires:

1. **An audience** — people who want serious ideas,
   who are not satisfied by entertainment
   disguised as thought.

2. **A medium** — a publication, a platform,
   that will sustain long-form serious work
   without requiring it to be immediately practical.

3. **Institutional support** — or freedom from institutional pressure —
   the ability to think across disciplines
   without career penalty.

4. **A tradition** — the sense that this is a legitimate role,
   that the philosopher-in-public
   is not wasting their time.

None of these is impossible.
All of them are structurally undermined
by current American conditions.

**The reason for cautious hope:**

The conditions that made the New York Intellectuals possible —
outsiders with no stake in academic respectability,
specific magazines that paid for serious work,
a moment of genuine political urgency —
can, in different forms, recur.

The political urgency is present.
The outsiders exist —
in the margins of the academy,
in the new online platforms,
in the communities that have always been
excluded from the comfortable consensus.

The question is whether
the specific American aversion to useless thinking —
the pragmatist inheritance,
the Jacksonian suspicion,
the tyranny of the majority —
can be overcome by the urgency
of the current moment.

Tocqueville thought it could not.
He may have been right.

He may also have been describing
the structural condition
rather than the permanent condition —
the features of a young democracy
that could, in time, mature
into something more willing
to tolerate the uncomfortable,
the abstract,
the useless.

We are still waiting.

## Coda

Baudrillard is known in France.
In America, he is known to people
who took a theory course in college
and remember the word "simulacrum."

This is not because Americans are less intelligent.
It is because America built a civilization
in which the question Baudrillard asked —
*what is real, and how do we know?* —
was, for most of American history,
less urgent than the question:
*what works, and how do we build it?*

A young country building itself
from wilderness to superpower
in two hundred years
did not have much use for Baudrillard.

It had use for engineers.
For lawyers.
For entrepreneurs.
For soldiers.

The thinker who questions the foundations —
who asks whether what we are building
is worth building,
whether the real is real,
whether the common sense is sense —

this thinker is useful
when the building is done.

America is not sure the building is done.

It may never be.

And so the philosopher waits —
visible to everyone in Europe,
invisible at home —

still asking the questions
that a civilization in a hurry
does not have time to hear.
