There's a Turkish idiom: *"Bana jazz yapma!"*

It means don't act irrational to me. Be serious.

I was about six years old when I first heard this phrase. I didn't understand it. *Jazz?* Why would being irrational have anything to do with jazz? The word sounded strange in my mouth. Foreign. Musical. Full of air.

Then I saw a documentary on TRT. A man with a big brass instrument. He blew into it. The sound was unlike anything I had ever heard. A saxophone. For some reason, I loved this word. This instrument. I don't know why.

From that moment on, I started saying I loved jazz music. I loved it before I understood it.

The adults around me — neighbors, my parents' friends — probably didn't know much about jazz either. But I kept saying it anyway.

---

## The Ticket

This past Christmas, I had a ticket from Dallas to Izmir. Ten days. I was packed. I was ready.

Then I got sick. My doctor said the flight would be difficult. She said I should cancel.

So I canceled.

And I went to New Orleans instead, Louisiana.

---

## Bourbon Street

I arrived in December. Christmas season. The streets were packed. Tourists everywhere. Street musicians on every corner. Jazz pouring out of bars and cafés and restaurants.

I grew up in a French-focused school in Turkey. We learned that Francophone culture was the height of sophistication. We learned to revere France.

Then I walked down the streets of New Orleans and heard French. Americans speaking French with American accents. A Parisian would cringe. But it was French.

The restaurants had pushed tables onto the sidewalks. Wrought-iron balconies above the street. People sitting on them, drinking. It looked like Paris. But it was New Orleans.

---

## Food

I ate constantly.

Gumbo. Seafood. So much seafood. Beignets at Café du Monde. Powdered sugar on my face, my hands, my dog Nikko.

I saw fried alligator on a menu. Americans eat interesting things, I thought.

Every restaurant welcomed Nikko. This is not common in Dallas. I dined in restaurants with my dog beside me.

---

## Walking

I walked through the French Quarter. Street by street.

Rampart Street. Canal Street. Then Bourbon Street. The heart of everything. The music is loudest there. Everything is happening at once.

I saw women outside strip clubs, nearly naked, posing for photos with tourists. I saw a family with a child doing the same thing. How is a child here at this hour? I thought. I said nothing.

This is America. This is New Orleans.

---

## The Music

There is no substitute for live jazz.

21st Amendment Bar had music. Most restaurants and cafés had live jazz. You didn't have to go to a bar. The music was just there. In the street.

Every corner had jazz. Every café. Every bar. It didn't matter where you went. Jazz was playing.

---

## The Museum

I went to the New Orleans Jazz Museum.

It was small. I loved it. I spent a long time there.

---

## Back to Dallas

The drive back took seven hours.

I had burned a ticket to Turkey. I had lost ten days with family. Instead, I went to a city where jazz lives in the streets, in the cafés, in the restaurants. Everywhere.

I will go back.
