*"Omnia, Lucili, aliena sunt, tempus tantum nostrum est."*

Everything, Lucilius, belongs to others.
Time alone is ours.

Seneca wrote this in a letter.
He was an old man.
He had been one of the most powerful men in Rome —
tutor and advisor to Nero,
the most feared emperor in history.
He had been extraordinarily wealthy.
He had been exiled, recalled, celebrated, suspected.

And he sat down and wrote to his friend Lucilius:
*time alone is ours.*

Not the palace. Not the money. Not the influence.
Not the books or the reputation or the name.

Time.

And then, immediately, the devastating follow-up:

*"Yet such is the ebb and flow of fortune
that we allow time — the one thing that is truly ours —
to be taken from us."*

---

## I. The Man

Lucius Annaeus Seneca was born around 4 BCE
in Córdoba, in Roman Spain.

He came to Rome as a child,
studied rhetoric and philosophy,
became a brilliant advocate and writer,
and rose to prominence under the emperor Claudius.

Then Claudius's wife accused him of adultery
with the emperor's niece.
He was exiled to Corsica for eight years.

He was recalled by Agrippina —
Claudius's new wife, Nero's mother —
to serve as tutor to her son.

When Nero became emperor at sixteen,
Seneca was his most important advisor.
For several years, the partnership worked —
Seneca's moderation restraining the emperor's cruelty.

Then it didn't.

Nero became what Nero became.
Seneca retreated from public life —
tried to give back his wealth,
tried to disengage from the court.

In 65 CE, Nero accused him of involvement
in the Pisonian conspiracy — a plot to assassinate the emperor.
The evidence was thin. It didn't matter.

Nero sent soldiers to Seneca's villa
with an order to die.

Seneca opened his veins.

He died slowly — the veins of an old man
do not bleed quickly.
He took poison; it didn't work.
He was placed in a hot bath to hasten the bleeding.

He dictated to scribes until he could no longer speak.

He died as he had written — discussing philosophy
in the face of death,
practicing what he had preached
in letter after letter,
essay after essay,
for thirty years.

## II. On the Shortness of Life

*De Brevitate Vitae* — On the Shortness of Life —
is perhaps the most important thing Seneca wrote.

It begins with a paradox:

*"It is not that we have a short time to live,
but that we waste a great deal of it."*

Life is not short.
Life is long enough —
more than long enough —
if used well.

The problem is not the quantity of time.
It is what we do with it.

We give our time to everyone who asks.
We guard our money carefully —
we are appalled if someone wastes our property —
but we let people waste our hours,
our days, our years,
without protest, without even noticing.

*"People are frugal in guarding their personal property;
but as soon as it comes to squandering time
they are most wasteful of the one thing
in which it is right to be stingy."*

Seneca identifies the thieves of time:

**The ambitious** — always preparing to live,
always arranging the conditions under which life will begin.
First I need this promotion. Then this house. Then this security.
Then I will begin to live.

**The pleasure-seekers** — always consuming,
never present, always moving to the next experience.

**The distracted** — pulled in every direction
by other people's urgencies,
never attending to their own.

**The greedy** — spending their life accumulating
what life cannot be lived without spending.

All of them, Seneca says, are dying badly —
not because death is near
but because they are not living.

*"They lose the day in expectation of the night,
and the night in fear of the dawn."*

## III. The Letters to Lucilius

The *Epistulae Morales* — Moral Letters to Lucilius —
are Seneca's masterpiece.

One hundred and twenty-four letters,
written in the last years of his life,
to his friend Gaius Lucilius Junior —
a man younger than Seneca,
a government official in Sicily,
who had apparently asked Seneca
to help him live better.

The letters are not treatises.
They are not systematic philosophy.
They are the most intimate philosophical document
from the ancient world —
a man thinking out loud,
revising himself,
admitting uncertainty,
sharing what he has learned and what he has failed to learn.

They begin, famously:

*"Ita fac, mi Lucili: vindica te tibi."*

*Do this, my Lucilius: claim yourself for yourself.*

The first letter. The first sentence.
Already everything is there.

Claim yourself for yourself —
take back the time that has been dispersed,
the attention that has been scattered,
the self that has been given away
in small pieces to everyone who asked.

Each letter takes up a theme —
friendship, travel, the fear of death,
anger, grief, luxury, poverty, solitude, crowds —
and works it through with a combination
of personal reflection, philosophical argument,
and the occasional devastating aphorism
that lodges in the memory and will not leave.

## IV. On Friendship

Seneca's letters are, above all, about friendship.

Not friendship as a social category —
as acquaintance, as usefulness, as pleasant company.

Friendship as the deepest form of human connection —
the relationship in which another person
is genuinely known and genuinely valued.

*"Hoc primum philosophia promittit:
sensum communem, humanitatem et congregationem."*

*"Philosophy promises above all:
common sense, humanity, and fellowship."*

And more directly:

*"Sine amico nemo tutus vivere potest."*

*"Without a friend, no one can live securely."*

But Seneca is precise about what friendship requires:

*"Before you make a friend,
judge whether he is worthy to be made a friend.
After you have made a friend,
trust him with everything."*

The order matters.
Judgment first. Then total trust.
There is no friendship without judgment —
the assessment of character, of values, of reliability.
And there is no friendship with only judgment —
the trust must be complete, or it is not friendship.

He also says something that feels almost modern
in its psychological precision:

*"Recede in te ipse quantum potes."*

*"Retire into yourself as much as you can."*

Solitude and friendship are not opposites for Seneca.
They are complementary disciplines.
You must know yourself before you can truly know another.
And you must know another to truly know yourself.

The friend is a mirror —
not a flattering one,
but an honest one.

*"Refer everything to a good person
who you would want to imitate.
Live as if he is watching."*

## V. On Death

Seneca returns to death in almost every letter.

Not morbidly — not with the fascination of depression —
but with the insistence of a man who has understood
that the refusal to think about death
is the primary source of the wasted life.

*"Omnia, Lucili, aliena sunt, tempus tantum nostrum est."*

Time is ours — and it runs out.
To not know this — to live as if you will not die —
is to be unable to choose.

You cannot choose well what to do with your life
if you do not know how much of it remains.
You cannot prioritize correctly
if you believe there will always be more time.

*"Cogita quantum temporis absumpserit."*

*"Think how much time has already been consumed."*

Not to produce guilt —
but to produce clarity.

The Stoic exercise of **memento mori** —
remember that you will die —
is not pessimism.
It is the most practical philosophical tool Seneca describes:
the practice of holding death in mind
as the clarifying horizon against which
all choices become legible.

What matters, if today were the last day?
What would you stop doing?
What would you start?

*"Dum differtur vita transcurrit."*

*"While we are postponing, life speeds by."*

And his most personal formulation —
written, perhaps, when the soldiers were already on their way:

*"Omnia, Lucili, aliena sunt, tempus tantum nostrum est.
In hoc unum avarus esse licet."*

*"Everything belongs to others, Lucilius; time alone is ours.
In this one thing it is permitted to be a miser."*

Be a miser with time.
Give it to no one carelessly.
Spend it on nothing that does not deserve it.

## VI. The Stoic Framework

Seneca was a Stoic —
part of the philosophical school founded by Zeno of Citium
in Athens around 300 BCE.

But he was an unusual Stoic —
the most human of them,
the most willing to admit difficulty,
the most honest about the gap
between what philosophy teaches
and what a person actually does.

The Stoic framework:

**The dichotomy of control:**
Some things are up to us — our judgments, desires, aversions.
Some things are not — our body, reputation, wealth, other people.

Unhappiness comes from wanting what is not up to us.
Freedom comes from wanting only what is up to us.

**The discipline of desire:**
Desire only what virtue requires.
Prefer — but do not require — health, wealth, friendship.
Be indifferent — in the technical sense — to everything external.

**Living according to nature:**
The rational animal's nature is reason.
To live well is to live rationally —
in accordance with the logos,
the rational principle that structures reality.

Seneca accepted all of this.
He also accepted that he often failed to live it.

This honesty is what distinguishes him
from the more austere Stoics —
Epictetus, who was enslaved and never complained,
Marcus Aurelius, who ruled an empire
and wrote his private doubts in a notebook.

Seneca was wealthy, powerful, sometimes hypocritical —
he wrote about the dangers of wealth
while being extraordinarily rich.
He wrote about the importance of not fearing death
while apparently fearing it.

His critics — then and now — point this out.

His response, in one of the letters:

*"I am not a wise man, nor shall I ever be one.
Demand of me not that I be equal to the best,
but that I be better than the wicked.
I am satisfied if I subtract something
from my vices each day."*

Not perfection. Improvement.
Not the ideal Stoic sage — a figure even the Stoics
admitted was theoretical.
A man trying, imperfectly, daily, to do better.

This is what makes him readable across two thousand years.

## VII. The Consolations

Seneca wrote three formal consolations —
letters to people in grief —
that are among the most moving prose
of the ancient world.

To Marcia — a woman who had lost her son.
To Helvia — his own mother, written during his exile.
To Polybius — a freedman at Claudius's court,
who had lost his brother.

The *Ad Helviam* — to his mother — is extraordinary.

He has been exiled. He is in Corsica —
far from Rome, far from his work, far from his friends.
And he writes to his mother
not to receive consolation
but to give it.

*"I have decided, dearest mother,
to conquer my grief rather than to cheat it."*

He does not pretend the exile doesn't hurt.
He does not perform Stoic indifference.
He acknowledges the pain — his pain, her pain —
and then works, carefully, philosophically,
to find the resources within that pain
for endurance.

The resources he finds:
the constancy of virtue, which cannot be exiled.
The life of the mind, which no location can confine.
The friendship of philosophy, which travels everywhere.
The love between mother and son, which distance cannot touch.

*"Wherever I turn, I see the proofs of your affection."*

This is Stoicism with a human face —
not the denial of feeling
but its transformation
through honest philosophical attention.

## VIII. Seneca and Power

The most difficult question about Seneca
is the one his critics have always asked:

How do you square the philosophy with the life?

He wrote about the dangers of wealth — and was fabulously wealthy.
He wrote about the importance of virtue — and served a murderer.
He wrote about the shortness of life — and spent much of it
in the service of Nero's court.

Did he know what Nero was?
Almost certainly.
Did he stay because he believed he could moderate the evil?
Perhaps. For a time.
Did he stay too long? Almost certainly.

The charge of hypocrisy is serious.
Philosophy that cannot be lived is not philosophy —
it is literature.

Seneca's answer, from the letters:

*"Do not look at who says this —
look at what is said."*

The teaching stands independent of the teacher.
The truth of *omnia aliena sunt, tempus tantum nostrum est*
does not depend on whether Seneca
spent his own time well.

This is both a defense and an evasion.
It is also, in a certain limited sense, right.

The letters are true whether Seneca lived them or not.
But they would be truer if he had.
And we should hold both truths simultaneously —
the wisdom of the letters
and the failure of the life —
without letting either cancel the other.

This is what it means to read a philosopher honestly.

## IX. The Death

When the soldiers came, Seneca was ready.

Or as ready as a man can be
who has spent decades preparing
and still, in the end, is a body that bleeds slowly.

He tried to comfort his wife, Paulina,
who wanted to die with him.
He asked her to live.

*"I showed you ways of looking after life;
you prefer the glory of dying.
I will not begrudge you such a noble example.
Let the fortitude of this our common end be alike —
but let there be more in your departure that is worthy of praise."*

She survived. Nero ordered it.
She lived many years after.
The scars on her wrists marked her for the rest of her life.

Seneca dictated his last words.
We do not know exactly what they were —
Tacitus says they were spoken to friends and scribes —
but he died speaking.

Still thinking.
Still in the letters.

---

## X. What He Gives Us

Seneca gives us something that most philosophers do not:

The permission to be imperfect
while still trying.

Not the Platonic philosopher-king who has seen the sun.
Not the Zen master who has achieved emptiness.
Not the perfect Stoic sage who is indifferent to everything external.

A man. Brilliant, flawed, compromised, trying.
Writing letters to a friend
about how to live better.

*"Nusquam est qui ubique est."*

*"One who is everywhere is nowhere."*

*"Recede in te ipse quantum potes."*

*"Retire into yourself as much as you can."*

*"Ita fac, mi Lucili: vindica te tibi."*

*"Claim yourself for yourself."*

Two thousand years old.
Written this morning.
Written for you.
