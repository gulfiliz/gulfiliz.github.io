Immanuel Kant never traveled more than ten miles from his birthplace.

He was born in Königsberg, Prussia, in 1724.
He studied in Königsberg, taught in Königsberg,
wrote in Königsberg, and died in Königsberg in 1804.

He never married. He had no children.
His daily routine was so precise
that the citizens of Königsberg reportedly set their clocks
by his afternoon walk — every day, at exactly 3:30 PM,
Kant would leave his house and walk the same route.

There is a story — probably apocryphal, but too good not to tell —
that Kant missed his walk on only two occasions in his adult life.

The first: the day he received Rousseau's *Émile.*
He was so absorbed he forgot to walk.

The second: the day news arrived of the French Revolution.
He was so excited he forgot to walk.

Rousseau and revolution — the two things that could stop Kant's clock.

This tells you almost everything you need to know
about Immanuel Kant.

---

## I. The Man

Let us dwell for a moment on Kant the person,
because he is one of the most entertaining figures
in the history of philosophy —
not because he was dramatic or scandalous,
but because he was so magnificently, catastrophically boring
in his personal life
and so completely, utterly revolutionary in his thought.

He was small — around five feet tall.
He had a slightly deformed chest, which he compensated for
by an extraordinarily rigid posture and precise dress.
He was obsessed with his health,
developing elaborate theories about digestion, breathing, and sleep
that he applied to himself with scientific rigor.

He had a theory that you should breathe only through your nose —
mouth breathing was dangerous —
and he reportedly would cross the street
to avoid walking behind someone
who might breathe on him.

He dined with friends every day — always exactly at noon,
always for exactly four hours —
but had a rule that the number of guests
should be at least as many as the Graces (three)
and no more than the Muses (nine).

He was a brilliant conversationalist on every topic except philosophy.
At dinner, he refused to discuss philosophy.
He talked about science, politics, literature, travel —
he was particularly interested in geography and travel writing,
having never traveled anywhere himself.

His lectures were famous — witty, accessible, filled with examples.
Students traveled from across Europe to attend them.
His published prose was the opposite:
dense, technical, relentlessly systematic,
requiring multiple readings to yield meaning.

He was aware of this.
He reportedly said that his *Critique of Pure Reason* —
the most important work of philosophy written in the last 300 years —
was unreadable.

He was right. It is.

Let us read it anyway.

## II. The Problem Kant Was Trying to Solve

To understand what Kant did,
you need to understand what was broken before him.

By the mid-18th century, European philosophy
had split into two camps that could not talk to each other.

**The Rationalists** — Descartes, Spinoza, Leibniz —
argued that the most important truths
could be known through reason alone,
independent of experience.
Mathematics was the model:
2 + 2 = 4 is true regardless of what you observe.
The rationalists believed that God, the soul, causation —
the deepest features of reality —
could be demonstrated through pure reasoning.

**The Empiricists** — Locke, Berkeley, Hume —
argued that all knowledge comes from experience.
The mind at birth is a blank slate — *tabula rasa*.
Everything we know, we learned from our senses.
There are no innate ideas.

Hume took empiricism to its logical conclusion,
and the conclusion was devastating.

If all knowledge comes from experience —
then **causation** cannot be known.

You observe the billiard ball strike another and the other move.
You observe this repeatedly.
But you never observe the *necessity* of the connection —
the fact that the first ball *must* cause the second to move.
You only observe constant conjunction.
The idea of necessary causal connection
is not in the data — it is something the mind *adds.*

And if causation is just a habit of the mind —
then science, which is built on causal laws,
has no rational foundation.

Hume, by his own account, was left in skeptical despair.

Kant read Hume and — famously —
said that Hume "awoke him from his dogmatic slumber."

The *Critique of Pure Reason* (1781) is his response.

It took him eleven years to think about it.
He wrote it in four to five months.
The haste shows. It is one of the most disorganized
great books ever written.
It is also one of the most important.

## III. The Copernican Revolution in Philosophy

Kant's move was breathtaking in its audacity.

The traditional assumption: the mind conforms to objects.
We perceive reality as it is because our minds
accurately represent the world.
Knowledge is the mind's attempt to match reality.

Kant's reversal: **objects conform to the mind.**

Not because the mind creates reality —
Kant was not an idealist in that sense.
But because the mind imposes a structure on experience
through which all objects must be filtered
before they can appear to us at all.

He called this the **Copernican Revolution in philosophy.**

Copernicus stopped asking why the stars move across the sky
and instead asked: what if the observer is moving?
The question is the same; the perspective is reversed.

Kant stopped asking how the mind comes to know reality
and instead asked: what structures must the mind possess
for experience to be possible at all?

The answer: **space, time, and the categories of the understanding.**

## IV. Space and Time Are Not Out There

This is where Kant gets genuinely strange.

Space and time, for Kant, are not features of reality
as it is in itself.
They are the **forms of intuition** —
the structures through which the mind organizes experience.

We do not perceive objects in space and time
because space and time are properties of objects.
We perceive objects in space and time
because space and time are the **lens** through which
the mind processes all sensory input.

You cannot experience anything that is not spatial and temporal
because your mind cannot process anything
that has not been run through the spatial-temporal filter.

This means: you will never know what reality is like
independent of the structures your mind imposes on it.

The **thing-in-itself** — *das Ding an sich* —
is forever inaccessible.
You only ever know the **phenomenon** —
reality as it appears through the structures of your cognition.

This is not skepticism — Kant is not saying we know nothing.
He is saying: we know the phenomenal world with certainty,
including scientific knowledge.
But the ultimate nature of reality — noumenal reality —
is beyond our grasp.

Kant's position: we have reliable knowledge of appearances.
We have no knowledge of things as they are in themselves.

The immediate implication: all the traditional metaphysics —
proofs of God's existence, the immortality of the soul,
the freedom of the will —
go beyond possible experience
and therefore cannot be established by theoretical reason.

Kant spent a considerable portion of the *Critique*
systematically dismantling every metaphysical argument
for God, soul, and freedom.

Then, in the *Critique of Practical Reason*,
he argued that morality requires us to *postulate* them anyway.

He is the most systematic philosopher in history.
He is also, in a very specific sense, a troll.

## V. Synthetic A Priori Judgments

Kant introduced a distinction
that has been fought over ever since.

**Analytic judgments:** true by definition.
"All bachelors are unmarried."
The predicate is contained in the subject.
These are necessarily true but tell us nothing new.

**Synthetic judgments:** tell us something new.
"The cat is on the mat."
The predicate is not contained in the subject.
These are informative but contingent.

**A priori:** known independently of experience.
**A posteriori:** known through experience.

Hume assumed: analytic = a priori, synthetic = a posteriori.
You either know by definition or by observation.
Those are the only options.

Kant's bombshell: there is a third category.
**Synthetic a priori judgments:**
necessarily true, known independently of experience,
AND genuinely informative about the world.

His examples:
- *"7 + 5 = 12"* — not true by definition (you must actually add),
  but necessarily true and known a priori.
- *"Every event has a cause"* — not true by definition,
  but necessarily true and not derived from experience.
- Geometry: *"The straight line between two points is the shortest"* —
  not contained in the concept of a straight line,
  but necessarily and a priori true.

These are possible, Kant argues, because
they describe not reality as it is in itself
but the **structure the mind imposes** on experience.
We know that every event has a cause a priori
because causation is one of the **categories of the understanding** —
a structural feature of how the mind processes experience.

Without the category of causation,
we couldn't have coherent experience at all.

This is elegant. It is also, as Hume's defenders point out,
somewhat circular.
We know causation applies to experience because causation
is part of what makes experience possible.
But why should we trust that the categories of our cognition
track anything real?

Kant's answer: because they are the conditions of possible experience.
If they weren't reliable for the phenomenal world,
we wouldn't have coherent experience to philosophize about.

## VI. The Categorical Imperative

Kant's ethics is, by his own standards,
derived entirely from reason.

He was contemptuous of moral theories grounded in
feeling, sympathy, consequences, or happiness.
Not because these things don't matter —
but because they cannot ground *obligation.*

I might feel sympathy for you today and not tomorrow.
My feelings are unreliable.
If morality is grounded in feelings,
morality is unreliable.
But moral obligation is *necessarily* binding — it does not vary.
Therefore, morality must be grounded in reason.

The **Categorical Imperative** — his supreme principle of morality —
has several formulations.
The most famous:

**Formula of Universal Law:**

*"Act only according to that maxim whereby
you can at the same time will
that it should become a universal law."*

In other words: before acting, ask —
what if everyone did this?

If you are considering lying to get out of trouble,
ask: what if everyone lied whenever convenient?
The institution of truthful communication would collapse.
Your lie depends on others telling the truth.
You cannot universalize your maxim without contradiction.
Therefore: do not lie.

This sounds like the Golden Rule.
It is not quite the same.
The Golden Rule is about reciprocity — treat others as you wish to be treated.
The Categorical Imperative is about universalizability —
could the principle of your action be a universal law?

**Formula of Humanity:**

*"Act so that you treat humanity,
whether in your own person or in that of another,
always as an end and never as a means only."*

This is arguably the most influential single sentence
in the history of ethics.

Never use a person merely as a tool for your purposes.
Every human being has a dignity —
a worth that is not conditional on usefulness —
that must be respected absolutely.

This grounds: the prohibition on slavery,
the prohibition on torture,
the prohibition on using people as experimental subjects without consent,
the prohibition on treating people as statistics.

The **Nuremberg Code**, the **Declaration of Helsinki**,
the entire framework of research ethics —
these are Kantian in their structure,
even when their authors didn't know it.

## VII. The Autonomy of the Will

Kant's deepest ethical insight
is also his most demanding.

Moral worth, for Kant, comes not from consequences,
not from following God's commands,
not from social convention —
but from **autonomy**: giving the law to yourself through reason.

*Auto-nomos* — self-legislating.

A person who acts morally because they fear punishment
is not acting morally — they are acting prudently.
A person who acts morally because they feel sympathy
is not acting morally — they are acting emotionally.
A person who acts morally because their culture told them to
is not acting morally — they are acting conventionally.

Moral action requires: acting from the recognition
that reason demands this — and choosing it for that reason alone.

This is extraordinarily demanding.
It implies that most of what we call moral behavior —
driven by habit, feeling, social pressure, self-interest —
lacks genuine moral worth.

It also implies something Kant found important:
the **dignity of moral agency.**

Because you are capable of autonomous moral action —
because you can give the law to yourself through reason —
you have a dignity that is absolutely unconditional.
Not because you are useful. Not because you are loved.
Because you are a rational agent, capable of autonomy.

This is the philosophical foundation of human rights.

## VIII. The Things Kant Got Wrong

In the spirit of honest philosophical engagement:

**The universalizability test is underspecified.**

Almost any maxim can be universalized with the right description.
"Lie to murderers asking where your friends are hiding" —
can this be universalized?
Kant said no. Most people disagree.

The problem: the test depends heavily on how you describe the maxim.
Kant's framework does not tell you how to describe it.

**The absolute prohibition on lying.**

Kant famously argued that lying is always wrong —
even to a murderer asking where your friend is hiding.

This conclusion is so obviously wrong
that generations of philosophers have proposed rescues —
finding ways to interpret Kant that avoid it.
Kant himself refused rescue. He meant it.

The friend who knocks on your door and asks you to hide them —
you lie to the murderer —
and Kant says you have acted wrongly.

This is a case where the theory has clearly run ahead of the morality.

**The treatment of women, non-Europeans, and animals.**

Kant's ethics grants full moral status only to rational agents.
He defined rationality in ways that excluded women
from full moral and political standing —
a conclusion he drew explicitly and without embarrassment.

His writings on non-European peoples are worse.
By his own ethical standards — treating persons as ends —
his actual views about non-European peoples
are not merely wrong but incoherent.

The universalist ethics and the parochial prejudices
coexist in his work in a way that should make us
deeply suspicious of philosophers who claim
their personal limitations don't affect their universal theories.

## IX. What Kant Got Permanently Right

Despite everything —

The **critical turn:** the insight that we cannot ask
what reality is like without first asking
what kind of knowers we are.
This is still the right starting point for epistemology.

The **dignity of persons:** the idea that rational agents
have a worth that is not conditional on usefulness or results.
This is the philosophical bedrock of human rights.
Whatever its origins in Kant's specific framework,
the insight survives the framework.

The **autonomy of morality:** the idea that moral reasons
are not reducible to consequences, feelings, or commands —
that morality has its own domain and its own authority.
This remains, despite all the challenges,
a defensible and important position.

The **limits of theoretical reason:** the demonstration
that certain questions — God, soul, freedom —
lie beyond what theoretical reason can resolve.
This clears the ground for genuine intellectual humility
about metaphysics.

And this:

The recognition that we are not passive receivers of reality —
that the mind is active, that it structures experience,
that who we are shapes what we can know.

This is not idealism. It is not relativism.
It is the insistence that the knower is part of the story,
that the question "what is reality like?"
cannot be separated from "what kind of creature is asking?"

We are still living in the aftermath of that insight.

---

## Coda

Kant died in 1804, aged seventy-nine,
in Königsberg.

His last words, reportedly, were:
*"Es ist gut."*

It is good.

The man who had mapped the limits of human reason,
who had grounded ethics in autonomy,
who had argued that beauty required free play of the faculties —
died with a two-word verdict on the whole enterprise.

*It is good.*

He never left Königsberg.
He changed everything anyway.

There is a lesson in that,
about the relationship between movement and thought,
between travel and depth,
between the size of the world you inhabit
and the size of the world you make.

His afternoon walk was enough.
