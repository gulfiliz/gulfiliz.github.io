New York does not have a learning curve.
It just is what it is,
and you figure it out as you go.

I have been several times now.
Each time I understand it a little better.
Each time there is more I haven't seen.

These are notes from those visits.

---

## I. The Met

The Metropolitan Museum of Art is too big
to see in one visit.
Or two.
The correct approach is to pick two or three rooms,
go deep, and leave the rest for next time.

I never manage this.
I try to see everything.
I see nothing properly.
I go back.

**Arms and Armor**

The Arms and Armor gallery is one of the most
spectacular rooms in the museum —
and somehow always a little underrated
in serious art conversation,
maybe because it's too immediately impressive.

*[photo: Met Arms and Armor gallery]*

Knights in full plate armor line the room —
European, Japanese, Ottoman —
standing as if a battle
has been postponed for five hundred years.

What strikes me: the decoration.
The armor is engraved, gilded, etched with patterns.
It was made to protect and to impress simultaneously.
A 16th-century Italian parade helmet
has lion-head cheekpieces and a winged serpent crest.
It was made for a single ceremonial procession.
Then kept. For five hundred years.

*[photo: helmet or armor detail]*

**The Egypt Galleries**

Large collection, serious rooms,
4000-year-old objects treated with appropriate care.

But what stopped me completely
was a wig.

A human-hair wig. Egyptian. About 3000 years old.

*[photo: the wig]*

Still hair. Still arranged. Still recognizably a wig.
The cotton wrappings beside it — also 3000 years old —
still textile, weave still visible.

I stood in front of this case for a long time.

Not because it's aesthetically remarkable.
Because it's a completely ordinary object.
Someone's hair wasn't quite right
and they put on a wig.
Three thousand years ago.
The same problem.

*[photo: cotton wrappings or Egypt gallery detail]*

**Smyrna**

In the Greek and Roman galleries,
there are objects from Smyrna.
İzmir. My city.

*[photo: Smyrna artifacts]*

Ancient Smyrna was one of the great cities
of the ancient world — on the Aegean coast,
claimed by multiple civilizations,
fought over for millennia.

Standing in front of objects
from the ground of my city,
made by people who looked at the same sea —
I don't have a clean word for what that feels like.
Not pride exactly. Not grief exactly.
Something in between.

**Van Gogh**

I walked into the Van Gogh exhibition
without knowing it was there.

*[photo: Van Gogh exhibition]*

Van Gogh in reproduction is everywhere —
postcards, posters, phone cases, mugs.
The reproduction is fine.
The painting is better.

What reproduction cannot show:
the surface.

The paint is thick — built up in layers,
brushstrokes visible as physical ridges.
The surface has topography.

Standing in front of the real thing,
you're looking at the record of a specific movement —
the exact path of the brush,
the exact pressure of a hand,
130 years ago.
Still there.

*[photo: Van Gogh painting or detail]*

## II. MoMA

A different museum, a different argument.

Where the Met is about continuity —
everything connected to everything —
MoMA is about the moment art
decided it didn't have to look like anything anymore.

**Basquiat**

Large paintings. Dense with text, symbol, mark —
layered, crossed-out, rewritten.

*[photo: Basquiat at MoMA]*

The crossed-out words are the key.
Basquiat crosses things out not to erase them
but to keep them visible — struck through but legible.
The things crossed out are exactly
the things that can't be gotten rid of.

It's not comfortable to stand in front of.
It's not supposed to be.

*[photo: Basquiat text detail]*

**Campbell's Soup Cans**

Warhol's thirty-two canvases,
one for each variety of Campbell's condensed soup.

*[photo: Warhol Campbell's Soup]*

The most famous critique of consumerism
that consumerism has ever absorbed,
reproduced, and sold back to itself.
The painting is now on actual soup cans.
I think about Baudrillard every time I see it.

**Van Gogh at MoMA**

MoMA has its own Van Goghs.

*[photo: Van Gogh at MoMA]*

Seeing Van Gogh at both the Met and MoMA —
in two different contexts, two different arguments —
changes the work slightly.

At the Met: end of something.
At MoMA: beginning of something.
Same paintings.

**Refik Anadol**

*[photo: Refik Anadol installation]*

I wasn't prepared for this one.

Anadol's installation uses machine learning
trained on enormous image datasets —
MoMA's collection, photographs of nature,
images from the internet —
to generate visual flows on enormous screens.

The room is immersive.
You don't look at it. You're inside it.

I stayed longer than I planned.

*[photo: Anadol installation detail]*

## III. The New York Public Library

The main branch on Fifth Avenue —
the one with the lions out front.

*[photo: NYPL exterior with lions]*

The reading rooms have high ceilings,
long tables, green-shaded lamps,
and a specific quality of quiet
that makes you want to sit down and read something.

*[photo: NYPL reading room]*

But the real reason to go:
the **Treasures** exhibition.

Permanent collection of rare documents and objects.
I saw the first draft of the Bill of Rights —
actual parchment, actual ink,
the handwriting of someone
who was writing the first draft of the Bill of Rights
and didn't know yet it would become
the Bill of Rights.

*[photo: Bill of Rights document]*

And Victor Hugo's writing desk.

*[photo: Victor Hugo's desk]*

The desk where he wrote.
The actual desk.
I stood in front of it for a while
thinking about Les Misérables
and whether it matters
that the desk is real.

I think it does matter.
I'm not sure why exactly.

## IV. Bryant Park

The best park in New York
for what it is —
small, surrounded by Midtown,
not trying to be Central Park.

*[photo: Bryant Park]*

I bought a lobster roll from Burger and Lobster —
directly across the street —
and took it into the park.

*[photo: lobster roll]*

Brioche bun, cold lobster, light dressing.
Simple and correct.

I lay on the grass, put in earphones,
looked at the buildings above me
and the sky between them.
Read for a while.
Didn't think about anything in particular.

Bryant Park allows this.
Not every park does.

*[photo: Bryant Park from the grass, or just the park]*

## V. Chelsea Market — Filaga Pizza

Roman-style pizza — pizza al taglio —
sold by the slice from large rectangular trays.

*[photo: Filaga pizza]*

Roman pizza is thicker than Neapolitan,
crispier on the bottom,
sold by weight or by the cut.

The potato and rosemary slice:
simple, correct,
the kind of thing that makes you
want to sit down even when there's nowhere to sit.

I ate two slices standing up
and then walked the High Line.

*[photo: pizza slice detail]*

This is the correct sequence.

## VI. A Few Notes

New York is the kind of city
where you can walk into a museum
and find a 3000-year-old wig from Egypt
and objects from your hometown
and Van Gogh's actual brushstrokes
and Basquiat's crossed-out words
and Victor Hugo's desk
all in the same afternoon.

It doesn't organize this for you.
You just walk around until you find things.

That's the city.
