I have a problem.

When a conversation stays on the surface —
when it skims, when it performs,
when it uses the vocabulary of depth
without the substance —

something in me closes.

Not rudely. Not visibly, usually.
But something withdraws.

I become polite instead of present.
I become courteous instead of curious.
I go through the motions of engagement
while something essential has already left the room.

I am intolerant of superficiality.

I have been told this is a flaw.
I have been told it makes me difficult,
that I expect too much,
that not everyone thinks the way I do,
that I should meet people where they are.

All of this is true.
And I have tried.

But I want to make a different argument today —
not against the criticism,
but alongside it.

I want to argue that the intolerance is also right.
That it is, in certain respects, a form of respect.
That the refusal to pretend
is its own kind of honesty.

And that living in the between —
between the demand for depth
and the reality of a world
that often does not provide it —
is not a pathology.

It is a condition.

---

## I. What Superficiality Is — Precisely

I want to be careful here,
because the accusation of superficiality
is often itself superficial —
used to dismiss people who are different,
or less educated, or less verbal,
or simply less interested in the same things.

That is not what I mean.

A farmer who knows the land
with a knowledge that has no words —
who reads weather and soil and season
with decades of embedded understanding —
is not superficial.

A carpenter who understands wood
the way a philosopher understands argument —
who feels the grain, anticipates the split,
knows what the material will and will not do —
is not superficial.

A grandmother who has lived through loss
and learned, without theorizing it,
what matters and what does not —
is not superficial.

Superficiality is not the absence of vocabulary.
It is not the absence of credentials.
It is not the absence of cultural consumption.

Superficiality is the **refusal of depth** —
the active choice to remain
on the surface of things,
to be satisfied with appearances,
to perform engagement without risking encounter.

The person who reads all the right books
and uses all the right words
and never once lets anything they read
disturb their assumptions —

that is superficiality.

The person who discusses philosophy
as a social credential
rather than as a live question —

that is superficiality.

The person who has opinions on everything
and has risked nothing on any of them —

that is superficiality.

It is the armor, not the absence of the armor.
It is the performance of knowing
that protects against the vulnerability of not-knowing.

---

## II. The Philosophical Pedigree

The intolerance for superficiality
has a long philosophical history.

It begins — where else — with Socrates.

Socrates' entire method —
the elenchus, the cross-examination,
the relentless questioning —
was an intolerance for superficiality made systematic.

He would not accept the first answer.
He would not accept the comfortable answer.
He would not let the conversation stay where it was.

He pushed — always — to the place
where the interlocutor had to acknowledge:
I do not actually know what I claimed to know.

This made him deeply unpopular.

The people he questioned
felt that he was making them look stupid,
that he was being aggressive,
that he was violating the social norms
of polite Athenian conversation.

He was executed for it.

But Socrates' intolerance for superficiality
was not contempt for people.
It was the opposite.

He believed that the unexamined life
was not worth living —
not as a judgment against others,
but as a statement about what life is for.

To live without examination
is to miss what life is.
Not to fail at life — but to not quite be living it.

The intolerance was a form of love —
a refusal to let people stay
in the comfortable unexamination
that Socrates believed was a kind of sleep.

---

## III. Pascal's Diversion, Again

We have met Pascal before.

His observation: humans cannot bear
to sit quietly in a room alone.
They seek diversion — *divertissement* —
to escape the confrontation with themselves.

Superficiality is institutionalized diversion.

The small talk, the social performance,
the careful avoidance of anything
that might require genuine response —
all of this serves the same function
as Pascal's hunting and gaming:

To keep the self from encountering itself.
To fill the silence before it can speak.
To be occupied so as not to have to be present.

The intolerant-of-superficiality person
is the one who notices the diversion —
and cannot pretend not to notice.

This is uncomfortable for everyone.

The person performing the diversion
feels seen — not flattered by this.
The intolerant person
cannot relax into the comfortable pretense.

Both parties are left slightly uneasy.

Pascal would say:
the intolerant one is closer to the truth.
The uneasy one is alive to what the comfortable one
has decided not to feel.

## IV. The Social Cost

Let me be honest about the cost.

**Loneliness.**

The intolerance for superficiality
narrows the field considerably.

Not everyone wants depth.
Not everyone has the energy for it.
Not everyone was trained for it.
Not everyone believes it is worth it.

These are all legitimate positions.

But they mean:
the person who cannot comfortably inhabit the surface
will often be alone in rooms full of people.

Not physically alone.
Conversationally alone.
Psychologically alone.

Present but not encountered.
Talking but not speaking.
Connected by proximity
but separated by depth.

This is a specific kind of loneliness —
more disorienting than ordinary solitude,
because it is solitude in company.

**Being perceived as arrogant.**

The intolerance for superficiality
is often read as contempt.

Sometimes it is contempt — I won't pretend otherwise.
The contempt for the performance of depth
without the substance.
The contempt for the name-drop
that has not been earned.
The contempt for the confident wrongness
that will not question itself.

But often it is not contempt.
It is a different kind of attention —
a listening that is waiting for something real,
that becomes visibly disappointed
when it does not arrive.

This disappointment looks like judgment.
It is felt as judgment.
It is not quite the same thing.

**The missed connections.**

The most concrete cost:
the people who would have been good
if given more time —
who had depth that needed drawing out —
who were performing superficiality
because the social situation demanded it —
but who, in different circumstances,
would have met you where you are.

The intolerance can close that door
before it has a chance to open.

This is a real failure.

---

## V. The Defense — Why It Is Also Right

Against all of this —
and I hold all of it honestly —
I want to make the defense.

**Honesty requires it.**

There is a kind of social performance
that involves pretending to be interested
when you are not.
Pretending to find something meaningful
when you don't.
Performing engagement
out of politeness or social obligation.

I understand why people do this.
I understand it is often kind.

But it is also a lie.
A small, socially lubricating lie —
but a lie.

The intolerance for superficiality
is, at its best, a refusal of this lie.

A refusal to perform interest I don't feel.
A refusal to pretend that the conversation
is going somewhere it is not going.
A refusal to let the social script
substitute for actual encounter.

This is a form of respect —
the respect that says:
I will not perform for you.
I will be actually present or not present at all.

**It protects something important.**

Every hour spent in deep conversation
is an hour not spent in shallow performance.

This is not a statement about other people —
it is a statement about limited time and attention.

The intolerance for superficiality
is, in part, a conservation strategy.

The person who can sit comfortably
in surface-level conversation for hours
has hours to give to it.

The person who cannot
is protecting something —
the capacity for depth,
the readiness for genuine encounter,
the attention that genuine encounter requires.

This is not superiority.
It is different allocation.

**It is a form of fidelity.**

To what?

To the conviction that things matter.
That ideas have weight.
That words should mean something.
That conversations can change you.
That the person in front of you
is capable of more than small talk —
and deserves to be met at that capacity,
not at the comfortable minimum.

The intolerance for superficiality
is fidelity to this conviction.

The refusal to accept less
is, simultaneously,
the refusal to offer less.

## VI. The Danger — When Intolerance Becomes Contempt

I need to name the failure mode.

The intolerance for superficiality
can become:

**Elitism** —
the belief that depth is available only to some,
that the inability to articulate depth
is evidence of its absence,
that the vocabulary of profundity
is the thing itself.

This is wrong.
And it is a form of superficiality —
the superficiality of confusing
the map for the territory,
the language for the thought,
the performance of depth for depth itself.

**Impatience** —
the refusal to wait for depth to emerge,
the closing of the door
before the room has had a chance to reveal itself.

Depth is not always immediately available.
People are not always performing at their best.
Anxiety, social pressure, unfamiliar territory —
all of these suppress depth
in people who are capable of it.

The intolerant person who cannot wait
misses the depth that was there
but needed more time.

**Loneliness mistaken for standards** —
the use of "I just can't tolerate superficiality"
as a way of not examining
why connection is difficult,
why intimacy is threatening,
why the surface might be preferred
not for its comfort
but for its safety.

This is the hardest one to look at.

Sometimes the intolerance for superficiality
is a way of ensuring
that no one gets close enough
to see that the depth we demand of others
is not always present in ourselves.

The person who cannot be met
is also the person who cannot be known.

---

## VII. The Between

I called this **filizinbetween.**

Between is the right word.

Between the demand for depth
and the reality that most social life
operates at the surface.

Between the loneliness of never quite fitting
and the occasional, extraordinary encounter
with someone who meets you
exactly where you are.

Between the failure mode of contempt
and the virtue of honest attention.

Between the exhaustion of maintaining the demand
and the impossibility of abandoning it.

This between is not a problem to be solved.
It is a condition to be inhabited.

The person who resolves it
by lowering the demand
has lost something.

The person who resolves it
by withdrawing entirely
has lost something else.

The person who lives in the between —
who maintains the demand
while holding it lightly enough
to be surprised by depth
in unexpected places —

that person is doing the harder thing.

And the harder thing,
in this case as in most,
is the right thing.

---

## VIII. What Depth Actually Requires

I want to end with something concrete —
because "depth" without content
is itself a kind of superficiality.

What does depth actually look like?

Not: using difficult vocabulary.
Not: having read the right books.
Not: holding the correct opinions.
Not: being able to perform profundity.

**Depth is the willingness to be changed
by what you encounter.**

The book that rewrites how you think.
The argument that you cannot answer
and cannot dismiss.
The person whose life experience
makes your assumptions about the world
suddenly inadequate.

Depth is the capacity for genuine encounter —
with ideas, with people, with the world —
that leaves you different from how you arrived.

The superficial person
encounters everything and is changed by nothing.
Everything confirms what they already knew.
Every conversation ends where it began.

The deep person
encounters things and is changed by them —
sometimes slightly, sometimes profoundly,
but always actually.

This is the thing I cannot tolerate the absence of.

Not performance.
Not vocabulary.
Not even intelligence, exactly.

The willingness to be changed.
The openness to encounter.
The refusal to close.

---

## IX. The Gift of the Intolerance

Here is what the intolerance gives:

**When you find depth — and you do find it —
the encounter is extraordinary.**

The conversation that goes somewhere unexpected.
The person who says something that makes you stop.
The idea that reorganizes everything around it.
The moment when two people
are actually present to each other —
not performing, not managing,
but genuinely there.

These moments are rare.
The intolerance makes them rarer —
by raising the standard,
by refusing the consolation prize,
by waiting.

But it also makes them more vivid
when they arrive.

The person who can be satisfied with surface
does not experience depth the same way.
They have not been waiting for it.
They have not been refusing the substitute.

The intolerant person —
who has sat through so many performances,
who has been so often almost-present
but not quite —

when they finally encounter the real thing,
they recognize it immediately.

And it matters in a way
it cannot matter to someone
for whom it was never scarce.

---

## Coda

This is a confession,
not a prescription.

I am not recommending the intolerance.
I am not claiming it is optimal.
I am not saying you should acquire it
if you don't have it.

I am saying:
if you have it —
if you recognize this —
if you have sat in rooms full of people
and felt that specific loneliness
of being present but not encountered —

you are not broken.

You are not arrogant —
or not only arrogant.
You are not antisocial —
or not only antisocial.

You are someone for whom
depth is not optional.

For whom the surface is not enough —
not as a preference,
but as a constitutional fact.

This makes certain things harder.

It also makes certain things —
the right things —
more real.

The demand is the cost.
The demand is also the gift.

Live in the between.

It is the only honest place.
