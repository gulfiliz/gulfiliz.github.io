When did you last do nothing?

Not meditation — that is doing something.
Not sleep — that is necessary function.
Not "relaxing" with a podcast or a show.

Nothing.

Sitting somewhere.
No phone. No book. No music.
Just — time passing.
Your thoughts going where they go.
Boredom arriving and not being fled from.

I am asking seriously.

Because I think most people reading this —
myself included, on most days —
cannot remember.

And I think this is one of the most important
things we have lost.

---

## I. The Abolition of Boredom

Boredom is being engineered out of existence.

Not accidentally. Deliberately.

Every major technology platform —
every app, every feed, every notification system —
is designed with one explicit goal:
the elimination of empty time.

The infinite scroll was designed so that
the feed never ends —
so that the moment you reach the bottom
and boredom threatens,
new content appears.

The autoplay feature on streaming services
was designed so that the gap between episodes —
the moment of decision, the moment of pause —
is eliminated.

The notification system was designed
to interrupt you at precisely the moment
when your attention might drift —
when boredom might set in —
and recapture it.

These are not side effects.
They are features.

The business model of attention economies
depends on the total colonization of empty time.

Every moment you are bored
is a moment you are not clicking,
not watching, not engaging —
not generating the data and the attention
that these platforms monetize.

Boredom is the enemy of the attention economy.

Therefore boredom must be destroyed.

---

## II. What Boredom Actually Is

Before we can defend boredom,
we need to understand it.

Boredom is not emptiness.
It is not the absence of experience.

Boredom is the experience of
the gap between the mind's desire for stimulation
and the environment's failure to provide it.

It is restlessness. Dissatisfaction. Itching.
The mind looking for something to do
and finding nothing adequate.

This sounds unpleasant.
It is.

And this is exactly why it is valuable.

The discomfort of boredom is the mind's signal
that it needs to generate its own content —
that it cannot simply receive,
but must produce.

When you are bored —
genuinely, uncomfortably bored —
and you do not reach for your phone,
something happens.

The mind begins to move on its own.

It returns to problems it had set aside.
It makes connections it had not made.
It generates images, ideas, narratives —
not in response to external prompts
but from its own internal resources.

This is not mystical.
It is neurological.

The **default mode network** —
the brain regions active when we are not focused
on external tasks —
is associated with:
- Self-referential thought
- Perspective-taking
- Creative ideation
- Future planning
- The integration of experience into narrative

The default mode network is most active
when we are doing nothing in particular.
When we are bored.

When we eliminate boredom —
when we fill every empty moment with input —
we suppress the default mode network.

We stop integrating.
We stop imagining.
We stop making meaning.

We become consumers of meaning
rather than producers of it.

## III. The History of Boredom

Boredom is surprisingly recent as a named concept.

The word "boredom" in English
appears first in the 1850s —
associated with the rapid urbanization
and standardization of industrial life.

Before industrialization, most human lives
were structured around seasonal rhythms,
communal practices, religious observance.
There were moments of intense labor
and moments of enforced rest.

The rest was not experienced as boredom
in the modern sense —
it was structured emptiness,
filled by conversation, by story, by craft,
by the slow attention of rural life.

Industrial time — clock time, factory time —
created a new experience:
time that was neither labor nor genuine rest,
time that had to be "filled,"
time whose emptiness felt threatening.

The Victorians were obsessed with idleness.
"The devil makes work for idle hands" —
the terror of unstructured time,
the association of emptiness with moral danger.

This terror has not gone away.
It has been monetized.

The attention economy did not create
our discomfort with boredom.
It inherited and exploited a discomfort
that industrialization had already produced.

But it intensified it beyond anything
the Victorians could have imagined.

## IV. Pascal's Pensée

Blaise Pascal, in the 17th century,
identified something that he called
the fundamental condition of human unhappiness:

*"All of humanity's problems stem from man's inability
to sit quietly in a room alone."*

He meant: we cannot bear to be with ourselves.

We flee from our own interiority —
into distraction, into company,
into the endless business of activity —
because what we find when we stop
is something we cannot face.

What do we find?

According to Pascal: our mortality.
The awareness of death, of meaninglessness,
of the fragility of everything we hold.

The diversion (*divertissement*) — hunting, gaming,
conversation, spectacle —
is not entertainment in the innocent sense.
It is flight.

We are afraid to stop because stopping
means confronting what is always there
beneath the noise:

The question of who we are
when we are not performing,
not consuming,
not producing.

The silence in which that question echoes.

Pascal thought the answer to this question was God.

You do not have to agree
to recognize the accuracy of the diagnosis.

We cannot sit quietly in a room alone.

Not because there is nothing there —
but because there is too much.

## V. Baudrillard and the Hyperreal Cure for Boredom

Here is where it gets interesting.

Baudrillard — whom we have been reading —
described the hyperreal as a world
in which simulation has replaced reality.

One of the functions of the hyperreal
is precisely the management of boredom.

The simulation is always more exciting than the real.
The television drama is more dramatic than life.
The social media feed is more interesting than the room you're in.
The video game is more stimulating than unstructured play.

The hyperreal cures boredom
by substituting for reality
a reality that is more real than real —
more vivid, more narrative, more perfectly calibrated
to the dopaminergic systems of the human brain.

This cure is addictive.

Every time you reach for your phone
when silence threatens —
you are accepting the hyperreal cure.

You are training yourself to need it.

And every time you accept the cure,
you become a little less able to sit with the emptiness —
a little more dependent on simulation,
a little less capable of generating your own content.

The attention economy does not just sell you distraction.
It atrophies your capacity for non-distraction.

## VI. The Neuroscience

The research on smartphones and attention
is becoming difficult to ignore.

**Attention restoration theory (Kaplan, 1995):**
The human attentional system has two modes.
Directed attention — focused, effortful —
is a limited resource that depletes.
Involuntary attention — the diffuse awareness
that arises in natural, unstructured environments —
restores the directed attention system.

Boredom, in natural environments,
transitions the mind from directed to involuntary attention.
This is restorative.

Boredom medicated by smartphones
does not trigger this transition.
Instead, it demands continued directed attention —
just on a lower-stakes stimulus.
The restoration does not occur.

**Mind-wandering and creativity (Smallwood & Schooler, 2015):**
Mind-wandering — the spontaneous, undirected thought
that occurs during boredom —
is associated with higher scores
on measures of creative problem-solving.

The mind that wanders makes unexpected connections.
The mind that is always directed toward external input
cannot wander.

**The smartphone and cognitive capacity (Ward et al., 2017):**
Participants performed cognitive tasks
with their smartphones either on the desk (face-down),
in their pocket, or in another room.
Performance was significantly better
when the phone was in another room —
even when it was face-down and silent.

The mere presence of the smartphone —
the knowledge that it is available —
consumes cognitive capacity.

It does not need to ring.
It does not need to be touched.
Its presence is enough
to partially colonize the mind.

## VII. What We Make in the Empty Spaces

Let me be concrete about what boredom produces.

**Newton:**
He retreated to his mother's farm in Woolsthorpe
during the plague of 1665-66,
when Cambridge was closed.

In those eighteen months of enforced quiet —
without colleagues, without students,
without the machinery of institutional life —
he developed calculus,
formulated the law of universal gravitation,
and laid the foundations of optics.

The most productive eighteen months
in the history of science.

Achieved in boredom.
In isolation.
In the empty time that plague enforced.

**Darwin:**
His method, by his own account,
was long solitary walks —
the "thinking path" at Down House —
in which he did nothing but walk and think.

Not reading. Not writing. Walking.
The problems turning over in his mind
without directed effort.

He called this his most productive time.

**Proust:**
*In Search of Lost Time* —
seven volumes, the longest novel
in the Western tradition —
was written by a man who spent years
in a cork-lined room,
largely in bed,
with almost no external stimulation.

The involuntary memory —
the madeleine dipped in tea,
the cobblestones of the courtyard —
that structures the entire novel
is a theory of what the unstimulated mind does:

It goes back. It recovers.
It finds, in the empty moment,
the fullness of what has been lived.

This recovery requires silence.
It requires the absence of input.
It requires, precisely, boredom.

## VIII. The Political Dimension

There is a political argument for boredom
that is rarely made.

The attention economy is not politically neutral.

When your attention is colonized —
when every empty moment is filled
with content that has been optimized
for engagement, for outrage, for tribal signal —
you are not a free citizen.

You are a node in a system
whose outputs benefit those
who own the platforms and the algorithms.

Your attention is the product.
Your opinions — shaped by what you are shown —
are the output.

The capacity for independent political thought
requires the capacity to be alone with your thoughts —
to consider, to doubt, to reconsider,
to arrive at positions through slow deliberation
rather than through the rapid emotional reactions
that algorithmic feeds are designed to produce.

Hannah Arendt wrote about **thinking** —
the two-in-one dialogue of the self with itself —
as the foundation of moral and political agency.

*"The sad truth is that most evil is done
by people who never make up their minds
to be good or evil."*

The banality of evil — her most famous concept —
is rooted in thoughtlessness.
Not stupidity. Thoughtlessness.

The failure to stop.
The failure to sit with the question.
The failure to be, for a moment, alone
with the implications of what one is doing.

The attention economy produces thoughtlessness
at industrial scale.

It is not neutral.

## IX. The Practice

I am not suggesting you throw away your phone.

I am suggesting something smaller
and therefore harder:

**Boredom practice.**

Not meditation — though that is related.
Not "digital detox" — though that is related.

Simply: scheduled encounters with emptiness.

Sit somewhere — anywhere.
No phone. No book. No music.
Ten minutes. Twenty. However long you can bear.

When the discomfort comes — and it will come —
do not flee it.

Sit with it.

Notice what the mind does
when it has nothing to do.

Notice what thoughts arise
without invitation.

Notice what problems you have been carrying
that you have not had space to feel.

Notice what boredom actually is —
not the unbearable thing you imagined,
but a space.

A space in which something can happen
that cannot happen anywhere else.

## X. The Radical Act

In a world designed for total stimulation —
in the hyperreal condition Baudrillard described —
doing nothing is a political act.

Not a revolutionary act.
Not a solution to structural problems.

But a refusal.

A refusal to be a node in the attention network.
A refusal to have your empty time colonized.
A refusal to let your capacity for solitude,
for daydream, for the slow movement of thought —
be atrophied by a system that profits from its absence.

The Stoics had a word: **ἀταραξία** — ataraxia.
Undisturbedness. The tranquil mind.

Not the absence of thought.
The presence of the self — gathered, quiet, available
to its own deepest resources.

This was their highest ideal.

It required practice.
It required the deliberate encounter with emptiness.
It required, in our language, the willingness to be bored.

*"Retire into yourself as much as you can."*
— Seneca

*"The faculty of voluntarily bringing back
a wandering attention, over and over again,
is the very root of judgment, character, and will."*
— William James

*"It is not the man who has too little
who is poor, but the man who hankers after more."*
— Seneca

Do nothing.

Let the mind wander.

Let boredom arrive and stay.

See what it brings.
