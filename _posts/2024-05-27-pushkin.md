Every Russian writer who came after him
owes him a debt that cannot be repaid.

Dostoevsky said: *"We all came from under Pushkin's overcoat."*

Wait — that was Gogol's overcoat.

But the sentiment applies equally to Pushkin.
Perhaps more so.

Because Pushkin did not just write great literature.
He created the language in which Russian literature
would be written.

Before Pushkin, Russian literary language was
a chaotic mixture — Church Slavonic, French borrowings,
bureaucratic formulas, folk speech —
with no agreed standard, no unified voice.

After Pushkin, there was Russian.

Not invented, exactly. Crystallized.
The way a supersaturated solution,
touched at one point,
suddenly becomes crystal throughout.

Pushkin was the touch.

## I. The Life

Alexander Sergeyevich Pushkin was born in Moscow
on June 6, 1799.

His ancestry was complicated and he was proud of it:
on his mother's side, he descended from
**Abram Petrovich Gannibal** —
an African prince brought to Russia as a child,
who became a general and favorite of Peter the Great.

Pushkin wrote about Gannibal.
He was not embarrassed by his African ancestry.
In an era of rigid Russian aristocratic identity,
this itself was a kind of provocation.

He was educated at the **Tsarskoye Selo Lyceum** —
a new institution, founded by Alexander I,
intended to produce the new Russian intelligentsia.

He was a difficult student.
He was also, by eighteen, 
already recognized as the greatest poet in Russia.

His first major poem — *Ruslan and Lyudmila* (1820) —
was published when he was twenty-one.
It was scandalous and celebrated simultaneously.
Too irreverent. Too playful. Too brilliant.

Then came the exile.

His early poems — *Ode to Liberty*, *The Village* —
contained political sentiments
that the Tsar found unacceptable.
He was exiled from St. Petersburg —
first to the south (Kishinev, Odessa),
then to his family estate Mikhailovskoye,
in the northern countryside.

Six years of exile.

He wrote constantly.

The southern exile produced the **Southern Poems** —
*The Prisoner of the Caucasus*, *The Fountain of Bakhchisaray*,
*The Gypsies* — Romantic, Byronic, brilliant.

Mikhailovskoye produced the bulk of *Eugene Onegin*,
*Boris Godunov*, and some of his greatest lyric poetry.

Exile did not silence him.
It concentrated him.

He was eventually pardoned by Nicholas I —
who became, in effect, his personal censor.
Every major work required the Tsar's approval.

This arrangement was humiliating.
Pushkin accepted it because he had no choice.

He married **Natalya Goncharova** in 1831 —
a woman of extraordinary beauty
and, by most accounts, limited intellectual interests.
Pushkin was besotted.
The marriage produced four children
and, eventually, a duel.

## II. The Duel

On January 27, 1837,
Pushkin fought a duel with
**Georges-Charles de Heeckeren d'Anthès** —
a French officer serving in the Russian imperial guard,
adopted son of the Dutch ambassador.

D'Anthès had been pursuing Natalya Pushkina publicly
for over a year.
Anonymous letters — their authorship never definitively established —
had mocked Pushkin as a cuckold.

Pushkin was consumed by jealousy and rage.
He was also, by most accounts, exhausted —
by debt, by the censorship, by society,
by the grinding humiliation of his position.

He challenged d'Anthès.

He knew he would probably lose.

D'Anthès was a young, experienced military officer
in his prime.
Pushkin was thirty-seven, in poor health,
not a duelist.

D'Anthès fired first.
The bullet entered Pushkin's abdomen.

Pushkin, wounded, steadied himself and fired.
He hit d'Anthès in the arm — a glancing wound.
D'Anthès survived.

Pushkin was carried home.
He died two days later,
on January 29, 1837.

He was thirty-seven years old.

On his deathbed, he said to his books:
*"Farewell, friends."*

Russia went into mourning.
Nicholas I, fearing a public demonstration,
had the funeral moved secretly, at night.

D'Anthès was expelled from Russia.
He returned to France and lived to be ninety-three.

## III. What He Did to the Russian Language

To understand Pushkin's linguistic achievement,
you need to understand the situation he inherited.

18th century Russian literature was written
in a language almost no one spoke —
a formal, Latinized, Church Slavonic-influenced idiom
that was prestigious and dead.

At the same time, educated Russians spoke French
among themselves. Russian was for servants.

There was a war — the **"Shishkov vs. Karamzin" debate** —
between those who wanted to purify Russian
by returning to Church Slavonic roots
and those who wanted to modernize it
along French lines.

Pushkin resolved this debate
by ignoring it.

He wrote in a language that was simultaneously:
- Rooted in living spoken Russian
- Capable of the highest poetic expression
- Free from artificial archaisms
- Absorbing of foreign influence where natural
- Flexible, precise, musical

He did not create a theory of language.
He demonstrated a practice.

When *Eugene Onegin* appeared —
when Russians read it —
they recognized something:

*This is how Russian sounds
when it is fully itself.*

The linguist Roman Jakobson wrote
that Pushkin's verse had achieved something
virtually unique in literary history —
the coincidence of the ideal poetic language
with the living spoken language of its time.

This coincidence was not accidental.
It was the achievement of a lifetime's work.

## IV. Eugene Onegin — The Novel in Verse

*Eugene Onegin* (1823-1831) is Pushkin's masterwork.

It is a novel. It is a poem.
It is written in a stanza form
Pushkin invented specifically for it —
the **Onegin stanza:**
fourteen lines, iambic tetrameter,
rhyme scheme AbAbCCddEffEgg
(where uppercase = feminine rhyme, lowercase = masculine).

The Onegin stanza is to Russian verse
what the Shakespearean sonnet is to English —
a form so perfected for one specific purpose
that it seems to have existed always,
waiting to be discovered.

The plot, briefly:

**Eugene Onegin** is a young St. Petersburg dandy —
bored, fashionable, disillusioned.
He inherits an estate in the countryside.
He befriends **Vladimir Lensky** — a young Romantic poet,
idealistic, in love with **Olga Larina**.

Olga's sister is **Tatyana** — bookish, dreaming,
raised on French Romantic novels,
utterly unlike the social world around her.

Tatyana falls in love with Onegin.
She writes him a letter — one of the most famous letters
in world literature —
confessing her love with complete, devastating honesty.

Onegin rejects her. Kindly, but firmly.
He is not capable of love. He tells her so.

At a party, bored, Onegin flirts with Olga
to provoke Lensky.
Lensky challenges him to a duel.

Onegin kills Lensky.

He leaves. Years pass.

When Onegin encounters Tatyana again —
now a princess, married to a general,
moving in the highest society —
he falls desperately in love with her.

He writes to her.

She meets him. She tells him:
she still loves him.
She will always love him.

And she will not leave her husband.

*"But I was given to another —
and I shall be faithful to him forever."*

The novel ends.

## V. Tatyana — The Most Important Woman in Russian Literature

Dostoevsky, in his famous Pushkin speech of 1880,
called Tatyana the ideal of Russian womanhood —
her sacrifice, her fidelity, her dignity.

He was right about Tatyana's importance.
He was reading her wrong.

Tatyana is not an ideal of self-sacrifice.
She is the novel's moral center
because she is the only character
who has genuinely developed.

At the novel's beginning:
she is a girl who has confused novels with reality,
who has projected her romantic fantasies onto Onegin —
a man she barely knows.

Her letter is beautiful. It is also naive.
She is in love with an idea, not a person.

Onegin's rejection is cruel.
It is also, in retrospect, a gift.

When we meet Tatyana again, years later:
she has become herself.
Not the dreaming girl. A woman.
Clear-eyed, dignified, without illusions.

She tells Onegin:
*"I love you — why should I dissemble? —
But to another I was given;
To him I shall be faithful forever."*

This is not resignation.
It is integrity.

She knows what she felt. She knows what she feels.
She also knows who she has chosen to be.

Pushkin's sympathy is entirely with her.

Not because fidelity is a conventional virtue —
Pushkin was not a conventional moralist —
but because Tatyana's refusal is an act of self-possession.

She refuses to be the object of Onegin's belated desire.
She refuses to repeat the mistake of her youth —
of letting passion override judgment.

She is, in the end, more free than Onegin.

## VI. Onegin — The Superfluous Man

Onegin gave Russian literature one of its central types:
the **superfluous man** (*лишний человек*).

Intelligent, educated, capable of feeling —
but incapable of sustained action,
sustained love, sustained commitment.

He is bored by everything.
He destroys what he touches — not maliciously,
but through his inability to be present,
to take anything seriously,
to risk himself.

He kills Lensky not from malice
but from social cowardice —
he does not want to seem afraid to duel.
He condemns his friend to death
to preserve a social performance.

He rejects Tatyana — the one person
who might have saved him from himself —
because he is honest enough to know
he cannot love her as she deserves.

Years later, when he finally feels —
when Tatyana's transformation awakens something real in him —
it is too late.
Not because Tatyana is cruel.
Because Onegin cannot be trusted
with what he claims to want.

Tatyana knows this.
Onegin, in the final scene, may finally know it too.

The superfluous man — Pechorin in *A Hero of Our Time*,
Oblomov in Goncharov's novel,
Rudin in Turgenev —
all descend from Onegin.

Dostoevsky spent his career arguing
with this type.

## VII. The Lyrics

The novel and the long poems are what made Pushkin famous.
The lyrics are what made him immortal.

Pushkin wrote several hundred lyric poems —
love poems, political poems, meditations on death,
nature poems, occasional verse, translations.

They are almost untranslatable.

The precision, the economy, the music —
these are features of Russian that English cannot replicate.

But let me try to gesture at what they do.

**"I loved you once..."** (1829):

*Я вас любил: любовь ещё, быть может,
В душе моей угасла не совсем;
Но пусть она вас больше не тревожит;
Я не хочу печалить вас ничем.*

*I loved you once: perhaps that love
Has not entirely died within my soul;
But let it not disturb you any longer;
I have no wish to cause you any grief.*

Eight lines. Iambic pentameter.
The most famous love poem in the Russian language.

What it does:

It says goodbye without bitterness.
It acknowledges feeling without demanding response.
It releases the beloved without punishing her.

*"May God grant you another love you so —
As tenderly and truly as I loved you."*

The final lines are the most astonishing:
the speaker wishes the woman he loves
to be loved as well as he loved her —
by someone else.

This is not resignation. Not martyrdom.
It is the highest form of love —
the one that wants the beloved's happiness
more than its own continuation.

In eight lines.

## VIII. The Political Poet

Pushkin's political poetry is less known in the West
but central to his legacy in Russia.

**Ode to Liberty** (1817) — written at nineteen —
attacked tyranny directly:

*"Autocratic villain!
I hate you, your throne,
Your death, the death of your children —
I see with cruel joy."*

This got him exiled.

**The Village** (1819) — on serfdom:

*"Here torment-lordship drags along,
Without feeling, without law,
Appropriated by violent rod
The labor, property, and time of the farmer."*

Also exiled.

But Pushkin's political poetry is more complex
than simple opposition.

He was not a revolutionary in the Decembrist sense.
He was not a democrat.
He believed in enlightened monarchy —
a Tsar who governed by law and reason.

His political ideal was Peter the Great —
the modernizing, Westernizing autocrat
who dragged Russia into the modern world.

He wrote a poem in defense of Tsar Nicholas I
after the Decembrist uprising.

This complexity — the combination of genuine
hatred of tyranny with genuine monarchism —
made him difficult to categorize then
and makes him difficult to use politically now.

Both Soviet and post-Soviet Russia have claimed him.
He belongs to neither.

He belongs to the language.

## IX. The Translation Problem

Nabokov spent years translating *Eugene Onegin* into English.

The result — four volumes, including two volumes of commentary —
is one of the most extraordinary works of literary scholarship
ever produced.

It is also, by almost universal agreement,
unreadable as a poem.

Nabokov made a decision: absolute fidelity to meaning
over any attempt at verse form.
He translated word by word,
preserving every nuance of the Russian
at the cost of all music.

He argued this was the only honest approach —
that any attempt to replicate the verse
would inevitably falsify the meaning.

Other translators — Johnston, Falen, Mitchell —
have attempted verse translations.
They are more readable.
They are less accurate.

The lesson: Pushkin is the most Russian of writers
precisely because his genius is most fully expressed
in his language's specific music.

To read Pushkin fully — to understand
why Russians place him where they place him —
you must, eventually, learn Russian.

This is both a challenge and an invitation.

## X. Why He Still Matters

On June 6, Pushkin's birthday,
Russians gather at his statues across the country
and recite his poetry.

Not organized events. Spontaneous gatherings.
People who have memorized hundreds of his lines —
because Russian schoolchildren memorize Pushkin
the way English schoolchildren used to memorize Shakespeare —
standing at his statue and speaking the lines aloud.

This has happened under the Tsars.
Under the Soviets.
After the Soviet Union.

It happens because Pushkin gave Russians
something that cannot be taken from them:

A language that is fully itself.
A literature that begins with him
and passes through him.
A way of speaking about love, death, freedom, and time
that is Russian in the deepest sense —
not nationalist, not ideological,
but linguistic, musical, irreducible.

*"And for a long time yet I shall be loved by people
For having woken gentle feelings with my lyre,
For having, in my cruel age, extolled freedom
And called for mercy toward the fallen."*

He wrote this in 1836.
One year before the duel.

He knew what he had made.
He knew it would last.

He was right.

---

*Start with the lyrics — any translation, just to hear the subjects.*
*Then Eugene Onegin — Johnston's verse translation*
*is the most readable; Nabokov's prose translation*
*is the most accurate.*
*Then The Bronze Horseman — the greatest short poem*
*in the Russian language.*
*Then, if you can, learn Russian.*
*You will not regret it.*
