Some parts of New York
are not about the city itself.

They are about what the city means —
what it has meant to the people
who arrived here with everything
in one bag,
looking at the skyline
for the first time.

Part III is about those places.

---

## I. The Ferry

To get to Ellis Island and the Statue of Liberty,
you take a ferry from Battery Park —
the southern tip of Manhattan.

*[photo: Battery Park or ferry dock]*

The ferry crosses the harbor slowly.
The city recedes behind you.
The Statue of Liberty gets larger ahead.

From the water,
New York looks different than from inside it.

From inside: overwhelming, vertical, immediate.
From the water: composed, complete,
a thing you can see all at once.

The skyline from the harbor
is one of the most recognizable images
in the world —
and seeing it in person,
from the water,
is still surprising.

It looks exactly like itself.
And it is larger than you expected.

*[photo: Manhattan skyline from the ferry]*

## II. The Statue of Liberty

The Statue of Liberty is 93 meters tall
from ground to torch.

Designed by Frédéric Auguste Bartholdi.
Engineered internally by Gustave Eiffel —
yes, that Eiffel.
Gifted by France to the United States in 1886.

*[photo: Statue of Liberty]*

Standing at the base and looking up:
the scale is different from photographs.
The face is serious, not triumphant.
The torch arm is raised but not gesturing —
holding, steadily, upward.

The full name: *Liberty Enlightening the World.*

Not liberty for Americans specifically.
Liberty as a light — visible from the harbor,
visible to anyone arriving by sea —
the first thing immigrants saw
after weeks on the water.

*[photo: Statue of Liberty face or torch detail]*

**The childhood photograph:**

I have a photograph of myself
at five or six years old —
standing somewhere,
one arm raised,
torch hand up,
doing my best impression
of the Statue of Liberty.

*[photo: childhood Statue of Liberty pose —
this one is yours to add]*

I did not know then
that I would one day stand
at the base of the actual statue,
looking up at the face
I had been imitating.

I thought about that small person
and her raised arm
for a while.

## The New Colossus

Before the ferry docks at Ellis Island,
a poem.

Emma Lazarus wrote it in 1883 —
to raise funds for the statue's pedestal.
It was cast in bronze and mounted inside the pedestal in 1903.

Most people know the last lines.
The whole poem is better:

---

*Not like the brazen giant of Greek fame,*
*With conquering limbs astride from land to land;*
*Here at our sea-washed, sunset gates shall stand*
*A mighty woman with a torch, whose flame*
*Is the imprisoned lightning, and her name*
*Mother of Exiles. From her beacon-hand*
*Glows world-wide welcome; her mild eyes command*
*The air-bridged harbor that twin cities frame.*
*"Keep, ancient lands, your storied pomp!" cries she*
*With silent lips. "Give me your tired, your poor,*
*Your huddled masses yearning to breathe free,*
*The wretched refuse of your teeming shore.*
*Send these, the homeless, tempest-tost to me,*
*I lift my lamp beside the golden door!"*

---

*Mother of Exiles.*

Not a conqueror standing astride the world.
A mother, holding a lamp,
at the door.

That is the difference.
That is the whole poem.

## III. Ellis Island

The ferry stops at Ellis Island
before or after Liberty Island —
twelve acres in the harbor,
now a museum,
formerly the entry point
for approximately twelve million immigrants
between 1892 and 1954.

*[photo: Ellis Island exterior]*

Twelve million people.

The Great Hall —
the main processing room —
is enormous, vaulted, tiled.
It has been restored.

*[photo: Ellis Island Great Hall]*

This is where they waited.

After weeks at sea — in steerage,
the cheapest berths,
below the waterline —
they arrived here.
They were examined by doctors
who walked among them,
marking coats with chalk
if something seemed wrong:
E for eyes, H for heart, X for mental illness.

They waited in lines.
They were questioned by inspectors
through interpreters.
Name. Age. Where from.
Do you have a job waiting?
Do you have money?
Is anyone meeting you?

Most were processed in a few hours
and sent through the door
marked *To New York.*

Some were detained.
Some were sent back.

The ones who made it through
walked out into America
with whatever they were carrying.

*[photo: Ellis Island registry room or exhibits]*

**The names:**

The museum has a wall of names —
the American Immigrant Wall of Honor —
listing names of immigrants
who passed through Ellis Island.

I walked along it slowly.

Names from everywhere.
Every country.
Every language written in its own alphabet
then transliterated into English
by an inspector who did the best he could.

Many names were changed here —
simplified, anglicized,
misspelled by accident or impatience —
and the new name stuck.

The person who arrived as one name
left as another.

A new country, a new name.
Starting from what you had in your hands.

*[photo: Wall of Honor or name details]*

**What Ellis Island is:**

Ellis Island is not a comfortable place.

It is a place where
the distance between arriving and being turned back
was a chalk mark on a coat,
a doctor's five-second assessment,
an inspector's question you may not have understood.

And also:
a place where twelve million people
walked through a door
and became part of something new.

Both things are true simultaneously.
The museum holds both.

## IV. The 9/11 Memorial

In Lower Manhattan,
where the Twin Towers stood,
there are now two pools.

*[photo: 9/11 Memorial pools]*

Each pool is one acre —
the footprint of each tower.
Water falls continuously
down the sides into the pools,
then falls again into a smaller square
in the center of each pool,
disappearing into darkness below.

The sound: constant water.
Not loud. Present.

Around the edges of each pool,
cut into the bronze parapets:
the names.

2,977 names.
Every person killed on September 11, 2001,
and in the 1993 World Trade Center bombing.

*[photo: names on the memorial parapet]*

The names are arranged not alphabetically
but by connection —
colleagues together, friends together,
the people who were with each other
at the end.

On birthdays, memorial staff place
a white rose in the cut-out of the name.

I visited on a day
when there were several white roses
in the bronze letters.

*[photo: white rose in the names, if you have one]*

I stood at the edge of the pool
for a long time.

The water keeps falling.
The names stay.

**The museum underground:**

Below the memorial, underground,
is the 9/11 Museum.

*[photo: museum entrance or interior]*

It is built around what remains —
the foundation walls of the towers,
the slurry wall that held back the Hudson,
the last column removed from the site,
the Survivors' Staircase
that hundreds of people walked down
to escape.

The museum is not easy to be in.
It should not be easy to be in.

I spent two hours there.
I needed air afterward.
I went back to the memorial pools
and stood by the water again.

## V. What the Harbor Holds

New York from the harbor:
the skyline, the statue, the island,
the memorial pools underground.

Arrivals and absences.
What was built and what was lost.
The people who came
and the people who never left.

The harbor holds all of this
without comment —
the water just moves,
the ferry crosses,
the city stands at the edge
and receives whatever comes toward it.

This is what New York is,
from the water:

Not just a city.
A place where things happened
that were larger than any one person —
and where the evidence of those things
is still here,
in the names cut into bronze,
in the vaulted hall where millions waited,
in the face of the statue
that has been looking outward
since 1886.

I took the ferry back to Manhattan
in the late afternoon.

The city came toward me across the water.

*[photo: returning to Manhattan by ferry,
skyline approaching]*

Same skyline.
Different weight.

---

## VI. One More Thing

Somewhere between Ellis Island
and the ferry back,
I thought about that photograph —
the small person with the raised arm,
doing her best impression of liberty.

She didn't know then
what the statue meant to the people
who first saw it from the water.
She just thought the pose was good.

Now I know.

The pose was right.
The reason was bigger than she understood.

That is fine.
You learn the weight of things
by being in them long enough.

*[photo: Statue of Liberty one more time,
or harbor at dusk]*

*New York, multiple visits.*
*Some things you only understand
from the water.*
